## Before You Start

You press the power button — nothing. You plug it in — no charging icon, no Apple logo, no sign of life. A dead iPad is scary, especially when you're not sure if it's the $10 cable or the $1,000 tablet that's broken. But here's the good news: a completely black, unresponsive iPad is almost never permanently dead. The overwhelming majority of "dead iPad" cases are caused by a deeply drained battery, a software freeze, a dirty charging port, or a failed cable — and none of those require a repair shop.

This guide covers every iPad model: iPad Pro (all generations), iPad Air (all generations), iPad Mini (all generations), and the standard iPad. The force restart button combinations differ between models with a Home button and models with Face ID, so each step includes instructions for both.

**What you'll need:**
- Your iPad and its charging cable
- The USB power adapter that came with your iPad (or a high-quality replacement)
- Access to a wall outlet (not a computer USB port — those provide too little power)
- A computer (Mac or Windows PC) with the latest version of iTunes or Finder — only needed for Step 6
- A flashlight or bright light (to inspect the charging port)
- Optional: a wooden toothpick (for cleaning the charging port)

> **Don't panic!** If your iPad was working yesterday and today the screen is black, it's probably just a deeply drained battery combined with a software freeze. Force restarting (Step 1) while charging (Step 2) fixes this in 80% of cases. Do NOT immediately assume you need a new iPad or an expensive repair — work through these steps first.

## Step 1: Force Restart Your iPad

A regular restart requires the screen to work — you tap "slide to power off" and wait. But when the screen is black and unresponsive, you need a **force restart**. This is a hardware-level reset that cuts power to the iPad's processor and forces it to reboot, regardless of what the software is doing. It's completely safe and does not erase any of your data.

A force restart solves the single most common cause of a black iPad screen: a software crash or freeze where the iPad is actually ON (processor running, battery charged) but the screen and touch are frozen. You just can't tell because the screen is black. The force restart breaks the freeze loop.

**For iPads WITHOUT a Home button (iPad Pro 2018+, iPad Air 4th gen+, iPad Mini 6th gen+, iPad 10th gen+):**

These iPads have Face ID and no Home button on the front. The top button is on the top edge (or the right edge if you're holding it in portrait).

1. Press and **quickly release** the **volume button closest to the top button** (Volume Up).
2. Press and **quickly release** the **volume button farthest from the top button** (Volume Down).
3. Press and **hold the top button**. Keep holding.
4. Do NOT release when you see the "slide to power off" slider — keep holding past it.
5. Keep holding until the screen goes completely black, then the **Apple logo** appears. This takes about 10–15 seconds.
6. Release the top button as soon as the Apple logo appears.

> **The timing matters.** The volume button presses must be quick — press and release each one in less than half a second. Many people press and hold them, but that doesn't trigger the force restart. Think of it as: click-click-HOOOOOLD.

**For iPads WITH a Home button (iPad 9th gen and older, iPad Mini 5th gen and older):**

These iPads have a circular Home button below the screen.

1. **Press and hold BOTH the top button AND the Home button simultaneously.**
2. Keep holding both buttons. The screen may flash or go black — keep holding.
3. Release both buttons when the **Apple logo** appears. This takes about 10–15 seconds.
4. If nothing happens after 20 seconds, release both buttons, wait 5 seconds, and try again — make sure you're pressing both buttons all the way down.

![Two iPads side by side: one Face ID model showing the volume-up, volume-down, then top-button-hold sequence, and one Home button model showing both buttons held simultaneously](PLACEHOLDER)

After the Apple logo appears, wait 30–60 seconds for the iPad to finish booting. If your iPad boots normally to the Lock Screen — great, you're done! If the screen goes black again or the Apple logo disappears and nothing happens, move to Step 2 and do the force restart AGAIN while the iPad is plugged into power.

> **Common mistake:** People tap the volume buttons and then hold the top button for only 3–5 seconds, see nothing happen, and give up. The force restart on Face ID iPads takes 10–15 seconds of CONTINUOUS holding. Count to 15 slowly before releasing. Many people report "it didn't work the first time" because they didn't hold long enough. Also, if you have a case with a thick bezel or button covers, the button may not be pressing fully — take the case off and try again with bare buttons.

> **Common mistake #2:** People try a force restart and when nothing happens, they immediately try it again while the iPad is still mid-process. If the iPad started booting but the screen stayed black (common when the battery is extremely low), pressing buttons again can interrupt the boot cycle. Do the force restart ONCE, then wait at least 30 seconds before trying again or moving to Step 2.

## Step 2: Charge the iPad — The Right Way

If a force restart didn't wake your iPad, the battery may be so deeply drained that the iPad doesn't have enough power to even show the Apple logo. When a lithium-ion battery drops below a certain voltage, the iPad enters a protective deep-discharge state and won't respond to button presses at all. It's not broken — it's protecting itself.

**Fix 2a — Use a wall outlet, not a computer**

1. Plug the charging cable into the **USB power adapter** that came with your iPad (or any Apple-certified adapter).
2. Plug the power adapter directly into a **wall outlet** — NOT a computer USB port, NOT a USB hub, and NOT a power strip USB port.
3. Why: an iPad needs **10–20 watts** of power to charge from dead. Computer USB ports typically provide only 2.5–5 watts. The iPad will charge on a computer port if the battery has some life, but when the battery is completely dead, a computer port can't provide enough power to wake the charging circuit. Use the wall.
4. Plug the Lightning or USB-C end into your iPad.

**Fix 2b — Wait longer than you think you need to**

1. Leave the iPad plugged in and do NOT touch it for **at least 30 minutes** — preferably an hour.
2. Do not press any buttons during this time. The iPad's charging circuit needs uninterrupted time to trickle-charge the battery back to a safe voltage before it can even turn on the screen.
3. After 30 minutes, press the top button briefly. If you see a **red battery icon with a lightning bolt**, the battery is critically low but charging — keep waiting.
4. If you see the **Apple logo**, the iPad is booting — wait for it to finish.
5. If you still see nothing, wait another 30 minutes and try a force restart (Step 1) WHILE the iPad is still plugged in.

**Fix 2c — Check the charging icon closely**

When an iPad is completely dead and begins charging, the screen behavior can be confusing:

- **No icon for the first 5–15 minutes:** Completely normal. A deeply drained iPad won't show anything until the battery reaches about 3% charge.
- **Red battery icon with a thin red line at the bottom:** The battery is below 5%. Keep charging — it could take 15–30 minutes to show the Apple logo.
- **Apple logo appears, then disappears, then reappears:** This is a "boot loop." The battery has enough power to start booting but not enough to finish. Keep charging for another hour without touching it, then force restart while plugged in.
- **"Connect to Power" screen with a cable pointing to a lightning bolt:** The battery is too low to boot but the charging circuit is working. Keep it plugged in.

![An iPad screen showing the red battery icon with a lightning bolt — the charging indicator you want to see during Step 2](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People plug the iPad into their laptop, see nothing after 10 minutes, and assume the iPad is dead forever. A laptop USB port cannot wake a completely dead iPad — it literally doesn't supply enough watts. The charging circuit in the iPad draws 5 volts but needs 2–4 amps to start from dead; a laptop port maxes out at 0.5–1 amp. Always use a wall adapter. If you don't have the original adapter, use any quality USB-C or USB-A wall adapter rated for at least 12 watts (iPad) or 18 watts (iPad Pro). Phone chargers (5 watts) can work but take much longer.

> **Common mistake #2:** People check every 2–3 minutes by pressing the power button. Every time you wake the screen to check, you're drawing power from a battery that has almost nothing to give. This can actually DELAY the charging process. Plug it in, leave it alone, and set a timer for 30 minutes. Come back when the timer goes off.

## Step 3: Inspect and Clean the Charging Port

Dirt, lint, and debris packed into the charging port is one of the most common — and most overlooked — causes of an iPad that won't charge. iPads get carried in backpacks, bags, and cases where pocket lint, dust, and crumbs find their way into the port. Over months of plugging and unplugging, that debris gets compressed into a hard, felt-like layer at the bottom of the port that prevents the charging cable from seating fully.

**Fix 3a — Inspect the port with a flashlight**

1. Grab a flashlight or use your phone's flashlight.
2. Shine it directly into the charging port (Lightning port on older iPads, USB-C port on newer iPads).
3. Look for any visible lint, dust, dirt, or debris. The port should look clean with visible metal contacts at the very back.
4. If the back of the port looks dark or "fuzzy" instead of clean metal, debris is packed in there.

**Fix 3b — Clean the port SAFELY**

> **⚠️ WARNING:** The charging port contains delicate metal pins that can be permanently damaged. Use ONLY non-conductive, non-metallic tools. NEVER use metal tweezers, paperclips, safety pins, or anything metal — you can short the pins and permanently destroy the charging circuit.

1. Use a **wooden toothpick** or a **plastic dental pick/flosser**. Wood and plastic don't conduct electricity, so they won't short the pins.
2. GENTLY insert the tip into the charging port — go straight in, not at an angle.
3. Scoop along the bottom edges, hugging the walls of the port. Most debris accumulates in the corners and along the bottom.
4. Be especially careful around the center — on Lightning ports, there's a row of 8 pins on each side; on USB-C ports, there's a thin tongue in the middle with pins on both sides. Do NOT scrape or pry against these pins.
5. Pull the toothpick out. You'll likely see a surprising amount of lint come out — gray, felt-like material. This is months or years of compressed pocket lint.
6. Repeat a few times, then shine the flashlight in again to check your progress.

**Alternative cleaning tool: a soft-bristled brush**

If you don't have a toothpick or aren't comfortable poking into the port, you can use a clean, dry, soft-bristled brush (like a clean makeup brush or a new soft toothbrush):
1. Brush gently back and forth across the port opening to dislodge surface debris.
2. Turn the iPad port-side-down and tap it gently against your palm to shake out loosened debris.
3. This is less effective for deeply compacted lint but safer if you're nervous about poking inside the port.

![Close-up of someone using a wooden toothpick to gently remove lint from an iPad's charging port, with a flashlight illuminating the port](PLACEHOLDER)

After cleaning the port, plug the charging cable in. It should now insert with a firm, satisfying click and sit completely flush against the iPad's body. If the metal connector is still visible (not flush against the body), debris may still be preventing full insertion — clean again. Then try charging normally (Step 2) for 30 minutes.

> **Common mistake:** People look at the port from the outside, see a little dust, and assume it's fine. The debris that causes charging problems is packed at the VERY BOTTOM of the port, where you can't see it without a flashlight. By the time lint is visible at the entrance, there's likely 2–3 times as much compacted deep inside. Always inspect with a flashlight.

> **Common mistake #2:** People use compressed air to blow out the port. This can actually push debris DEEPER into the port and lodge it more firmly against the pins. Worse, the propellant in some compressed air cans contains chemicals that can leave a residue on the contacts. Manual removal with a toothpick is safer and more effective.

> **Common mistake #3:** People clean the port, see improvement, but the problem comes back. This means there's still debris deep in the port that shifted temporarily when you cleaned. Be persistent — it often takes 3–4 rounds of cleaning to get everything out of a port that's been accumulating lint for years.

## Step 4: Check Your Charging Cable and Power Adapter

If your iPad still won't turn on or charge after cleaning the port and waiting, the problem may be the cable or the power adapter — not the iPad itself. Charging cables fail far more often than iPads do, and the failure is often invisible.

**Fix 4a — Test with a different cable**

Not all charging cables are created equal. Many cheap or old cables are "charge-only" (missing the data wires) or have developed internal breaks from bending and tugging.

1. Find a **different** Lightning or USB-C cable — ideally one that you KNOW works with another Apple device.
2. Use the ORIGINAL power adapter (the one that came with the iPad) but swap the cable.
3. Plug in and wait 15 minutes. If the iPad begins charging, your original cable is dead — throw it away and use the new one.

**Fix 4b — Test with a different power adapter**

1. Keep the cable that you know works (or try both), and swap the POWER ADAPTER — use the adapter from another iPad, an iPhone charger rated at 12W+, or any quality USB-C charger rated at 18W+.
2. Plug in and wait 15 minutes.

**Fix 4c — Check for cable and adapter damage**

- Inspect the cable along its entire length. Look for frayed sections, kinks, exposed wires, or areas where the cable feels "squishy" instead of firm. The most common failure point is right where the cable meets the connector (the strain relief area) — this gets bent every time the cable is used and eventually breaks internally.
- Check the gold or silver contacts on the Lightning/USB-C connector. If they're dark, corroded, or have black spots, the connector is damaged.
- Look at the USB end. Bent or misaligned metal prongs inside the USB-A or USB-C connector can prevent a proper connection.

**Fix 4d — Test your cable and adapter on another device**

1. Plug the exact same cable and adapter into another device (another iPad, an iPhone, or any USB-C device).
2. If the other device charges, the cable and adapter are probably fine — the problem is your iPad.
3. If the other device ALSO doesn't charge, the cable or adapter is the problem. Replace both.

![A side-by-side comparison showing a frayed, damaged charging cable next to a fresh cable, with an iPad plugged in and showing the charging battery icon](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People use the same cable for years and don't realize that Lightning and USB-C cables have a limited lifespan. The constant bending — especially if you use your iPad while it's plugged in — creates microscopic fractures in the copper wires inside the cable. Eventually, enough wires break that the cable can't carry enough current to charge the iPad. The cable may still work intermittently when held at a certain angle, which tricks people into thinking it's fine. If your iPad charges when you hold the cable at a specific angle but stops when you let go, the cable is broken — replace it.

> **Common mistake #2:** People use iPhone chargers (5W, the tiny white cube) to charge an iPad, then wonder why it takes forever or doesn't charge from dead. An iPad battery is 3–5 times larger than an iPhone battery and needs proportionally more power. The 5W iPhone charger CAN charge an iPad, but it's so slow that from completely dead, it may take 6–8 hours. Always use a 12W, 18W, 20W, or 30W adapter — the higher the wattage, the faster the charge. Apple's 20W USB-C adapter is the standard charger for all modern iPads and charges most models from dead to 50% in about 35 minutes.

## Step 5: Check for Temperature Issues

iPads have built-in temperature protection. If the iPad is too hot or too cold, it will refuse to charge or turn on — this is a safety mechanism to protect the battery from permanent damage.

**Fix 5a — If the iPad feels hot to the touch**

If your iPad was left in direct sunlight, in a hot car, or was running an intensive game before it died:
1. Unplug the iPad from the charger immediately.
2. Move it to a cool, shaded area — indoors with air conditioning is ideal.
3. Leave it alone for at least 15–30 minutes to cool down.
4. Once it feels cool to the touch, plug it in and try charging again.

You may see a temperature warning screen: a yellow triangle with a thermometer icon and the message "iPad needs to cool down before you can use it." Wait until this message disappears before charging.

**Fix 5b — If the iPad feels very cold**

If your iPad was left in a cold car overnight or in an unheated room during winter:
1. Move the iPad to room temperature (a warm room, NOT a heater or radiator).
2. Let it sit for 20–30 minutes. Drastic temperature changes can cause condensation inside the device, so warm it gradually.
3. Once the iPad is at room temperature, plug it in and attempt to charge.

> **Apple's official operating temperature range for iPads is 32°F to 95°F (0°C to 35°C).** Outside this range, the iPad may disable charging, dim the screen, or shut down entirely. The battery can be permanently damaged if charged while above 95°F.

![A temperature warning screen on an iPad showing the yellow thermometer icon with "iPad needs to cool down before you can use it"](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People bring a cold iPad inside, immediately plug it in, and panic when it doesn't charge. Give it 30 minutes to reach room temperature. A lithium-ion battery that's near freezing literally cannot accept a charge — the chemical reaction inside the battery slows to a crawl. The iPad knows this and blocks charging to prevent damage. Warming it to room temperature is all that's needed.

> **Common mistake #2:** People leave their iPad on the dashboard or seat of a car on a sunny day. Even on a 75°F day, the interior of a car can reach 120°F within an hour. If your iPad was in a hot car and won't turn on, it has triggered thermal protection. Get it somewhere cool and leave it alone for 30+ minutes.

## Step 6: Connect to a Computer and Use Recovery Mode

If your iPad still won't turn on, won't charge, or is stuck on the Apple logo, Recovery Mode is your next step. Recovery Mode connects the iPad to a computer and lets you reinstall iPadOS — the iPad's operating system — without erasing your data (as long as you choose "Update," NOT "Restore").

**What you'll need:**
- A Mac running macOS Catalina (10.15) or later — uses **Finder**
- OR a Mac running macOS Mojave (10.14) or earlier — uses **iTunes**
- OR a Windows PC with **iTunes** or the **Apple Devices app** installed (download from apple.com or the Microsoft Store)
- The charging cable that came with your iPad (or a known-good data cable)

**Step-by-step for iPads WITHOUT a Home button (Face ID models):**

1. **Connect the iPad to your computer** using the USB cable.
2. **Open Finder** (on Mac) or **iTunes/Apple Devices** (on Windows).
3. On the iPad, perform the force restart sequence from Step 1: press and quickly release Volume Up, press and quickly release Volume Down, then **press and hold the top button**.
4. **Keep holding** the top button even after the Apple logo appears. Do NOT release.
5. Continue holding until you see the **Recovery Mode screen** on the iPad — a computer icon with a cable pointing to it and "support.apple.com/ipad/restore" text.
6. On your computer, a pop-up will appear: "There is a problem with the iPad that requires it to be updated or restored."
7. Click **Update** (NOT "Restore"). Update reinstalls iPadOS while keeping your data. Restore erases everything.
8. Your computer will download the latest iPadOS software. This file is 6–8 GB and can take 15 minutes to over an hour depending on your internet speed.
9. If the download takes more than 15 minutes, the iPad may exit Recovery Mode. If that happens, just repeat steps 3–6 — the download continues where it left off.

**Step-by-step for iPads WITH a Home button:**

1. **Connect the iPad to your computer** using the USB cable.
2. **Open Finder** or **iTunes/Apple Devices**.
3. On the iPad, **press and hold BOTH the Home button and the top button simultaneously**.
4. Keep holding both buttons through the Apple logo. Do NOT release.
5. Continue holding until you see the **Recovery Mode screen** (computer icon with cable).
6. On your computer, click **Update** (NOT "Restore").
7. Wait for the download and installation to complete.

**If Recovery Mode doesn't help:**

After the Update completes, your iPad will restart. If it boots normally — great! If it goes back to a black screen or returns to the Apple logo and freezes, the iPad needs a more advanced restore. In that case, repeat the Recovery Mode steps above, but this time click **Restore** instead of Update. **⚠️ WARNING: Choosing Restore erases ALL data on the iPad.** Only use this if Update didn't work and you have an iCloud backup (or you've accepted losing your data).

![An iPad displaying the Recovery Mode screen: a computer icon with a cable, alongside a Mac screen showing the Finder pop-up with Update and Restore options](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People see "Restore" and "Update" and pick Restore because it sounds more thorough. "Update" is almost always the right choice — it reinstalls the operating system while keeping all your photos, apps, and data. "Restore" completely wipes the iPad. Only choose Restore if Update has failed and you've exhausted all other options.

> **Common mistake #2:** People use a charge-only cable (no data wires) and Recovery Mode can't work because the computer can't communicate with the iPad. The iPad must be able to send data over the cable for Recovery Mode. If your computer doesn't detect the iPad at all — no pop-up, no device appearing in Finder/iTunes — your cable is probably charge-only. Use the cable that came with the iPad or any cable explicitly labeled "sync and charge" or "data transfer."

## Step 7: DFU Restore (Last Resort)

If Recovery Mode didn't work — the computer didn't detect the iPad, or the Update/Restore process failed — there's one more thing to try: **DFU (Device Firmware Update) mode.** DFU mode is a lower-level recovery than standard Recovery Mode. It completely bypasses the iPad's operating system and communicates directly with the device's firmware — the code that runs even before iPadOS loads.

DFU mode is what Apple technicians use when an iPad seems completely dead. If DFU mode works, the iPad's hardware is fine — the firmware or software was just corrupted. If DFU mode fails (computer doesn't detect the iPad even in DFU mode), there's a hardware problem that requires Apple service.

**DFU Mode for iPads WITHOUT a Home button (Face ID models):**

1. **Connect the iPad to your computer** and open Finder/iTunes.
2. Press and quickly release **Volume Up**.
3. Press and quickly release **Volume Down**.
4. Press and **hold the top button** for 3 seconds.
5. While STILL holding the top button, also press and hold **Volume Down**.
6. Hold **BOTH buttons** for 10 seconds.
7. After 10 seconds, **release the top button but KEEP holding Volume Down**.
8. Keep holding Volume Down for another 5 seconds.

If successful: the iPad's screen will remain **completely black** (no Apple logo, no Recovery Mode icon). But on your computer, Finder or iTunes will show a pop-up: "iTunes has detected an iPad in recovery mode." This is DFU mode — the iPad's screen is black but the computer can see it. 

If you see the Apple logo or the Recovery Mode screen, you held the buttons for the wrong amount of time. The timing is tricky — it's normal to need 2–3 attempts.

**DFU Mode for iPads WITH a Home button:**

1. **Connect the iPad to your computer** and open iTunes/Finder.
2. Press and hold **BOTH the top button and the Home button** for exactly 8 seconds.
3. After 8 seconds, **release the top button but KEEP holding the Home button**.
4. Keep holding the Home button for another 5–10 seconds.

The screen will be black, but iTunes/Finder will show a recovery pop-up.

**Once in DFU mode:**
1. Click **Restore** on the computer pop-up (in DFU mode, there's only the Restore option — Update isn't available).
2. **⚠️ WARNING:** DFU Restore completely erases everything on the iPad — all data, settings, and the operating system — then installs a fresh copy of iPadOS. You will lose all data that isn't backed up to iCloud or a computer.
3. Wait for the download and restore process (20–60 minutes).
4. After the restore completes, the iPad will restart and show the "Hello" setup screen — like a brand new iPad out of the box.

> **If DFU mode doesn't work — the computer never detects the iPad:** This points to a hardware failure. The most common hardware failures are: a completely dead battery (battery won't hold any charge at all), a failed charging port (physical damage to the port), or a logic board issue (water damage, component failure). At this point, the iPad needs professional service. Contact Apple Support or visit an Apple Store. Out-of-warranty iPad battery replacement costs $99–$119 depending on the model. Charging port repair is $100–$250.

![A completely black iPad screen (DFU mode — no visible signs of life) with a Mac showing an iTunes pop-up indicating detection of an iPad in recovery mode](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People confuse DFU mode with standard Recovery Mode. Recovery Mode shows a computer icon on the iPad screen. DFU mode shows a completely BLACK screen — the iPad looks dead. People see the black screen, assume nothing is happening, and unplug the iPad right when the computer was about to detect it. If the computer shows a pop-up, the iPad is in DFU mode even if the screen is black. Trust the computer, not the iPad screen.

> **Common mistake #2:** The timing for DFU mode on Face ID iPads is unforgiving. You must hold the top button for 3 seconds, then add Volume Down for 10 seconds, then release the top button and hold Volume Down for 5 seconds. Deviating by even 1–2 seconds usually results in a regular restart or Recovery Mode. It's completely normal to need 3–5 attempts to get the timing right. Watch a YouTube video of someone doing it for your specific iPad model and try to match their timing exactly.

## Still Stuck?

- **The charging cable works with other devices but not your iPad?** The iPad's charging port may be physically damaged. Look inside with a flashlight — bent or missing pins, visible corrosion (green/white crust), or a loose USB-C tongue are signs of port damage. A damaged port needs professional repair.

- **iPad was exposed to water or liquid?** If your iPad got wet — even days or weeks ago — liquid damage can cause delayed failures. The charging port is especially vulnerable. Do NOT plug the iPad in — charging a water-damaged iPad can cause a short circuit that permanently destroys the logic board. Bring it to a repair shop for inspection. Apple Stores can run a liquid contact indicator (LCI) test to confirm water exposure.

- **iPad is more than 4–5 years old and the battery has been draining faster and faster?** Lithium-ion batteries naturally degrade over time. After 500–800 charge cycles (about 2–3 years of daily use), the battery capacity drops significantly. A degraded battery may show 20% charge one moment and drop to 1% the next, or fail to hold any charge at all. An Apple battery replacement ($99–$119) can restore your iPad to like-new battery life. To check your iPad's battery health: go to Settings → Battery → Battery Health (available on iPadOS 18+). If "Maximum Capacity" is below 80% and your iPad is frequently dying unexpectedly, the battery needs replacement.

- **iPad turns on but the screen stays black?** If you can hear notification sounds, feel vibrations when plugging in, or Siri responds when you say "Hey Siri" — but the screen remains black — the display itself has failed, not the iPad's brain. The digitizer or LCD cable may have come loose, or the screen may be physically damaged. A screen replacement costs $199–$699 depending on the model (iPad Pro screens are the most expensive). Try connecting the iPad to an external monitor using a USB-C to HDMI adapter — if the external display works, the iPad's video output is fine and the screen needs replacement.

- **iPad charges but immediately dies when unplugged?** The battery can no longer hold a charge — it's a "surface charge" that reads 100% but collapses under any load. This is a failed battery. Get the battery replaced.

- **iPad is stuck in a boot loop (Apple logo appears, disappears, reappears over and over)?** Try Recovery Mode (Step 6) and choose Update. If Update fails, use DFU mode (Step 7) and choose Restore. A boot loop usually means the operating system is corrupted, and reinstalling iPadOS is the fix.

- **Using a third-party or non-Apple charging cable?** Non-certified cables (especially the cheapest ones from gas stations, dollar stores, or random Amazon listings) often lack proper voltage regulation chips. These cables can work fine for months and then suddenly fail — or worse, deliver inconsistent power that confuses the iPad's charging circuit. Always use Apple-certified (MFi) cables. Look for the "Made for iPhone/iPad" logo on the packaging. Reliable brands: Apple, Anker, Belkin, Nomad, and Native Union.
