## Before You Start

Your Samsung TV was streaming fine yesterday, but today it can't find your WiFi network — or worse, it says "Connected" but apps won't load. WiFi problems are the most common Samsung TV complaint on forums, and they usually have simple fixes that don't require a repair person.

Samsung TVs are sensitive to router changes, software updates gone wrong, and simple memory glitches from never being fully powered off. This guide starts with the fastest, easiest fixes and works up to deeper solutions. Most people solve the problem by Fix 2 or 3.

**What you'll need:**
- Your Samsung TV remote (the Samsung Smart Remote or standard black remote)
- Access to your WiFi router (you may need to unplug it)
- Your WiFi network name and password
- A phone or computer connected to the same WiFi (to test if the internet is working)

> **Work through the steps in order.** Each fix builds on the last. Don't skip ahead — the early fixes solve most cases and take under two minutes.

## Step 1: Cold Boot Your Samsung TV

Most people turn their Samsung TV "off" with the remote, but it only goes into standby mode — like a laptop with the lid closed. It never actually shuts down. Over weeks or months, the TV's memory fills up with junk data and temporary glitches build up until WiFi stops working.

A cold boot forces a full shutdown and restart, clearing the memory and all temporary settings. This is the single most reliable fix for sudden WiFi problems — and it works more often than people expect.

**Fix 1 — Perform a cold boot**

1. With the TV turned on, **press and hold the Power button on your Samsung remote** for about 5-10 seconds. Don't just tap it — keep holding until the screen goes black and the TV completely shuts off
2. The TV will turn off and then restart on its own. You may see the Samsung logo appear after a few seconds — this means the cold boot worked
3. If holding the remote power button doesn't work (some older remotes don't support this), use the physical method instead: **unplug the TV's power cable from the wall outlet**, wait a full **60 seconds**, then plug it back in
4. Once the TV restarts, press the **Home button** on your remote, go to **Settings** (the gear icon), select **All Settings** > **Connection** > **Network**, and check if your WiFi network appears and connects

![A Samsung TV remote with the power button highlighted, showing how to hold it for a cold boot](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People press the power button for just a second, see the screen go black, and think they restarted. That's just standby mode — the TV's computer didn't actually restart. You MUST hold the power button down for 5+ seconds until the TV fully shuts off and restarts. If you only see the Samsung logo flash briefly, you didn't hold long enough.

> **Tip:** If your Samsung TV is wall-mounted with the power cord hidden behind the wall, unplugging it might be a hassle. The remote power button cold boot (holding for 5+ seconds) does the same thing and is much easier. Make this a habit — cold boot your TV once a month, even when nothing's wrong. It keeps everything running smoothly.

## Step 2: Restart Your Router and Modem

If the TV can see your WiFi network but won't connect — or connects but says "No Internet" — the problem may be your router, not the TV. Routers are small computers that also build up memory glitches over time. A router restart clears its temporary issues, renews its connection to your internet provider, and often solves the problem instantly.

The trick is doing the restart in the right order. Connecting devices back in the wrong sequence can create new glitches.

**Fix 2 — Power cycle your network equipment in order**

1. **Unplug the power from ALL three devices:** your modem, your router, AND your Samsung TV. Don't skip the TV — we want everything to start fresh
2. Wait a full **2 minutes**. Set a timer on your phone. This waiting period lets any stored electricity drain from the devices' circuits, which forces a true reset (30 seconds isn't enough for many modems)
3. **Plug in the modem first** (the box connected to the cable or phone line from the wall). Wait until all its lights stabilize — this usually takes 1-2 minutes. Look for a solid "Online" or "Internet" light
4. **Plug in the router next** (the box that broadcasts WiFi). Wait until its lights stabilize — about 1 minute. The WiFi light or 2.4G/5G lights should be solid
5. **Finally, plug in your Samsung TV** and let it fully start up
6. Go to **Settings** > **All Settings** > **Connection** > **Network** > **Open Network Settings** > **Wireless**. Select your WiFi network, enter your password, and try connecting

![A diagram showing the correct order to power cycle modem, router, and TV — with wait times labeled](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People unplug just the router and TV, forgetting the modem. If your modem is separate from your router (two different boxes), both need to be restarted. Also, plugging everything back in at the same time doesn't work — the modem needs to establish a connection to your ISP before the router starts asking for one. If the router boots faster than the modem, it gets confused and may not assign a proper IP address to your TV.

> **Tip:** While the network equipment is restarting, use your phone to test the WiFi. Open a web browser or YouTube on your phone — if your phone also can't get online, the problem is definitely your internet connection, not the TV. Call your internet provider rather than continuing with TV-specific fixes.

## Step 3: Reset Network Settings and Reconnect

Your Samsung TV stores information about every WiFi network it's ever connected to — passwords, IP addresses, DNS settings, and connection preferences. If any of this saved data gets corrupted (which can happen after a router replacement, a password change, or a software update), the TV will keep failing to connect even though nothing appears wrong.

Resetting the network wipes all this saved data and lets the TV start over with a clean slate.

**Fix 3 — Reset the TV's network settings**

1. Press the **Home button** on your Samsung remote
2. Go to **Settings** > **All Settings** > **Connection** > **Network**
3. Select **Reset Network**. A warning will appear saying this will reset all network settings — confirm by selecting **Reset**
4. The TV will briefly disconnect and clear all saved networks, passwords, and network preferences
5. After the reset completes, go back to **Network** > **Open Network Settings** > **Wireless**
6. Your TV will scan for available WiFi networks. Select your network from the list
7. **Carefully re-enter your WiFi password.** Watch for uppercase/lowercase letters — passwords are case-sensitive. Use the **Show Password** option (eye icon) on the on-screen keyboard so you can see what you're typing
8. Select **Connect** and wait for the confirmation message

![The Samsung TV Network menu showing the Reset Network option highlighted](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** After a Network Reset, people rush through the password entry and make a typo. Samsung's on-screen keyboard is slow and easy to misclick on. If the TV says "Incorrect password" or "Authentication failed" multiple times, use your phone to double-check the exact password. Open your phone's WiFi settings, find your network, and look for a "Share" or "Show password" option — or check the sticker on the bottom of your router. Type slowly and use the Show Password toggle (the eye icon) to verify every character.

> **Tip:** If you recently changed your WiFi password or got a new router, and now the TV won't connect, the Network Reset is probably all you need. The TV is still trying to use the old password or old router settings it memorized. A Network Reset forces the TV to forget everything and start fresh, which solves the mismatch.

## Step 4: Change DNS Server to 8.8.8.8

Here's a scenario that drives people crazy: your Samsung TV shows "Connected" to WiFi with full signal bars, but apps like Netflix, YouTube, and Prime Video won't load. The TV says "No Internet" even though the WiFi is connected.

This happens when your TV successfully connects to the router but can't reach the internet because of a DNS problem. DNS is like a phonebook — it translates website names (like "netflix.com") into computer addresses your TV can use. If your internet provider's DNS server is slow or failing, your TV knows it's on WiFi but can't find any websites. Switching to Google's public DNS (8.8.8.8) bypasses your ISP's flaky DNS and often fixes the problem in under a minute.

**Fix 4 — Set a manual DNS server**

1. Press the **Home button** on your Samsung remote
2. Go to **Settings** > **All Settings** > **Connection** > **Network** > **Network Status**
3. Wait for the status check to complete. You may see a message saying "Connected to your local network, but cannot connect to the internet" — this confirms a DNS issue
4. At the bottom of the Network Status screen, select **IP Settings**
5. Scroll down to **DNS Setting** and change it from **Get Automatically** to **Enter Manually**
6. A new field called **DNS Server** will appear. Select it to open the on-screen number pad
7. Enter **8 . 8 . 8 . 8** (four separate entries: 8, then 8, then 8, then 8). After typing each number, press **Done** or the **Enter/OK button** to move to the next box
8. Once all four are entered, select **OK** or **Done** to save
9. Press the **Back button** to return to the Network Status screen, then select **OK** to re-run the test
10. The status should now show a green checkmark and "Connected to the internet"

![The Samsung TV IP Settings screen with DNS Server set to 8.8.8.8 in manual entry mode](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People enter 8.8.8.8 and expect the internet to work immediately without re-running the Network Status test. Changing DNS settings doesn't automatically reconnect — the TV needs to check the connection again. After entering the DNS, you MUST go back to the Network Status screen and run the test (press OK). Only then does the TV start using the new DNS server.

> **Tip:** If 8.8.8.8 doesn't work, try Google's backup DNS: **8.8.4.4**. You can also try Cloudflare's fast DNS: **1.1.1.1**. Both are free, public DNS servers that are often faster and more reliable than what your internet provider uses. If your ISP's DNS goes down (which happens more often than providers admit), these public alternatives keep your TV online while everything else in the house might be struggling.

## Step 5: Update TV Firmware or Factory Reset

If you've tried all four fixes above and your Samsung TV still won't connect to WiFi, the problem is likely in the TV's software (firmware). Samsung periodically releases firmware updates that fix WiFi bugs, but if your TV can't connect to the internet, it can't download those updates the normal way.

This fix covers two paths: updating the firmware using a USB drive (if the update is available), and factory resetting the TV (which wipes everything and can fix deep software corruption).

**Fix 5a — Update firmware via USB (if TV can't connect to download updates)**

1. On a computer or phone that has internet access, go to **[samsung.com/us/support/downloads](https://www.samsung.com/us/support/downloads/)**
2. Type your TV's exact model number into the search bar. The model number is printed on a sticker on the back of your TV or on the original box — it looks like "QN65Q80CAFXZA" or "UN55TU8000FXZA"
3. Download the latest firmware file (it will be a .zip or .exe file) to your computer
4. If the file is a .exe, double-click it to extract the contents. If it's a .zip, right-click and select **Extract All**
5. Copy all the extracted files and folders to the **root (main folder)** of an empty USB flash drive — not inside any subfolder
6. Plug the USB drive into the USB port on the back or side of your Samsung TV
7. On the TV, go to **Settings** > **All Settings** > **Support** > **Software Update**
8. Select **Update Now**. The TV will detect the firmware on the USB drive and begin installing it
9. The TV will restart several times during the update. **Do not unplug the TV or remove the USB drive** until the update is fully complete — this can take 10-20 minutes
10. Once the TV restarts to the home screen, try connecting to WiFi again

![A USB flash drive inserted into a Samsung TV USB port with the Software Update screen displayed](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 5b — Factory reset the TV (last resort)**

> **Warning:** A factory reset erases EVERYTHING on your TV. All apps, logins, passwords, picture settings, sound settings, and channel lists are wiped. Your TV will restart as if you just took it out of the box. You'll need to reinstall apps, sign into your Samsung account, and reconfigure everything. Write down your important settings before resetting.

1. Press the **Home button** on your remote
2. Go to **Settings** > **All Settings** > **General & Privacy** (or just **General** on older models)
3. Select **Reset**
4. If prompted for a PIN, enter **0000** (the default Samsung reset PIN, unless you changed it)
5. A warning screen will appear listing everything that will be erased. Read it carefully, then select **Reset** to confirm
6. The TV will shut down, show a progress bar, then restart to the initial setup screen — this takes 5-10 minutes
7. Follow the on-screen setup: choose language, connect to WiFi (hopefully it works now!), sign into your Samsung account
8. Reinstall your streaming apps from the Samsung App Store and sign into each one

![The Samsung TV factory reset confirmation screen showing a warning and a Reset button](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People factory reset the TV, then immediately restore all their old settings from a backup — including the corrupted network configuration that caused the problem in the first place. A factory reset only helps if you set things up fresh. Don't restore any network backups. Manually reconnect to WiFi and set up your apps. Yes, it takes time, but restoring old settings defeats the purpose.

> **Tip:** If your TV is under warranty and WiFi still doesn't work after a factory reset, the WiFi hardware module inside the TV may have failed. This is a physical problem that software can't fix. Contact Samsung Support (1-800-SAMSUNG) or the store where you bought the TV. WiFi module failures are most common on TVs that are 3-5 years old and often covered under extended warranties.

## Still Stuck?

- **TV connects on 2.4 GHz but not 5 GHz?** Some 2018 Samsung models (NU6xxx, NU71xx, NU72xx, NU73xx series) only support 2.4 GHz WiFi. They can't even see a 5 GHz network. Check your TV's user manual or model number online to confirm. If you have a newer TV that supports 5 GHz but won't connect to it, your router's 5 GHz channel may be set to a channel the TV doesn't support. Log into your router settings and change the 5 GHz channel to a lower number (36, 40, 44, or 48).

- **TV works fine on a mobile hotspot but not your home WiFi?** The problem is definitely your home network, not the TV. Your router may have device limits, MAC address filtering, or a firewall that's blocking the TV. Log into your router settings (usually 192.168.0.1 or 192.168.1.1 in a web browser) and check for "Access Control," "MAC Filtering," or "Parental Controls" that might be blocking new devices. Turn these off temporarily and try connecting again.

- **WiFi keeps disconnecting every time you turn the TV off and on?** This is a known Samsung bug on some 2020-2022 models. Go to **Settings** > **All Settings** > **General & Privacy** > **Power and Energy Saving** and turn **OFF** any setting called "Auto Power Saving" or "Power Saving Mode." These settings sometimes cut power to the WiFi chip when the TV goes into standby, causing a disconnect that the TV can't recover from on its own.

- **The WiFi password is correct but the TV says "Authentication Error"?** Your router may be using a security type the TV doesn't support. Go to **Settings** > **All Settings** > **Connection** > **Network** > **Open Network Settings** > **Wireless**. When selecting your network, look at the security type listed under the network name. Samsung TVs work best with WPA2-PSK (AES). If your router is set to WPA3 or WPA2/WPA3 mixed mode, some Samsung TVs can't handle it. Log into your router and switch to WPA2-PSK only.

- **Still no luck after trying everything?** Use a wired Ethernet connection as a permanent workaround. Buy a standard Ethernet cable (Cat 5e or Cat 6, about $5-10 for 25 feet) and plug one end into your router and the other into the Ethernet port on the back of your Samsung TV. Wired connections are faster and more reliable than WiFi for streaming 4K content. If your TV is far from the router, you can use a powerline Ethernet adapter (about $40-60) that sends internet through your home's electrical wiring.

- **Samsung TV is older than 2017 and WiFi stopped working suddenly?** Older Samsung TVs (2016 and earlier) sometimes have WiFi modules that fail permanently due to heat and age. If a factory reset doesn't fix it, the WiFi hardware is probably dead. You have three options: use a wired Ethernet connection (if your TV has an Ethernet port), buy a universal WiFi-to-Ethernet adapter (about $25-35) that connects to the TV's Ethernet port, or connect a streaming stick (Roku, Fire Stick, Chromecast) to an HDMI port and use that instead of the built-in smart features.
