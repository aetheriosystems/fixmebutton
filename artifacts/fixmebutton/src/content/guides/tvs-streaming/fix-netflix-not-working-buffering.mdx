## Before You Start

You sit down to watch your show, press play, and… the red loading circle spins. And spins. Or Netflix won't load at all — you get a black screen, an error code, or the app crashes the moment you open it. Maybe the video starts but pauses every ten seconds to buffer.

Netflix problems are frustrating because they can come from so many places: your WiFi, your TV or streaming device, the Netflix app itself, or even Netflix's servers. The good news: most Netflix issues are fixable in under 10 minutes, and you rarely need to call anyone.

This guide covers every device you might use to watch Netflix — Smart TVs, Roku, Fire Stick, Apple TV, Chromecast, game consoles (PlayStation and Xbox), iPhones, Android phones, Windows PCs, and Macs. Work through the steps in order. Most people are back to watching by Step 2.

**What you'll need:**
- The device you use to watch Netflix (TV, streaming stick, phone, computer, console)
- Your WiFi network name and password (you might need to reconnect)
- About 15 minutes

> **Before you do anything else:** Check if Netflix itself is down. Open a browser on your phone or computer and go to [downdetector.com/status/netflix](https://downdetector.com/status/netflix). If you see a giant spike in outage reports, Netflix's servers are having trouble — and all you can do is wait. This happens more often during big premiere nights and holiday weekends. If others are reporting the same problem, it's not your device.

## Step 1: Restart Your Device and Check Your Internet

It sounds too simple, but restarting your device clears out temporary glitches — and Netflix's own support team says this fixes the problem for nearly half of all callers. Also check your internet connection because slow or dropped WiFi is the single most common cause of buffering.

**Fix 1 — Restart your streaming device or TV:**

**Smart TV (Samsung, LG, Sony, Vizio, TCL, Hisense, etc.):**
1. Don't just press the power button on the remote — that puts the TV in standby, which doesn't clear anything
2. Unplug the TV's power cord from the wall outlet
3. Wait **60 seconds** (use your phone timer — don't guess)
4. While you wait, press and hold the TV's power button (on the TV itself, not the remote) for 10 seconds — this drains any leftover power from the internal circuits
5. Plug the TV back in and turn it on
6. Open Netflix and try again

**Roku:**
1. Go to **Settings → System → System restart → Restart**
2. If the menu is frozen: unplug the Roku from power, wait 30 seconds, plug it back in

**Fire TV Stick:**
1. Go to **Settings → My Fire TV → Restart**
2. Or unplug from power for 30 seconds

**Apple TV:**
1. Go to **Settings → System → Restart**
2. Or press and hold the **Back (◀) button** and **TV button** on the Siri Remote at the same time until the Apple TV light blinks rapidly

**Chromecast:**
1. Unplug from power, wait 30 seconds, plug back in

**PlayStation 4 or 5:**
1. Press the **PS button** on your controller → **Power → Restart PS4/PS5**

**Xbox One or Series X/S:**
1. Press the **Xbox button** on your controller → **Profile & system → Power → Restart console**

**iPhone or iPad:**
1. Press and hold the **power button** and **volume button** until "slide to power off" appears
2. Slide to power off, wait 30 seconds, then turn it back on

**Android phone or tablet:**
1. Press and hold the **power button** until the power menu appears → **Restart**

**Windows PC or Mac:**
1. Click **Start → Power → Restart** (Windows) or **Apple menu → Restart** (Mac)

**Fix 2 — Check your internet speed:**

Netflix needs a minimum internet speed to work. If your WiFi is too slow — or too many devices are using it at once — buffering is inevitable.

1. On your phone or computer, go to **fast.com** (this is Netflix's own speed test — it tests the connection specifically to Netflix's servers)
2. Let it run for 10 seconds and write down the number you see in Mbps
3. Here's what Netflix requires:
   - **Standard Definition (SD):** at least 3 Mbps
   - **High Definition (HD):** at least 5 Mbps
   - **4K Ultra HD:** at least 15 Mbps (Netflix recommends 25 Mbps for stable 4K)
4. If your speed is below what you need, try:
   - Moving your router closer to your TV or streaming device
   - Turning off WiFi on other devices (phones, laptops, gaming consoles) that aren't being used
   - Stopping any large downloads on your network
   - If your speed is still too low, follow our [How to Fix Slow Internet](/guides/internet-wifi/fix-slow-internet) guide

**Fix 3 — Restart your WiFi router and modem:**

Your router can get bogged down just like any other device. A full restart clears its memory and often improves speed.

1. Unplug the power from **both** your modem and router
2. Wait a full **60 seconds**
3. Plug the **modem** back in first — wait for all its lights to stabilize (about 2 minutes)
4. Plug the **router** back in — wait another 2 minutes
5. Reconnect your streaming device to WiFi and test Netflix

![A hand unplugging a WiFi router from a wall outlet](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People restart their TV or streaming stick but forget to restart the router. Your TV can be perfectly fine, but if the router is having trouble, Netflix will still buffer. When in doubt, restart both. Also, pressing the power button on a TV remote to turn the screen off and on is NOT a restart — that's just standby mode. You must unplug the TV from the wall or use the Settings menu to do a full restart.

## Step 2: Clear the Netflix App's Data and Sign Back In

Netflix stores temporary files on your device — cached show artwork, partially downloaded video data, and saved login information. When these files get corrupted (which happens over time, especially after Netflix app updates), the app can freeze, crash, or get stuck in a loading loop. Clearing this data gives Netflix a fresh start — you won't lose your account, watch history, or My List. You'll just need to sign back in.

**Smart TV (Samsung):**
1. Press the **Home button** on your remote
2. Go to **Settings → Support → Device Care → Manage Storage**
3. Find **Netflix** in the list, select it, and choose **Clear Cache** and **Clear Data**
4. Alternatively: go to **Settings → Apps → Netflix → Clear Cache**, then **Clear Data**
5. Open Netflix again — you'll be signed out. Sign back in with your email and password

**Smart TV (LG):**
1. Press the **Home button** → **Settings (gear icon)** → **All Settings → General → Apps**
2. Find Netflix, select it, and choose **Clear Cache**, then **Clear Data**
3. Reopen Netflix and sign back in

**Smart TV (Sony / Android TV / Google TV):**
1. Press the **Home button** → **Settings (gear icon)** → **Apps**
2. Scroll to **Netflix** and select it
3. Choose **Clear Cache**, then **Clear Data**
4. Confirm, reopen Netflix, and sign back in

**Roku:**
1. Press the **Home button** on your Roku remote
2. Highlight the **Netflix** app (don't open it — just highlight it)
3. Press the **star (*)** button on your remote
4. Select **Remove channel → Remove**
5. Go back to the Roku home screen, then go to **Streaming Channels** (or **Search**)
6. Search for **Netflix**, select it, and choose **Add Channel**
7. Open Netflix and sign back in

**Fire TV Stick:**
1. Go to **Settings → Applications → Manage Installed Applications**
2. Find and select **Netflix**
3. Choose **Clear Cache**, then **Clear Data**
4. Reopen Netflix and sign in

**Apple TV:**
Apple TV doesn't have a "clear cache" button. Instead, you need to delete and reinstall the app:
1. Highlight the **Netflix** app on the home screen
2. Press and hold the **touchpad** on the Siri Remote until the apps start jiggling
3. Press the **Play/Pause button** and select **Delete**
4. Go to the **App Store**, search for Netflix, and reinstall it
5. Open Netflix and sign back in

**iPhone or iPad:**
1. Go to **Settings → General → iPhone/iPad Storage → Netflix**
2. Tap **Offload App** — this removes the app but keeps your documents and data
3. After it finishes, tap **Reinstall App**
4. If that doesn't work, go back and tap **Delete App** instead, then reinstall from the App Store

**Android:**
1. Go to **Settings → Apps → Netflix**
2. Tap **Storage & cache → Clear Cache**, then **Clear Storage** (or "Clear Data")
3. Reopen Netflix and sign in

**Windows PC (Netflix app from Microsoft Store):**
1. Click **Start → Settings → Apps → Installed apps** (Windows 11) or **Apps & features** (Windows 10)
2. Search for **Netflix**, click the three dots, and select **Advanced options**
3. Click **Reset** (this clears the app data), then click **Reset** again to confirm
4. Open the Netflix app and sign back in

**Windows PC or Mac (watching in a web browser):**
1. In Chrome, Edge, Firefox, or Safari: click the **lock icon** or **shield icon** in the address bar (left of the URL)
2. Click **Cookies** or **Site settings**, then remove all entries for **netflix.com**
3. At the top of the Netflix page, click your **profile icon → Sign out of Netflix**
4. Close your browser completely, reopen it, go to netflix.com, and sign back in
5. Alternatively, try a different browser — if Netflix works in Chrome but not Edge (or vice versa), the problem is with your browser, not Netflix

![A Samsung Smart TV screen showing the Apps menu with Netflix selected, highlighting the Clear Cache and Clear Data options](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People sign out of Netflix and sign back in, thinking that clears the app data. It doesn't. Signing out only resets your login session — it leaves all the corrupted cache files behind. You need to actually clear the app data or reinstall the app. Also, on Smart TVs, the "Clear Data" option will sign you out — that's normal and expected. You'll need to enter your email and password again.

## Step 3: Check Your Netflix Playback Settings and Account Status

Sometimes the problem isn't your device or your internet — it's your Netflix account settings. Two settings in particular cause buffering and loading problems: the data usage per screen setting, and your subscription plan's simultaneous stream limit.

**Fix 1 — Lower the data usage per screen setting:**

Netflix lets you control how much data each screen uses. If it's set to "High" or "Auto" but your internet speed can't keep up, Netflix constantly tries to load a higher-quality stream and falls back — causing buffering loops.

1. Open Netflix and sign in
2. Click or tap your **profile icon** in the top-right corner
3. Select **Account** (on some devices, you may need to go to netflix.com/account in a web browser)
4. Scroll down to **Profile & Parental Controls** and click the arrow next to your profile name
5. Find **Playback settings** and click **Change**
6. Under **Data usage per screen**, select **Medium** or **Low**:
   - **Auto:** Netflix adjusts quality automatically (good for fast internet, but can cause buffering loops on slow connections)
   - **High:** Up to 3 GB per hour (HD) or 7 GB per hour (4K Ultra HD)
   - **Medium:** Up to 0.7 GB per hour (Standard Definition)
   - **Low:** Up to 0.3 GB per hour (basic quality — looks fine on phones and smaller TVs)
7. Click **Save**
8. Wait 5 minutes for the change to take effect, then try watching something again

**Fix 2 — Check how many people are using your Netflix account:**

Netflix limits how many screens can watch at the same time. If too many people are streaming at once, new streams either won't start or will keep getting kicked off.

1. Go to your **Account** page (netflix.com/account in a browser)
2. Look for **Plan Details** near the top
3. Check your plan's screen limit:
   - **Standard with ads:** 2 screens at once
   - **Standard:** 2 screens at once
   - **Premium:** 4 screens at once
4. Scroll down to **Security & Privacy → Manage access and devices**
5. Click **Sign out of all devices** if you think someone is using your account without permission
6. After signing everyone out, change your Netflix password (also on the Account page under **Membership & Billing → Change password**)
7. Sign back in on your device and test Netflix

> **Common mistake:** People upgrade to a faster internet plan thinking that will fix Netflix buffering, but their Netflix playback setting is still set to "Low" data usage — so Netflix deliberately streams at lower quality regardless of internet speed. Or the opposite: their data usage is set to "High" but their internet is too slow, causing nonstop buffering. Check both your internet speed AND your Netflix playback settings.

## Step 4: Check for Device Software Updates and Reinstall Netflix

Sometimes a recent Netflix update introduces a bug that only affects certain devices. Or your device's operating system is out of date and can't properly run the latest version of Netflix. Updates fix these compatibility issues.

**Fix 1 — Update your device's operating system:**

**Smart TV:**
Most Smart TVs update automatically, but you can check manually:
- **Samsung:** Settings → Support → Software Update → Update Now
- **LG:** Settings → All Settings → General → About This TV → Check for Updates
- **Sony / Android TV:** Settings → Device Preferences → About → System Update
- **Vizio:** Settings → System → Check for Updates
- **TCL / Hisense (Android TV or Roku TV):** Follow the Roku or Android TV steps above

**Roku:**
1. Go to **Settings → System → System update → Check now**
2. If an update is available, it will download and install automatically
3. Your Roku will restart when done

**Fire TV Stick:**
1. Go to **Settings → My Fire TV → About → Check for updates**
2. Install any available updates

**Apple TV:**
1. Go to **Settings → System → Software Updates → Update Software**
2. Install any available updates

**Game consoles:**
- **PlayStation 5:** Settings → System → System Software → System Software Update and Settings → Update System Software
- **Xbox Series X/S:** Press Xbox button → Profile & system → Settings → System → Updates → Console update available

**iPhone / iPad:**
1. Go to **Settings → General → Software Update**
2. Install any available update

**Android:**
1. Go to **Settings → System → System update** (on Samsung: **Settings → Software update**)
2. Check for and install updates

**Windows PC:**
1. Go to **Settings → Windows Update → Check for updates**
2. Install all available updates

**Mac:**
1. **Apple menu → System Settings → General → Software Update**

**Fix 2 — Update the Netflix app specifically:**

- **Smart TV, Roku, Fire Stick, Apple TV:** App updates usually happen automatically. To force an update, reinstall the app (use the steps from Step 2).
- **iPhone / iPad:** Open the **App Store → tap your profile icon** (top-right) → scroll down to Available Updates → find Netflix → tap **Update**
- **Android:** Open the **Google Play Store → tap your profile icon → Manage apps & device → Updates available → find Netflix → Update**
- **Windows PC (Microsoft Store app):** Open the **Microsoft Store → Library → Get updates** → if Netflix has an update, it will install automatically

**Fix 3 — Try an alternate device:**

If Netflix works on your phone but not on your TV (or the other way around), this tells you something important: the problem is with the specific device, not your Netflix account or your internet.

1. Try watching Netflix on a different device in the same room, on the same WiFi
2. If Netflix works on that device: the problematic device needs a deeper fix (continue to Step 5)
3. If Netflix also doesn't work on the second device: the problem is your internet or your Netflix account (go back to Step 1 and Step 3)

![A person holding a smartphone with Netflix playing smoothly next to a TV stuck on the Netflix loading screen](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People see that Netflix works on their phone and assume their TV is broken. Sometimes the TV is fine — its older WiFi chip simply can't reach the router as well as the newer phone can. Before blaming the TV, test Netflix on your phone while standing right next to the TV. If the phone also buffers in that spot, the issue is WiFi strength in that location — not the TV.

## Step 5: Fix Specific Netflix Error Codes and Network Settings

If you're seeing a specific error code on screen, Netflix is trying to tell you exactly what's wrong. These are the most common codes and what they mean.

**Error NW-2-5 (or NW-3-6, NW-4-7): "Netflix encountered an error. Please try again."**
This is a network connectivity error — your device can't reach Netflix's servers. Try: restarting your device and router (Step 1), then check your DNS settings below.

**Error UI-800-3 (or UI-113): "Could not connect to Netflix."**
This is a stored data problem — the Netflix app's saved information is corrupted. Fix: clear the Netflix app data (Step 2) and sign back in.

**Error M7111-1331 (or M7121-3078, M7353): Browser-related error (watching on a computer)**
You're watching in a web browser and something is blocking Netflix. Fix:
1. Close ALL browser windows (completely quit the browser)
2. Reopen your browser and go to netflix.com
3. If still not working: try a different browser. Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and Safari all support Netflix — if Netflix works in one but not another, the problem browser has a corrupted extension or stored data
4. Clear your browser cache completely (Settings → Privacy & Security → Clear browsing data → select "All time" → check "Cached images and files" → Clear data)

**Error TVQ-ST-103 (or TVQ-PB-101): Slow or unstable internet**
Your internet is too slow for streaming. Fix: run the speed test at fast.com (Step 1, Fix 2). If your speed is below Netflix's minimum, follow the slow internet fixes. If your speed is fine but you still get this error, your WiFi is unreliable — try connecting your device with an Ethernet cable instead of WiFi (many Smart TVs, game consoles, and streaming boxes have an Ethernet port).

**Error 100 (or Error 11800, Error AVF): Device compatibility or HDCP error**
Your TV or streaming device is having trouble playing protected content. This is most common on older TVs and some computer setups.
1. Unplug your TV and your streaming device from power for 1 minute (Step 1)
2. If using an HDMI cable, try a different HDMI port on your TV — some ports don't support HDCP (High-bandwidth Digital Content Protection)
3. Try a different HDMI cable — old or damaged cables can cause HDCP handshake failures
4. If on a computer: try a different browser, or lower your screen resolution

**Fix — Change your DNS settings (advanced fix for NW errors and region problems):**

If you keep getting NW- errors after restarting everything, your internet provider's DNS servers (which translate website names into computer addresses) might be slow or blocking Netflix.

**On a Smart TV or streaming device (if your device allows manual DNS):**
1. Go to your device's **Network Settings** (usually under Settings → Network)
2. Change DNS from **Automatic** to **Manual**
3. Enter these numbers for the **Primary DNS:** `8.8.8.8` and **Secondary DNS:** `8.8.4.4` (these are Google's free public DNS servers — fast and reliable)
4. Save and restart your device

**On your router (this fixes it for ALL devices on your network):**
1. Open a web browser and go to your router's address — usually `192.168.0.1` or `192.168.1.1` (check the sticker on your router)
2. Log in with the admin username and password (often "admin" / "password" — also on the sticker)
3. Find the **DNS** or **Internet Setup** section
4. Change DNS from "Automatic from ISP" to **Manual**
5. Enter `8.8.8.8` as Primary and `8.8.4.4` as Secondary
6. Save and restart your router

![A TV screen showing the Netflix error code NW-2-5 with the "Try Again" button](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People see an error code and immediately call Netflix support without trying the restart first. Netflix support will also ask you to restart your device — it's always the first step. Save yourself the phone call and try restarting (Step 1) and clearing app data (Step 2) before googling error codes. Also, when changing DNS settings, people sometimes type the numbers wrong — it's `8.8.8.8` and `8.8.4.4`, not `8.8.8.8.8` or other variations.

> **Warning:** Changing DNS settings on your router affects your entire home network. While Google's DNS (8.8.8.8) is safe and widely used, some internet providers use custom DNS for their services. If you change your router's DNS and other apps or services stop working, switch it back to Automatic.

## Still Stuck?

- **Netflix only buffers during certain times of day (like evenings)?** This is almost always an internet congestion problem. Evenings are when everyone in your neighborhood is streaming, gaming, and video-calling — all at the same time. Your internet provider's network gets overloaded. You can: (1) watch at a different time, (2) lower your Netflix data usage to "Low" (Step 3, Fix 1), which requires less bandwidth and buffers less during peak hours, or (3) contact your internet provider to see if they offer a faster plan.

- **Netflix only buffers on ONE specific TV, but works fine on every other device?** That specific TV may have a weaker WiFi antenna or be too far from the router. Try: (1) moving the router closer, (2) getting a WiFi extender ($20-40), or (3) connecting the TV directly to the router with an Ethernet cable. Most Smart TVs have an Ethernet port on the back — a wired connection is always more stable than WiFi.

- **Netflix works but the video quality looks blurry or pixelated?** Netflix starts every video at a low quality and ramps up to HD or 4K as your internet speed allows. If your internet speed fluctuates, Netflix may never ramp up. Fix: go to your Netflix Account → Playback settings → Data usage per screen → set to "High" (Step 3, Fix 1). Also, check your Netflix plan — the "Standard with ads" plan only supports 1080p HD, not 4K. You need the Premium plan for 4K Ultra HD.

- **Getting the message "Your account can't be used on this device" or "Too many devices"?** Netflix has been cracking down on password sharing. If you're using someone else's account or Netflix detects you're not in the primary household, you'll get this message. The account owner needs to add you as an "extra member" ($7.99/month in the US) or you'll need your own account. This is a policy change, not a technical bug — there's no way around it.

- **Netflix app won't even open — crashes immediately?** On Android: go to **Settings → Apps → Netflix → Force Stop**, then reopen it. On iPhone/iPad: delete and reinstall the app (Step 2). On Smart TVs: if the app crashes immediately after opening, a full factory reset of the TV (Settings → General → Reset) is a last resort — but this erases all your TV settings.

- **Tried everything and Netflix still doesn't work?** Contact Netflix support directly: go to [help.netflix.com](https://help.netflix.com) and click **Contact Us** at the bottom of the page. You can chat or call them — they're available 24/7. Before contacting them, write down: (1) your device type and model, (2) the exact error code or what the screen shows, (3) what steps you've already tried. This saves time on the support call.

- **Considering canceling Netflix?** If buffering and loading problems are so frequent that Netflix is unwatchable, and you've worked through every step in this guide, the problem may be your internet service — not Netflix. Consider switching internet providers or upgrading to a faster plan before canceling. You can also try downloading shows to your phone or tablet (using the Netflix app's download feature) and watching them without any buffering — downloads play from your device's storage with zero streaming required.
