## Before You Start

You open Netflix, it shows the logo for a second, then kicks you back to the home screen. YouTube opens but freezes on a black screen. Prime Video loads, but crashes the moment you hit play. One broken app is annoying — but when *all* your TV apps start crashing, it feels like your whole smart TV just became dumb.

Here's what's actually happening: smart TVs are basically small computers. They have limited memory, limited storage, and an operating system that builds up junk over time — just like a phone or laptop. When the memory gets full, or an app's cache gets corrupted, or the TV's software is out of date, apps start crashing. In most cases, the fix takes under five minutes and doesn't require any tools.

**What you'll need:**
- Your TV remote
- Your WiFi network name and password
- A phone or computer connected to the same WiFi (to verify your internet is working)

> **Work through the steps in order.** Each fix builds on the last. Don't skip ahead — the early fixes solve most cases and take under two minutes.

## Step 1: Cold Boot Your TV

Here's something most people don't realize: when you press the power button on your TV remote, the TV doesn't actually turn off. It goes into **standby mode** — like closing a laptop lid instead of shutting it down. The TV's computer keeps running, memory stays filled, and any glitch that's causing apps to crash just stays there waiting for you to come back.

A **cold boot** forces the TV to fully shut down and restart from scratch — clearing the memory and wiping temporary glitches. This single step fixes more app-crashing problems than everything else combined.

**Fix 1 — Cold boot your TV**

1. **With the remote (works on most smart TVs):** Press and **hold the Power button** on your remote for 5-10 seconds. Don't just tap it — keep holding. The TV screen will go black, and after a few seconds, you'll see the manufacturer's logo (Samsung, LG, Sony, TCL, etc.) appear as the TV restarts itself
2. **If the remote method doesn't work:** Unplug the TV's power cable from the wall outlet. Wait a full **60 seconds** — set a timer on your phone. Then plug it back in and turn the TV on normally
3. Once the TV is back on, open the app that was crashing and test it

![A TV remote with the power button highlighted, showing how to press and hold for a cold boot](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People tap the power button, see the screen go black, and think they restarted. That's standby mode — the TV's computer didn't actually restart. You MUST hold the power button for at least 5 seconds until you see the manufacturer's logo appear on screen. If you only see a brief black screen and then your show resumes, you didn't hold long enough. The logo appearing is your proof that a real restart happened.

> **Tip:** Make cold booting a monthly habit, even when nothing's wrong. Think of it like restarting your phone — it clears out junk you can't see and prevents the crashes from happening in the first place. If your TV is wall-mounted with the power cord hidden behind the wall, the remote method (holding the power button) is your best friend.

## Step 2: Check Your Internet Connection

Apps crash for two reasons: something is wrong with the app, or something is wrong with the connection to the internet. Even if your TV says "Connected" in the corner of the screen, the connection might be too weak or unstable to actually stream video — and many apps crash instead of showing a graceful error message when the internet cuts out mid-load.

**Fix 2a — Verify your internet is actually working**

1. Grab your phone or laptop **while connected to the same WiFi network as your TV**
2. Open YouTube or Netflix and play a video for 30 seconds. Does it load and play smoothly?
3. If your phone also struggles to load videos, the problem is your home internet — not your TV. Restart your router and modem (unplug both, wait 60 seconds, plug the modem back in first, wait 2 minutes, then plug the router back in)
4. If your phone plays videos perfectly but your TV apps still crash, continue to Fix 2b

**Fix 2b — Test signal strength on your TV**

1. Go to your TV's network settings:
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > Connection > Network > Network Status
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > Network > Wi-Fi Connection
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > Network & Internet > select your WiFi network
   - **Roku TV (TCL/Hisense):** Settings > Network > Check Connection
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > Network > highlight your WiFi name > press Play/Pause button
2. Look for your signal strength. It should say "Very Good" or "Excellent." If it says "Fair," "Poor," or you see only one or two bars out of four, your WiFi signal is too weak
3. **Quick fix for weak signal:** Move your router closer to the TV, or move the TV away from large metal objects (TV stands, metal shelves, fish tanks, mirrors) that block WiFi signals. Even moving the router two feet can make a difference

![A TV screen showing the Network Status menu with signal strength indicated as "Poor"](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People see the WiFi icon on their TV and assume the connection is fine. But the icon only tells you the TV is connected to the router — it doesn't tell you if the internet is actually working or if the signal is strong enough to stream video. Think of it like having one bar of cell service: your phone says "connected," but you can't actually load a webpage. Always run the network status check from the TV's settings menu to see the real numbers.

> **Tip:** If your TV is far from your router and moving either one isn't practical, try switching from the 5 GHz WiFi band to 2.4 GHz. 5 GHz is faster but has shorter range — it struggles through walls and across long distances. 2.4 GHz is a bit slower but reaches much farther and is often more stable for streaming. On most TVs, you can see both bands in the WiFi list — 2.4 GHz networks usually just show the network name, while 5 GHz networks have "5G" or "5GHz" at the end.

## Step 3: Clear the App Cache and Reinstall the Problem App

Every app on your smart TV stores temporary files called "cache" — things like thumbnail images, recently watched lists, and partial video data. Over weeks or months of use, this cache can grow large or get corrupted, especially on TVs with limited storage (which is most smart TVs). A corrupted cache is like a scratched CD — the app tries to read it, gets confused, and crashes.

Some smart TVs let you clear an app's cache directly. Others don't, and you'll need to delete and reinstall the app. Both approaches solve the same problem.

**Fix 3a — Clear the app cache (if your TV supports it)**

1. Go to your TV's app management menu:
   - **Samsung (2017+):** Settings > Support > Device Care > Manage Storage. Select the crashing app and choose **Clear Cache**
   - **Samsung (older):** Settings > Apps > select the app > **Clear Cache**
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > Applications > Manage Installed Applications > select the app > **Clear Cache**
   - **Android/Google TV (Sony, Hisense):** Settings > Apps > See all apps > select the app > **Clear Cache**
2. Look at the cache size. If it's more than 50-100 MB, you've found a likely culprit
3. Select **Clear Cache** (do NOT select "Clear Data" — that deletes your login and all app settings)
4. Go back and repeat for any other apps that are crashing
5. Cold boot your TV again (hold the power button for 5+ seconds), then test the apps

![A Samsung TV Manage Storage screen showing the Clear Cache button for the Netflix app, with cache size displayed](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 3b — Delete and reinstall the app (works on all TVs)**

If your TV doesn't have a "Clear Cache" option, or clearing the cache didn't work:

> **Note:** Deleting an app signs you out of it completely. Have your login email and password ready before you delete. If you use a password manager on your phone, check it for the app's login before proceeding.

1. Navigate to the app on your TV's home screen
2. **Samsung:** Highlight the app, press and hold the Select/OK button, then choose **Delete** (if Delete is grayed out, choose **Reinstall** instead)
   **LG:** Press and hold the Wheel (OK) button while highlighting the app, then select **Delete**
   **Roku TV:** Highlight the app, press the Star (*) button, then select **Remove channel**
   **Fire TV:** Highlight the app, press the Menu button (three lines), then select **Uninstall**
   **Android/Google TV:** Highlight the app, press and hold Select, then choose **Uninstall**
3. Confirm the deletion, then restart your TV (cold boot)
4. After the TV restarts, go to the App Store or Channel Store on your TV and search for the app by name
5. Download and install it again
6. Open the app and sign in. Test a video

![An LG TV home screen with the YouTube app highlighted and the Delete option appearing in a popup menu](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People delete an app but forget to restart the TV before reinstalling. The old app's corrupted data can linger in the TV's memory even after deletion. Always cold boot between deleting and reinstalling — that forces the TV to fully flush out the old app's leftovers. If you reinstall without a restart, you might be putting a fresh app on top of a dirty foundation.

> **Common mistake #2:** People click "Clear Data" instead of "Clear Cache" thinking they do the same thing. Clear Data wipes everything — your login, your watch history, your saved preferences, and any downloaded content. Clear Cache only removes temporary junk files. On Samsung and Fire TVs, these two buttons are right next to each other and the labels look similar. Read carefully before you click.

> **Tip:** If only ONE app is crashing and all your other apps work fine, the problem is that specific app — not your TV. Uninstall and reinstall it once, and if it still crashes, check if other users are reporting the same problem. Go to your phone, search "[app name] crashing on [your TV brand] June 2026" — you might find it's a known bug from a recent app update, and the app developer is already working on a fix. In that case, you just have to wait for them to push an update.

## Step 4: Update Your TV's Software (Firmware)

Your smart TV runs an operating system — Tizen (Samsung), webOS (LG), Google TV (Sony, Hisense, TCL), Roku OS (TCL Roku TVs), or Fire OS (Amazon Fire TVs). Like any operating system, it gets updates that fix bugs — including bugs that cause apps to crash. If your TV's software is several versions behind, apps built for the latest version may crash on your older system.

Most smart TVs update automatically, but the auto-update feature sometimes fails silently — especially if the TV was turned off during the scheduled update time or if the storage is too full to download the update.

**Fix 4 — Check for and install software updates**

1. Go to your TV's software update menu:
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > Support > Software Update > Update Now
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > System > Software Update > Check for Updates
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > System > About > System Update > Check for Update
   - **Roku TV (TCL/Hisense):** Settings > System > System Update > Check Now
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > My Fire TV > About > Check for Updates
   - **Vizio SmartCast:** Settings > System > Check for Updates
2. If an update is found, select **Update Now** or **Download and Install**
3. **Do not touch anything** while the update installs. The TV will likely restart itself one or more times. The screen may go black for a few minutes — this is normal. Do NOT unplug the TV or press any buttons during the update
4. Once the TV restarts to the home screen, open the app that was crashing and test it

![A Sony Google TV System Update screen showing "Update available — Android TV OS 12" with a Download button](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 4b — If your TV can't download updates (no internet or update fails)**

If your TV can't get online to download updates, or if the on-screen update keeps failing, use a USB drive:

1. On a computer or phone that has internet access, go to your TV manufacturer's support website:
   - Samsung: samsung.com/us/support/downloads
   - LG: lg.com/us/support/software-firmware-drivers
   - Sony: sony.com/electronics/support/downloads
2. Search for your TV's exact model number. The model number is printed on a sticker on the back of the TV, on the original box, or in the TV's Settings > About menu. It looks like "QN65Q80CAFXZA" (Samsung), "OLED55C4PUA" (LG), or "XR-55A80L" (Sony)
3. Download the latest firmware file to your computer. It'll usually be a .zip or .exe file
4. If it's a .zip, right-click and select **Extract All**. If it's a .exe, double-click to extract. Copy ALL extracted files and folders to the **root** of an empty USB flash drive (do NOT put them inside any folder on the USB drive)
5. Plug the USB drive into a USB port on the back or side of your TV
6. On the TV, go to the Software Update menu (same paths as above) and select **Update Now**. The TV will detect the firmware on the USB and begin installing
7. Wait for the update to complete — this can take 10-20 minutes. Do not unplug the USB drive or turn off the TV

![A USB flash drive being inserted into the USB port on the back of a smart TV](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People start a software update, get impatient when the TV screen goes black for several minutes, and pull the power plug to "try again." This can permanently damage the TV's software — a condition called "bricking" that requires professional repair. When your TV is updating, the black screen is part of the process. Set a timer for 30 minutes and walk away. If the TV hasn't recovered after 30 minutes, then you can start troubleshooting the failed update — but interrupting it early is how TVs get permanently broken.

> **Tip:** While waiting for the update, use your phone to check if your TV manufacturer has a support forum or subreddit. Searching "[your TV brand] [your model number] update stuck" can tell you how long that specific update normally takes and whether other users had issues with it. Some LG updates, for example, can take over 30 minutes on older models — and knowing that ahead of time saves you from panicking.

## Step 5: Reset Smart Hub or Factory Reset the TV

If apps are still crashing after cold booting, checking your internet, clearing caches, reinstalling apps, and updating the software, the problem is deeper — the TV's operating system itself may have corrupted system files. This happens when a software update fails partway through, when the TV's storage gets so full the system can't write temporary files, or when a power outage corrupts the TV's internal memory.

At this stage, you have two options: a Smart Hub reset (Samsung) or their equivalents on other brands, which resets the app platform without wiping the whole TV — or a full factory reset, which wipes everything.

**Fix 5a — Reset Smart Hub (Samsung) or equivalent platform reset**

> **Note:** Resetting Smart Hub signs you out of your Samsung account and all apps, and removes all downloaded apps. Your TV's picture settings, sound settings, and WiFi password are NOT affected. You'll need to re-download and sign into your apps afterward.

1. **Samsung (2019+):** Settings > All Settings > Support > Device Care > Self Diagnosis > Reset Smart Hub
   **Samsung (2018 and earlier):** Settings > All Settings > Support > Self Diagnosis > Reset Smart Hub
2. If asked for a PIN, enter **0000** (the default Samsung PIN, unless you changed it yourself)
3. Confirm the reset and wait for the TV to restart
4. The TV will walk you through Smart Hub setup — accept the terms, then sign in to your Samsung account (or skip this step)
5. Re-download your apps from the App Store and sign into each one
6. Test the apps that were crashing

![The Samsung TV Self Diagnosis menu with Reset Smart Hub highlighted](PLACEHOLDER)

**For other TV brands — platform reset equivalents:**
- **LG (webOS):** Settings > All Settings > General > Reset to Initial Settings. This resets the whole TV, not just the app platform — LG doesn't have a separate app-reset option
- **Roku TV (TCL/Hisense):** Settings > System > Advanced System Settings > Factory Reset. Roku doesn't have a partial reset option
- **Fire TV:** Settings > My Fire TV > Reset to Factory Defaults
- **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > System > About > Factory Reset
- **Vizio SmartCast:** Settings > Admin & Privacy > Reset TV to Factory Defaults

**Fix 5b — Full factory reset (last resort — works on all TVs)**

> **Warning:** A factory reset erases EVERYTHING. All apps, all logins, all passwords, your picture settings, your sound settings, your WiFi network password, and any custom channel lists are completely wiped. Your TV will restart as if you just unboxed it. Before resetting, write down or take a photo of your important settings so you can set them up again.

1. Go to your TV's factory reset menu using the paths listed above
2. You may be asked for a PIN. The default is usually **0000** for Samsung and LG, or **1234** for some older Sony models. If you set a custom PIN and can't remember it, try 0000 first
3. Read the warning screen carefully — it will list everything that will be erased
4. Select **Reset** or **Factory Reset** to confirm
5. The TV will shut down, show a progress bar or spinning icon for 5-15 minutes, then restart to the initial setup screen
6. Follow the on-screen setup: choose language, connect to WiFi, sign in to your TV manufacturer account if you have one
7. When the setup asks about data collection, privacy, and advertising settings, say **no** to everything — these features run in the background and use up memory and processing power that you want available for your apps
8. Re-download your streaming apps from the App Store and sign into each one
9. Test the apps

![A TV screen showing a factory reset confirmation dialog with a warning message and Reset button](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People factory reset the TV, go through the lengthy setup process, and then immediately restore their old settings from a cloud backup — which includes the exact same corrupted data that caused the apps to crash in the first place. If your TV offers to restore from a backup during setup, say NO. Set everything up from scratch. Yes, it takes more time, but restoring a backup after a factory reset is like cleaning your house by sweeping all the dirt into a closet — you didn't actually fix anything.

> **Common mistake #2:** After a factory reset, people load up the TV with every app they've ever downloaded. Smart TVs have limited storage — usually 4 GB to 8 GB total, and the operating system takes up half of that. If you install 20+ apps, you'll eventually run out of space, and apps will start crashing again. After a factory reset, only install the 5-6 apps you actually use regularly. Treat your TV's storage like a small closet — if you haven't opened an app in three months, you don't need it.

> **Tip:** If you're doing a factory reset because apps keep crashing, take the opportunity to fix your WiFi setup too. Before resetting, move your router closer to the TV, or switch to the 2.4 GHz band (more reliable range), or run an Ethernet cable from the router to the TV if possible. A factory reset won't fix a bad WiFi signal — and a weak connection is one of the top causes of app crashes.

## Still Stuck?

- **Only one specific app crashes, even after reinstalling?** The app developer likely released a buggy update. Check your phone: go to the app's subreddit, Twitter/X account, or support page and search for recent crash reports. If it's a known issue, the developer will usually post an update within a few days. In the meantime, try using that service through a different device — for example, cast Netflix from your phone to the TV using Chromecast or AirPlay instead of using the TV's built-in Netflix app.

- **Apps crash only when you try to play 4K or HDR content?** Your internet speed might not be fast enough. Netflix recommends at least 15 Mbps for 4K streaming. Run a speed test on your phone while standing next to your TV (search "internet speed test" on Google and use the built-in tool). If your speed is below 15 Mbps, try lowering the video quality in the app's settings, or upgrade your internet plan.

- **TV storage is completely full?** Smart TVs show a "Storage Full" or "Insufficient Storage" message when there's no room left for app data. Go to your TV's storage settings and uninstall every app you don't regularly use. Also check if the TV has any downloaded videos or screen recordings taking up space. On Samsung TVs, a common culprit is the built-in screen recording feature accidentally saving large video files.

- **TV is more than 5-6 years old and apps keep crashing no matter what?** Smart TV hardware ages quickly. Older TVs have slower processors and less memory than newer models, and modern apps (especially Netflix, YouTube, and Prime Video) get bigger and more demanding every year. If your TV is older and can't keep up, you don't need to buy a new TV — just plug a streaming stick (Roku, Fire Stick, Chromecast with Google TV, or Apple TV) into one of your TV's HDMI ports. A $30 streaming stick has a faster processor than most 5-year-old smart TVs, and it'll run all your apps smoothly while you keep using the same screen.

- **TV freezes completely — not just apps, but the whole menu system?** This is usually a hardware problem — overheating, failing internal storage, or a failing power supply. If a factory reset doesn't fix it, the TV needs professional repair. Before calling a repair shop, try unplugging the TV overnight (8+ hours). Sometimes the internal components need a long power drain to fully reset. If the freezing comes back the next day, it's almost certainly a hardware issue.
