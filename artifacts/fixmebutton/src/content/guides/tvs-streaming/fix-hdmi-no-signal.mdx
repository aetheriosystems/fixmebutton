## Before You Start

You sit down to watch your favorite show, press the power button — and your screen shows a blank, empty message: "HDMI 1 — No Signal." Or maybe the message floats in a blue box, or bounces around the screen like a screensaver. You check the cable, you try another input, you restart everything — and that same message stares back at you.

"No Signal" means your TV is listening on the right channel, but nothing is talking back. The cable box, streaming stick, game console, or laptop you've plugged in isn't sending video the TV can understand. The good news: in most cases, this is NOT a broken TV. It's a connection problem, a wrong setting, or a device that needs a restart — all fixable at home in minutes.

**What you'll need:**
- Your TV remote
- Your device's remote or power button (cable box, game console, streaming stick)
- A spare HDMI cable (just in case)
- A second device with HDMI output — like a laptop, game console, or spare streaming stick (to help narrow down the problem)

> **Work through the steps in order.** Each fix builds on the last. The first two fixes solve more than 70% of all "No Signal" cases and take under three minutes each.

## Fix 1: Select the Correct Input and Power Cycle Both Devices

Most "No Signal" problems come from one of two simple things: the TV is on the wrong HDMI input, or the external device needs a restart. Before you touch a single cable, rule these out.

**Fix 1a — Cycle through every HDMI input on your TV**

1. Press the **Input**, **Source**, or **TV/Video** button on your TV remote. On most remotes, this button looks like a rectangle with an arrow pointing into it — but it might also say "Source," "Input," or have a picture of a square with a line
2. A menu appears listing all your TV's inputs: HDMI 1, HDMI 2, HDMI 3, sometimes AV or Component. If you can see this menu, your TV screen is working — the problem is external
3. Select each HDMI input one at a time, waiting a full **10 seconds** on each one. Some devices take several seconds to wake up and start sending a signal after they're selected
4. If you find the right input and a picture appears, you've solved the problem. If you still see "No Signal" on every HDMI input, move on to Fix 1b

![A TV remote with the Input/Source button highlighted, and the on-screen input selection menu showing HDMI 1, HDMI 2, HDMI 3](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 1b — Power cycle both your TV and the external device**

Your cable box, streaming stick, or game console is a tiny computer. Like any computer, it can freeze or glitch and stop sending a video signal. A full power cycle clears those glitches.

1. Turn off both your TV and the external device (cable box, streaming stick, game console, or laptop) using their power buttons or remotes
2. **Unplug BOTH devices from power.** Unplug the TV's power cord from the wall. Unplug the external device's power cord too. Do NOT skip either one — both need a full power-off
3. For streaming sticks that plug directly into the TV's HDMI port and draw power from the TV's USB port, unplug the stick from BOTH the HDMI port and the USB power port
4. Wait a full **60 seconds.** Set a timer on your phone. This lets the internal capacitors in both devices fully drain so the systems reset completely
5. Plug the external device back in FIRST and wait 30 seconds for it to fully boot up. You should see its power light come on and stabilize
6. Plug the TV back in and turn it on
7. Press the Input button and select the correct HDMI port
8. Check if you have a picture

![Two power cords unplugged — one from a TV, one from a cable box — with a phone timer showing a 60-second countdown](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People unplug the TV, count to five, and plug it back in. Modern electronics hold a charge for 30 seconds or more — five seconds isn't enough for anything to actually reset. You MUST do the full 60-second wait, and you MUST unplug BOTH the TV and the external device. Power cycling just the TV while the cable box is still frozen solves nothing. Both ends of the connection need a fresh start.

> **Common mistake #2:** People press the Input button, see "HDMI 1" highlighted, and assume they're on the correct input. The label tells you which port is selected — it does NOT tell you that something is plugged into that port or that the device is turned on. A "No Signal" message with "HDMI 1" in the corner means the TV is watching HDMI 1 and waiting — but nothing is sending video there. Before you do anything else, make sure your external device is actually powered on. Look for a green light, a glowing logo, or any sign of life on the device itself.

## Fix 2: Check Every Cable Connection and Try a Different HDMI Cable

HDMI cables are sensitive. They can work loose over time, get damaged by pets or vacuum cleaners, or simply fail after years of use. Even a cable that looks fine on the outside can have broken wires inside. The connection at either end — TV side or device side — may be partial, allowing the TV to sense something is plugged in but not enough to carry the video signal.

**Fix 2 — Physically inspect, reseat, and if needed, replace the HDMI cable**

1. Go to the back of your TV and find the HDMI cable plugged into the port you're using. Make note of which port number it's in (HDMI 1, HDMI 2, etc.)
2. **Unplug the HDMI cable** from the TV completely. Look at the metal connector on the end — check for bent pins, rust, or damage. If the connector looks crushed or the gold pins are bent, the cable is damaged
3. Plug the HDMI cable back in firmly. Push until you feel it click or seat fully — a properly connected HDMI cable should not wiggle or feel loose
4. Follow the HDMI cable to the other end — where it plugs into your cable box, streaming stick, game console, or laptop. Unplug it there too and inspect the connector
5. Plug it back in firmly on the device side. Make sure it's in the HDMI OUT or HDMI video output port — some devices (like game consoles and cable boxes) have only one HDMI port, but some have multiple
6. Check both ends of the connection. Turn the devices back on and test

![A close-up of hands unplugging and replugging an HDMI cable into the back of a TV, with the HDMI port labels clearly visible](PLACEHOLDER)

**If reseating doesn't fix the problem, try a different HDMI cable:**

1. Borrow an HDMI cable from another device in your house that you know works — from a different TV, a game console, or a computer monitor
2. Swap out the old cable with the known-working cable. Connect the same two devices with the new cable
3. Turn everything on and test. If you get a picture, the old cable was bad — throw it out and keep using the new one
4. If you don't have a spare cable to borrow, buy a standard HDMI cable (any $6–$10 cable works for most setups). You do NOT need an expensive "high-speed" or "4K" cable unless you're running 4K at 120Hz for gaming, or your cable is longer than 25 feet

**Try a different HDMI port on the TV:**

1. Move the HDMI cable from the current port (say, HDMI 1) to a different port (say, HDMI 2 or HDMI 3)
2. Press the Input button on your remote and select the new port (HDMI 2)
3. If you get a picture on the new port, the original HDMI port may be physically damaged. This is fixable — you can simply keep using the working port permanently. The only inconvenience is if you run out of ports and need to use the broken one

![A person swapping an HDMI cable from HDMI Port 1 to HDMI Port 2 on the back of a TV](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People see the HDMI cable is plugged in and assume it must be working. HDMI cables can fail internally — the wires inside can break even though the plastic outside looks perfect. If you've been using the same HDMI cable for 5+ years, or if the cable has been bent sharply around furniture corners, it's a strong suspect. Always test with a known-working cable before assuming the TV or device is broken. A $6 cable swap fixes an unbelievable number of "No Signal" problems.

> **Common mistake #2:** Some devices — especially cable boxes and satellite receivers — have both an HDMI port and older analog ports (red/white/yellow RCA jacks, or component ports). Make sure you're plugged into the **HDMI OUT** port on the device, not the analog outputs. If the device is sending video through the analog ports but you're watching the HDMI input on the TV, you'll get "No Signal" forever. Look at the back of your device: the HDMI port is the thin, rectangular one — it's unmistakably different from the round analog jacks.

## Fix 3: Isolate the Problem by Testing Different Devices, Cables, and TVs

When Fix 1 and Fix 2 don't solve the problem, you need to figure out exactly which piece of equipment is broken — the TV, the HDMI cable, or the external device. This swap-test method is exactly what professional technicians do. By testing each component with known-working replacements, you narrow down the culprit.

**Fix 3 — Systematic swap testing**

Follow these tests in order. Stop as soon as you find the broken piece.

**Test A: Different device, same TV, same cable**

1. Unplug your current device (cable box, streaming stick, game console) from the HDMI cable
2. Connect a **different device** you know works to the same HDMI cable and same TV port. Good test devices: a laptop with HDMI output, a game console from another room, a streaming stick from a different TV, or a DVD/Blu-ray player
3. Turn on the test device and select the same HDMI input on the TV
4. **If you get a picture:** The TV and cable are fine. Your original external device is the problem — it may need a factory reset, repair, or replacement
5. **If you still get "No Signal":** The problem is either the TV, the HDMI cable, or the HDMI port. Move on to Test B

**Test B: Same device, same TV, different HDMI cable**

1. Replace the HDMI cable with a known-working cable borrowed from another setup
2. Test with your original device
3. **If you get a picture:** The old cable was bad. Replace it permanently
4. **If you still get "No Signal":** Move on to Test C

**Test C: Same device, same cable, different HDMI port on the TV**

1. Move the HDMI cable to a different HDMI port on your TV (like HDMI 3 instead of HDMI 1)
2. Select the new input on the TV and test
3. **If you get a picture:** The original HDMI port is damaged. Use the working port going forward
4. **If you still get "No Signal" on every port:** Move on to Test D

**Test D: Same device and cable, different TV**

1. Take your external device and HDMI cable to a different TV in your house (or a friend's house)
2. Plug everything in and test
3. **If you get a picture:** Your original TV has a problem — its HDMI ports or internal HDMI board may be damaged. Skip to Fix 4 and Fix 5 for software fixes, or consult a repair shop for hardware repair
4. **If you still get "No Signal" on a different TV:** Your external device is almost certainly the problem. Try factory resetting the device, or contact its manufacturer for support

![A diagram showing a TV connected to three different devices — a game console, a streaming stick, and a laptop — with checkmarks and X marks showing which combinations work and which don't](PLACEHOLDER)

**Results summary:**

| What you tested | If picture appears | The problem is... |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Different device on same TV/cable | Yes | Original external device |
| Different HDMI cable | Yes | Old HDMI cable is bad |
| Different HDMI port on TV | Yes | Original HDMI port is damaged |
| Same setup on different TV | Yes | Original TV has an HDMI problem |
| Nothing works, anywhere | — | External device likely broken |

> **Common mistake:** People test one thing — like swapping the cable — see it doesn't fix the problem, and stop. Then they assume the TV is broken and spend hundreds on a new one. You must test ALL the components — device, cable, port, and TV — before you can confidently say which piece failed. Sometimes two things break at once (a bad HDMI port AND a dying streaming stick). The only way to be sure is to test each component individually with a known-working replacement. This takes 15 minutes and can save you from buying a new TV when all you needed was a new streaming stick.

> **Tip:** If you don't have spare equipment lying around, use a laptop as your test device. Almost every laptop made in the last 10 years has an HDMI output port. Connect the laptop to your TV with an HDMI cable. If the laptop's desktop appears on your TV screen, your TV and cable are working — the problem is your original external device. This single test answers the biggest question in 30 seconds: is my TV broken or not?

## Fix 4: Check Display Resolution and HDMI-CEC/HDCP Settings

If your TV detects a device is plugged in (the input label isn't grayed out) but still shows "No Signal," there may be a settings mismatch. The device might be sending a video resolution your TV can't display, or there's a handshake problem between the two devices — a communication failure in the HDMI connection itself.

**Fix 4a — Lower the external device's resolution (if you can access it)**

Sometimes a device is set to output 4K, but your TV can only handle 1080p. Or the device is set to a refresh rate your TV doesn't support. The TV rejects the signal because it can't understand it.

**For game consoles (PlayStation, Xbox, Nintendo Switch):**

1. If you can get a picture on a different TV or monitor, connect the console there first
2. Go to the console's video settings and set the resolution to **1080p** (or "Automatic") instead of 4K
3. Disable HDR (High Dynamic Range) temporarily
4. Save the settings, then reconnect the console to your original TV
5. Once you get a picture, you can try increasing the resolution one step at a time to find what your TV supports

**For streaming sticks (Fire Stick, Roku, Chromecast, Apple TV):**

1. Connect the streaming stick to a different TV or monitor where it works
2. Go to the device's display settings and set the resolution to **1080p** or **Auto**
3. On Fire Stick: Settings > Display & Sounds > Display > Video Resolution > set to 1080p
4. On Roku: Settings > Display type > set to 1080p
5. On Apple TV: Settings > Video and Audio > Format > set to 1080p SDR
6. Reconnect to your original TV and test

**For cable boxes and satellite receivers:**

1. There's usually a physical **RESOLUTION** button on the front of the box or on the remote. Press it repeatedly to cycle through available resolutions (480p → 720p → 1080i → 1080p). Wait 10 seconds on each one to see if a picture appears
2. If you can't find a resolution button, unplug the cable box for 60 seconds, then plug it back in while holding the **Power** button or **Menu** button on the box itself — many boxes boot into a "safe mode" at the lowest resolution when started this way

![A game console video settings screen showing resolution options: 720p, 1080p, 4K, with 1080p highlighted](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 4b — Toggle HDMI-CEC and HDCP settings**

HDMI-CEC (Consumer Electronics Control) is a feature that lets your TV remote control other devices — like your TV remote controlling your soundbar volume. HDCP (High-bandwidth Digital Content Protection) is copy protection that verifies your devices are allowed to play protected content. Either can cause "No Signal" if they're misconfigured.

1. On your TV, turn HDMI-CEC OFF temporarily:
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > Connection > External Device Manager > Anynet+ (HDMI-CEC) > turn OFF
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > External Devices > HDMI Settings > SIMPLINK (HDMI-CEC) > turn OFF
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > External Inputs > Bravia Sync Settings > Bravia Sync > turn OFF
   - **Roku TV:** Settings > System > Control other devices (CEC) > uncheck all boxes
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > Display & Sounds > HDMI CEC Device Control > turn OFF
   - **Vizio SmartCast:** Settings > System > CEC > turn OFF
2. Test if you get a picture with CEC turned off. If yes, leave CEC off — you'll just need to use each device's own remote instead of one universal remote
3. If your device has HDCP settings (most streaming sticks do):
   - **Fire Stick:** Settings > Display & Sounds > Display > HDCP > toggle it ON if it's OFF, or OFF if it's ON
   - **Roku:** Settings > System > Advanced system settings > HDCP > try disabling and re-enabling
4. Restart both the TV and the external device after changing these settings

![A TV settings menu showing HDMI-CEC / Anynet+ toggle being switched off](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People buy a new 4K streaming device and plug it into an older 1080p TV, then panic when they see "No Signal." The device is sending a 4K signal, but the TV can only understand 1080p — the TV literally can't decode the video. Before buying new equipment, always check whether your TV supports the resolution your device is outputting. If you're not sure what resolution your TV supports, look up your TV's model number on the manufacturer's website and check the "Native Resolution" or "Display Resolution" spec.

> **Common mistake #2:** HDMI-CEC conflicts are surprisingly common. If you have multiple devices plugged in — a soundbar, a game console, and a streaming stick — they can all fight over who gets to control the TV through HDMI-CEC. One device tries to take over, another device pushes back, and the result is a "No Signal" on one or all inputs. Disabling HDMI-CEC on all devices except the one you use most (usually your streaming stick or cable box) often clears the conflict instantly.

## Fix 5: Update TV Firmware and Factory Reset (Last Resort)

When hardware testing rules out the cable and external device, and settings changes don't help, the problem may be in the TV's internal software. Smart TVs run complex operating systems — and a corrupted system file from a failed update, a power surge, or years of accumulated junk can break the TV's ability to process incoming HDMI signals.

**Fix 5a — Update your TV's firmware**

1. Your TV needs an internet connection. If your TV isn't connected to WiFi, connect it now: press the **Menu** or **Settings** button on your remote, go to Network or WiFi settings, and connect
2. Navigate to the software update menu:
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > Support > Software Update > Update Now
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > System > Software Update > Check for Updates
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > System > About > System Update > Check for Update
   - **Roku TV:** Settings > System > System Update > Check Now
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > My Fire TV > About > Check for Updates
   - **Vizio SmartCast:** Settings > System > Check for Updates
3. If an update is found, select **Download and Install** or **Update Now**
4. **Do not touch anything** while the update installs. The screen may flash, go black, or show a progress bar. The TV may restart several times. This is normal — do NOT unplug the TV during an update
5. After the TV restarts, test your HDMI connection again

![A TV screen showing a firmware update progress bar with "Do not unplug your TV" warning text](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 5b — Update firmware via USB (if TV has no internet or screen issues)**

1. On a computer with internet, go to your TV manufacturer's support website:
   - Samsung: samsung.com/us/support/downloads
   - LG: lg.com/us/support/software-firmware-drivers
   - Sony: sony.com/electronics/support/downloads
   - TCL/Roku: support.tcl.com or support.roku.com
2. Search for your TV's **exact model number**. You'll find it on a sticker on the back of the TV, or in Settings > Support > About This TV. It looks like "QN65Q80CAFXZA" (Samsung), "OLED55C4PUA" (LG), or "XR-55A80L" (Sony)
3. Download the latest firmware file to your computer. It'll be a .zip file
4. Right-click the .zip file and select **Extract All**. Copy ALL extracted files and folders to the **root** of an empty USB flash drive (not inside any folder)
5. Safely eject the USB drive and plug it into a USB port on your TV
6. On the TV, go to the Software Update menu (same paths as Fix 5a) and select **Update Now**. The TV will find the firmware on the USB
7. Wait 10–30 minutes. The TV will restart when complete

**Fix 5c — Factory reset your TV (last resort before repair)**

> **Warning:** A factory reset erases EVERYTHING. All apps, WiFi passwords, picture settings, sound settings, and account logins are wiped. Your TV will restart as if it just came out of the box. You'll need to set it up from scratch. Write down your WiFi password and a list of apps you use before resetting.

1. Navigate to the factory reset menu:
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > General & Privacy > Reset > enter PIN (default is **0000**)
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > System > Reset to Initial Settings
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > System > About > Reset > Factory Reset
   - **Roku TV:** Settings > System > Advanced System Settings > Factory Reset
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > My Fire TV > Reset to Factory Defaults
   - **Vizio SmartCast:** Settings > Admin & Privacy > Reset TV to Factory Defaults
2. Read the warning screen carefully
3. Enter your PIN if asked. The default is **0000** for Samsung, LG, and most brands
4. Select **Reset** to confirm. The TV will shut down, show a progress bar for 5–15 minutes, then restart to the initial setup screen
5. Set up the TV as new — do NOT restore any backups or sign into cloud accounts during setup (corrupted settings from a backup can bring the problem right back)
6. After setup, test your HDMI connection

**If the TV screen is completely black and you can't see the menu:** Many TVs have a physical reset pinhole on the back panel — a tiny hole near the HDMI ports labeled "RESET." Straighten a paperclip, insert it gently, press and hold the button inside for 30–60 seconds. The TV will restart. Look up your exact model online to confirm the pinhole location.

![A paperclip being inserted into the reset pinhole on the back of a TV, with the word RESET printed next to the hole](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** After a factory reset, people immediately sign into their Samsung or LG account and restore their old settings from the cloud. If a corrupted setting file caused the "No Signal" problem, restoring that backup brings the corruption right back. Treat your TV like it's brand new — set it up fresh, skip all account logins and data syncing, and only connect the one device you need to test. If HDMI works after a clean setup, you know the old settings were the problem. You can then carefully add back your settings one at a time over the next few days to see which one breaks it.

> **Common mistake #2:** If your Samsung TV uses a **One Connect Box** — a separate box that all HDMI cables plug into, connected to the TV by a single thin cable — a "No Signal" message can be caused by a faulty One Connect Box or One Connect cable, not the TV itself. Before factory resetting, unplug the One Connect Box from power and disconnect it from the TV. Wait 60 seconds, reconnect everything firmly, and test. If the problem goes away temporarily and comes back, the One Connect Box or its cable needs replacement. Samsung often replaces these under warranty.

## Still Stuck?

- **All HDMI ports show "No Signal" but the TV's built-in apps (Netflix, YouTube, Smart Hub) work fine?** The TV screen itself is working, and the TV's internal computer is fine. The problem is isolated to the HDMI input board inside your TV. This is a hardware failure — the HDMI controller chip (the chip that processes all incoming HDMI signals) has died. On most TVs, this means replacing the main board. A repair shop can do this for $80–$200 depending on your TV model, or you can buy the board yourself from sites like ShopJimmy.com and follow a YouTube repair guide. If your TV is under warranty, contact the manufacturer — this is a covered repair.

- **No Signal only happens with one specific device, but other devices work on the same HDMI port?** The problem is that specific device, not the TV. Try factory resetting the device (see its manual). For streaming sticks older than 3–4 years, aging hardware may simply struggle to maintain a stable HDMI connection — consider upgrading to a newer model.

- **TV is more than 7–8 years old and this problem just started?** HDMI ports can wear out from years of plugging and unplugging. The gold-plated contacts inside the port can corrode, bend, or break. On older TVs, replacing a single HDMI port is rarely cost-effective. Instead, consider adding an **HDMI switch** — a small box that plugs into one working HDMI port and gives you 3–5 new ports. HDMI switches cost $10–$25 on Amazon and are a cheap way to extend the life of an older TV with failing ports.

- **Picture appears for a few seconds, then goes black with "No Signal"?** This pattern usually points to an **HDCP handshake failure** — the copy protection system between your TV and device is failing to authenticate. Try a different HDMI cable (HDCP sometimes fails on older cables). Also, unplug ALL other HDMI devices from the TV (even ones on different ports) — sometimes a device on HDMI 2 can interfere with the HDCP handshake on HDMI 1. Test with only one device plugged in at a time.

- **No Signal on HDMI but the same device works through AV/component cables?** This confirms the TV's HDMI board has a problem while the rest of the TV works. If your TV has component inputs (red/green/blue RCA jacks), you can use a cheap **HDMI-to-component converter box** ($20–$30 on Amazon) to connect HDMI devices through the component input, bypassing the broken HDMI ports entirely.

- **TV is under warranty?** Stop troubleshooting and use your warranty. Opening the TV or attempting internal repairs voids most manufacturer warranties. Check your warranty status on the manufacturer's website using your TV's model number and serial number (found on the sticker on the back of the TV). Most TVs come with a 1-year manufacturer warranty, and some credit cards extend that by an additional year.
