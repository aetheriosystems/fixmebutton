## Before You Start

You turn on your TV — and you hear voices. The news anchor is talking. The show's soundtrack plays perfectly. But your screen is completely black, as if the TV never turned on at all. Sound, no picture. It feels like the TV is half-dead, and your first thought might be that it's broken for good.

Here's the good news: if your TV is producing sound, the power supply and main computer are working. The screen is dark because the video signal is getting blocked, interrupted, or lost somewhere along the way. In most cases, the fix is simple and free — a loose HDMI cable, a wrong input setting, or a software glitch that a power cycle clears right up.

**What you'll need:**
- Your TV remote
- A flashlight (the one on your phone works fine)
- A spare HDMI cable (just in case)
- Access to the back of your TV (to check connections)

> **Work through the steps in order.** Each fix builds on the last. The first two fixes solve more than half of all black-screen-with-sound cases and take under two minutes each.

## Fix 1: Power Cycle Your TV the Right Way

Most people "turn off" their TV by tapping the power button on the remote — but that doesn't actually turn it off. Modern smart TVs go into **standby mode**, which keeps the internal computer running. If a software glitch is blocking the picture, that glitch stays alive in standby. You need a full **power cycle**, also called a cold boot, to flush the system completely.

**Fix 1 — The 60-Second Unplug Method (works on every TV brand)**

1. Turn on your TV so you can confirm it has sound but no picture
2. **Unplug** the TV's power cord from the wall outlet — do NOT just turn it off with the remote
3. While the TV is unplugged, find the **physical Power button** on the TV itself (it's usually on the bottom edge, side panel, or back of the TV — not on the remote)
4. Press and **hold** that physical Power button for **25–30 seconds** (set a timer on your phone)
5. Release the power button
6. Wait a full **60 seconds** total from the moment you unplugged (the extra time lets internal capacitors fully drain)
7. Plug the power cord back into the wall outlet — make sure it goes directly into the wall, not a power strip that might be turned off
8. Turn on the TV using the remote or the physical button
9. Check if the picture is back

![A hand unplugging a TV power cord from a wall outlet, with a phone timer counting down from 60 seconds visible next to it](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People unplug the TV, count to five, plug it back in, and call it done. Modern TVs have capacitors that hold a charge for 30 seconds or more. If you don't do the full 60-second wait AND hold the power button to drain the remaining charge, the TV's brain never actually resets. You changed the plug but not the problem. The power button hold is the secret — it forces the capacitors to release their stored energy so the system starts truly fresh.

> **Tip:** If your TV is wall-mounted and the power cord is hidden behind the wall or hard to reach, you can sometimes power-cycle by switching off the circuit breaker that controls that wall outlet. Label the breaker first so you know which one you're turning off, leave it off for 60 seconds, then flip it back on. This does the same thing as unplugging — just make sure no other important devices (like a fridge or computer) are on the same breaker.

## Fix 2: Check Your Input Source and HDMI Connections

If the power cycle didn't bring the picture back, the next most likely cause is a connection problem. Your TV might be on the wrong input, or the HDMI cable carrying the video might be loose, damaged, or plugged into the wrong port. HDMI is tricky — it can carry audio even when the video connection is weak, so you hear sound from a source but see nothing on screen.

**Fix 2a — Verify you're on the right input**

1. Press the **Input** or **Source** button on your TV remote (it might also be labeled as a rectangle with an arrow pointing into it)
2. A list of inputs appears on screen showing HDMI 1, HDMI 2, HDMI 3, etc. If you can see this menu, the TV screen itself is working — the problem is external
3. Cycle through each HDMI input one at a time, waiting 5 seconds on each one to see if a picture appears
4. If you're using a cable box, game console, or streaming stick, make sure that device is actually **turned on**. A powered-off device sends no video signal, so the TV shows a black screen even on the right input
5. On the TV, go to:
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > Connection > External Device Manager — make sure HDMI-CEC (Anynet+) is enabled
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > External Devices > HDMI Settings — turn on SIMPLINK (HDMI-CEC)
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > External Inputs > Bravia Sync Settings — enable Bravia Sync
   - **Roku TV:** Settings > System > Control other devices (CEC) — enable 1-touch play
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > Display & Sounds > HDMI CEC Device Control — turn it ON

![A TV screen showing the input/source selection menu with HDMI 1, HDMI 2, HDMI 3, and AV listed, highlighting the selected input](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 2b — Physically check every cable connection**

1. Go to the back of your TV and look at the HDMI ports. Find which HDMI cable is plugged in and which port it's in (the ports are labeled HDMI 1, HDMI 2, etc.)
2. **Unplug** the HDMI cable from the TV and **plug it back in firmly** — push until you feel it click or seat fully. A half-plugged HDMI cable can carry audio but fail to send video
3. Follow the HDMI cable to the other end (cable box, streaming stick, game console, or soundbar). Unplug it there too and plug it back in firmly
4. Repeat for every HDMI cable connected to your TV, one at a time
5. After re-seating all cables, turn your external device **off and back on** (power-cycle the cable box, streaming stick, or game console — not the TV)

![Two hands reconnecting an HDMI cable into the back of a TV, with the HDMI port labels visible](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 2c — Try a different HDMI port and a different HDMI cable**

1. Move the HDMI cable from the current port to a **different** HDMI port on the TV (for example, move from HDMI 1 to HDMI 2)
2. Press the Input button on your remote and select the new port (HDMI 2)
3. If you get a picture on the new port, the original HDMI port may be damaged. You can keep using the new port permanently — no repair needed unless you run out of ports
4. If switching ports doesn't help, try a **different HDMI cable** entirely. Borrow one from another device that you know works, swap it in, and test
5. **Samsung TVs only:** Some models have a built-in HDMI cable test. Go to Settings > Support > Device Care > Self Diagnosis > Signal Information > HDMI Cable Test > Start Test. If the test says the cable is bad, replace it

![A person swapping an HDMI cable from one port to another on the back of a TV](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People press the Input button, see "HDMI 1" on screen, and assume they're on the right input. But the label only tells you which port is selected — it doesn't tell you if anything is actually plugged into that port or if the device on the other end is turned on. A black screen with "HDMI 1" in the corner means the TV is waiting for a signal that isn't arriving. Before you troubleshoot the TV, make sure your cable box or streaming stick is actually powered on — check for a power light on the device itself.

> **Common mistake #2:** People keep buying expensive "high-speed" HDMI cables thinking they need a special version. For 99% of setups — streaming at 1080p or 4K — any standard HDMI cable works fine. A $6 cable carries the same video signal as a $60 cable. The only exception is if you're running HDMI cables longer than 25 feet, or trying to push 4K at 120Hz for gaming — in those rare cases, you need a certified Ultra High Speed HDMI cable.

## Fix 3: Test If the TV Screen Itself Is Working

Before going further, we need to answer one critical question: is the TV's screen physically broken, or is it just not receiving a video signal? A simple flashlight test answers this in 30 seconds.

**This test works because:** LED and LCD TVs have two layers. The **LCD panel** creates the image you see (think of it like a film negative — the picture is there, but you can't see it in the dark). Then **backlights** (rows of tiny LEDs behind the screen) shine light through the panel so you can actually see the picture. If the backlights fail, the LCD panel still works — the image is there, but there's no light to make it visible. Sound keeps working because audio doesn't depend on the backlights at all.

**Fix 3 — The Flashlight Test**

1. Turn your TV on and play something you know should have a moving picture — a live TV channel, a streaming app, or a YouTube video. You need the content to be changing so you can spot movement
2. Grab the brightest flashlight you have (your phone's flashlight works, but a standalone flashlight is better)
3. Turn off the room lights — the darker the room, the easier it is to see the results
4. Press the flashlight directly against the TV screen and shine it at a **steep angle** (almost parallel to the screen surface, not straight at it)
5. Slowly move the flashlight across the screen while looking closely at the lit area. Check in the corners, the center, and along the edges
6. **What to look for:** Faint, dark shapes or movement. You might see the outline of a person, text, or a logo — it'll be very dim, like a shadow
7. **If you see any faint image:** The LCD panel is working, and your backlights have failed. This is fixable, but it requires replacing the backlight LED strips inside the TV — a repair that costs $50–$150 at a repair shop, depending on your TV size and model. Skip to Fix 5 for your options
8. **If you see absolutely nothing — total darkness, no shapes at all:** The problem is likely with the TV's main board, power supply board, or T-Con board (the internal parts that process the video signal). Proceed to Fix 4

![A person holding a smartphone flashlight against a TV screen at an angle, with faint shadow-like shapes visible in the lit area](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People shine the flashlight straight at the screen from two feet away and see nothing, then declare the test failed. You MUST press the flashlight right up against the screen and angle it sideways — almost like you're trying to skip a stone across water. Light needs to enter the LCD panel from the edge to reveal the hidden image. If you're not sure you're doing it right, try in a completely dark room and move very slowly across the screen. The faint image looks like a dark silhouette — it takes a moment for your eyes to pick it up.

> **Tip:** If the flashlight test shows an image, try pressing the **Menu** or **Settings** button on your remote while shining the flashlight. If you can faintly see the menu text, you have double confirmation: the screen panel is fine, and the backlights are the problem. Take a photo of this with your phone (in the dark with the flashlight) — it'll help if you take the TV to a repair shop, because you can show them the panel is still good.

## Fix 4: Update Your TV Firmware or Perform a Factory Reset

If the flashlight test showed no image at all, the problem may be a software corruption in the TV's operating system. Smart TVs run complex software — Samsung uses Tizen, LG uses webOS, Sony uses Google TV, and Roku TVs use Roku OS. If a system file gets corrupted by a failed update, a power surge, or a full storage drive, the TV can lose its ability to process video while audio still works fine.

**Fix 4a — Check for and install a software update**

Since your screen is black, you'll need to navigate these steps by sound or by feel. If you can temporarily get a picture (for example, by wiggling the HDMI cable or after a successful power cycle), jump into the settings before the picture disappears again.

1. On a working screen (or by navigating from memory):
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > Support > Software Update > Update Now
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > System > Software Update > Check for Updates
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > System > About > System Update > Check for Update
   - **Roku TV:** Settings > System > System Update > Check Now
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > My Fire TV > About > Check for Updates
   - **Vizio SmartCast:** Settings > System > Check for Updates
2. If an update is found, select **Download and Install** or **Update Now**
3. **Do not touch anything** while the update installs. The screen may flash, go black, or show a progress bar. The TV may restart itself several times. This is normal — do NOT unplug the TV during an update
4. After the TV finishes and returns to the home screen, check if your picture is back
5. If the TV can't get online to download updates (no WiFi or the screen is too dark to navigate), use the USB firmware method from Fix 4b

**Fix 4b — Update firmware using a USB drive (works on all TVs when screen is black)**

1. On a computer or phone with internet access, go to your TV manufacturer's support website:
   - Samsung: samsung.com/us/support/downloads
   - LG: lg.com/us/support/software-firmware-drivers
   - Sony: sony.com/electronics/support/downloads
   - TCL/Roku: support.tcl.com or support.roku.com
2. Search for your TV's **exact model number**. You'll find it on a sticker on the back of the TV, on the original box, or in the TV's Settings > About/Support > System Information menu. It looks like "QN65Q80CAFXZA" (Samsung), "OLED55C4PUA" (LG), or "XR-55A80L" (Sony)
3. Download the latest firmware file to your computer. It'll typically be a .zip file
4. Right-click the .zip file and select **Extract All**. Copy ALL extracted files and folders to the **root** of an empty USB flash drive (do NOT put them inside any folder on the USB)
5. Safely eject the USB drive from your computer, then plug it into a USB port on the back or side of your TV
6. On your TV, navigate to the Software Update menu (same paths as Fix 4a) and select **Update Now**. The TV will detect the firmware on the USB and begin installing
7. Wait for the update to complete — this can take 10–30 minutes. The TV will restart itself when done

![A USB flash drive being inserted into the USB port on the back of a TV, with a laptop screen showing a firmware download page in the background](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 4c — Factory reset the TV (last resort before hardware repair)**

> **Warning:** A factory reset erases EVERYTHING on your TV. All apps are removed. All picture, sound, and WiFi settings are wiped. Your TV will restart as if it just came out of the box. You will need to set it up from scratch: choose language, connect to WiFi, sign into accounts, and re-download your apps. Before resetting, write down or photograph your important settings (picture mode, WiFi password, app list) so you can set them up again.

If you can see the screen at all (even temporarily), navigate to the reset option. If the screen is completely black, you may need to use a factory reset button or pinhole on the TV itself — see the "If you can't see the menu" section below.

1. Navigate to your TV's factory reset menu:
   - **Samsung:** Settings > All Settings > General & Privacy > Reset > enter PIN (default is **0000**)
   - **LG:** Settings > All Settings > General > System > Reset to Initial Settings
   - **Sony/Google TV:** Settings > System > About > Reset > Factory Reset
   - **Roku TV:** Settings > System > Advanced System Settings > Factory Reset
   - **Fire TV:** Settings > My Fire TV > Reset to Factory Defaults
   - **Vizio SmartCast:** Settings > Admin & Privacy > Reset TV to Factory Defaults
2. Read the warning screen carefully — it lists everything that will be erased
3. You may be asked for a PIN. The default is **0000** for Samsung, LG, and most brands. If you changed your PIN and can't remember it, try 0000 first
4. Select **Reset** or **Factory Reset** to confirm
5. The TV will shut down, show a progress bar or spinning icon for 5–15 minutes, then restart to the initial setup screen
6. Follow the on-screen setup, then test if the picture is back

**If you can't see the menu (screen is completely black):** Many TVs have a physical reset pinhole on the back panel — a tiny hole labeled "RESET." It's usually near the HDMI ports or the power cord. Straighten a paperclip, insert it gently into the hole, and press and hold the button inside for **30–60 seconds**. The TV will restart. Look up your exact model on the manufacturer's website to confirm the reset pinhole location.

![A paperclip being inserted into the reset pinhole on the back of a TV, with the RESET label visible](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** After a factory reset, people immediately restore their settings from a cloud backup or sign into their Samsung/LG account and sync their old settings. If corrupted settings caused the black screen, restoring the backup brings the corruption right back. Set everything up as a brand-new TV — skip all the data tracking and advertising options during setup, and don't restore any backups. Yes, it takes more time, but it's the only way to be sure you've actually wiped the problem.

> **Common mistake #2:** If your TV uses a **One Connect Box** (Samsung) — a separate box that all cables plug into, connected to the TV by a single thin cable — a black screen can be caused by a faulty One Connect Box or One Connect cable. Before factory resetting the TV, disconnect the One Connect Box from power (unplug its power cord) and check if ANYTHING appears on screen. If a message or picture briefly shows when you disconnect the box, the One Connect Box needs replacement, not the whole TV. Samsung will usually send a replacement box under warranty.

## Fix 5: Isolate the Faulty Component (Multi-Device Testing)

If the flashlight test was negative and a factory reset didn't help, the problem is almost certainly hardware — either the TV, the HDMI cable, or the external device you're trying to watch. This systematic swap-test tells you which piece is broken so you don't waste money replacing the wrong thing.

**Fix 5 — Test with different cables, ports, and devices**

1. **Swap the HDMI cable:** Borrow an HDMI cable from another working setup in your house (like a game console or a different TV). Replace the cable connecting your external device to the TV. Test. If picture appears, the old cable was bad — throw it out and use the new one
2. **Swap the external device:** Unplug your cable box or streaming stick and connect a different device to the same TV port using the same cable — for example, connect a laptop, a game console, or a known-working streaming stick to that HDMI port. If the new device shows a picture, your TV and cable are fine — the problem is your original external device
3. **Move the external device to a different TV:** Take your cable box or streaming stick to another TV in your house. Plug it in using the same HDMI cable. If it shows a picture on the other TV, your original TV is the problem
4. **Try the TV's built-in apps:** If your TV is a smart TV, press the **Home** or **Smart Hub** button on the remote. Do the built-in apps (like Netflix, YouTube, or the TV's settings menu) show a picture? If yes, the TV screen works fine, and the problem is with whatever is plugged into the HDMI port
5. **Try a different type of connection:** If your external device has outputs other than HDMI (like component, composite, or optical), try connecting with those instead. A temporary picture through a different connection type points to a failing HDMI board inside the TV

**Results flowchart:**
- **Different cable works** → old cable is bad. Replace it
- **Different device works on same TV port** → your original external device is broken
- **Original device works on different TV** → your original TV has a hardware problem
- **TV's built-in apps show picture** → the HDMI board in the TV is faulty
- **Nothing works on any port with any cable** → the TV's main board or power supply has failed

![A diagram showing a TV connected to three different external devices — a cable box, a game console, and a streaming stick — with arrows showing which combinations to test](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People test one thing, see it's not the problem, and stop. You need to test ALL three — cable, device, and TV port — because sometimes two things are broken at once (a bad HDMI cable AND a loose port). The only way to be sure which piece is the culprit is to isolate each component by swapping in known-working replacements. This is how professional technicians diagnose the problem — it's called "swap-til-you-drop" testing, and it works every time.

> **Tip:** If you don't have spare equipment to swap in, ask a friend or neighbor if you can borrow their streaming stick or game console for 10 minutes. Or, use a laptop with an HDMI output — almost every laptop made in the last 10 years has one. Connect the laptop to your TV, and if the laptop's desktop appears on screen, your TV is working. The test takes 30 seconds and saves you from buying a new TV when all you needed was a $10 HDMI cable.

## Still Stuck?

- **Flashlight test showed a faint image (backlight failure confirmed)?** You have three options. (1) Have the backlights professionally replaced at a TV repair shop — expect to pay $50–$150 depending on your TV size. (2) If you're handy, buy replacement LED strips for your exact TV model from sites like ShopJimmy.com or eBay and follow a YouTube repair guide. (3) If the TV is more than 5 years old, the repair cost may not be worth it — consider replacing the TV, as modern TVs have gotten much cheaper. A new 55-inch 4K TV can cost less than $300.

- **TV is under warranty?** Stop troubleshooting and use your warranty. Opening the TV or attempting internal repairs voids most manufacturer warranties. Check your TV's warranty status on the manufacturer's website (you'll need the model number and serial number from the sticker on the back). Most TVs come with a 1-year manufacturer warranty, and some credit cards extend that by an additional year.

- **Picture comes and goes — shows for an hour, then goes black again?** This strongly points to overheating. When the TV's internal components get too hot, the main board or power supply can temporarily stop sending video while audio continues. Check that the TV's ventilation slots (on the back and sides) aren't blocked by dust, walls, or curtains. Unplug the TV, use a vacuum with a brush attachment to clean the vents, and make sure there's at least 4 inches of space behind the TV for airflow. If the problem happens more in summer or after the TV has been on for a while, overheating is almost certainly the cause.

- **TV screen is black but you see a faint red standby light blinking in a pattern?** Many TVs use blinking light codes to tell you what's wrong. For example, Samsung TVs: 2 blinks means a power supply issue, 5 blinks means a bad main board, and continuous blinking means the screen panel itself has failed. Search "[your TV brand] blinking light error codes" on your phone to decode the pattern. This can tell you exactly which part needs to be replaced before you spend any money.

- **TV is older than 7–8 years and none of these fixes work?** LED backlights have a typical lifespan of 50,000–100,000 hours (about 6–11 years of average use). If your TV is approaching this age and the picture has been getting dimmer over the past year before finally going black, the backlights have reached the end of their natural life. Repairs are possible but often not cost-effective on older models. Instead of buying a new TV, consider getting a streaming projector — you can get a decent 1080p projector for under $100 that projects onto any wall and gives you a bigger picture than most TVs.

- **Sound only works through a soundbar or external speakers, not the TV's own speakers?** Try disconnecting the soundbar or external audio system completely and testing with the TV's built-in speakers. If the picture comes back when the soundbar is unplugged, there's an HDMI-CEC or ARC (Audio Return Channel) conflict between the TV and the soundbar. Go into the TV's sound settings and disable HDMI-CEC or change the audio output to "TV Speaker" instead of "External Speaker" or "HDMI ARC."
