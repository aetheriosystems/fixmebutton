## Before You Start

You plug your phone in and… nothing. No charging symbol, no vibration, no battery percentage ticking up. Before you panic or rush to a repair shop, know this: most "won't charge" problems are caused by something simple — lint in the charging port, a bad cable, or a software glitch. Nine times out of ten, you can fix it yourself in under 15 minutes.

This guide works for iPhones (Lightning and USB-C), Samsung Galaxy, Google Pixel, and pretty much every other Android phone.

**What you'll need:**
- Your phone and its usual charger
- A spare charging cable and charger brick (if you have one)
- A wooden toothpick or plastic floss pick (NOT metal — see Step 1)
- A flashlight (your phone's built-in flashlight won't work if it's dead — use another phone or a lamp)

> **Warning:** Do NOT use metal objects (paper clips, safety pins, SIM tray tools) to clean your charging port. Metal can scratch the delicate pins inside and cause permanent damage. Wood or plastic only.

## Step 1: Clean Out Your Charging Port (Most Likely Fix)

This is the #1 reason phones stop charging. Over weeks and months, pocket lint, dust, and debris get packed into the charging port. Every time you plug in your charger, you push that gunk deeper. Eventually, the charger can't make a solid connection anymore — and your phone stops charging.

**iPhone with Lightning port (iPhone 14 and older):**

1. Shine a flashlight into the charging port and look for lint or dirt (it often looks like a fuzzy gray layer at the bottom)
2. Take a **wooden toothpick** or a **plastic dental floss pick** (the kind with a pointed end)
3. Gently insert the tip into the port, scraping along the bottom edge — NOT the sides where the pins are
4. Scoop outward, pulling lint toward you. Don't push deeper in
5. Repeat 5-10 times until no more lint comes out
6. Plug in your charger to test

**Android USB-C phones (Samsung Galaxy, Google Pixel, most modern Androids):**

1. Shine a flashlight into the USB-C port
2. Look for pocket lint packed around the small tongue-shaped piece in the center of the port
3. Use a **wooden toothpick** — or cut a plastic straw at a sharp angle to make a scoop
4. Gently scrape around the outside of the center tongue, pulling debris outward
5. Be extra careful not to wiggle or bend the center tongue — it's fragile
6. Test with your charger

![Close-up of someone cleaning lint out of a charging port with a wooden toothpick](/images/guides/smartphones/cleaning-charging-port.jpg)

> **Tip:** After cleaning, your charger should click firmly into place instead of feeling loose or wobbly. A solid click means the port is clean and the connection is good.

**Common mistake:** Using a SIM tray ejector tool, paperclip, or sewing needle. Metal on metal inside a charging port can scratch the gold-plated contact pins or even short-circuit them. Wood and plastic are safe.

## Step 2: Try a Different Cable and Charger Brick

Charging cables take a beating — they get bent, twisted, stepped on, and stuffed into bags. They fail quietly. Same goes for the charger brick (the wall adapter).

1. **Try a different cable** — borrow one from a family member if you need to. Use one you know works. Plug your phone in and wait 30 seconds.
2. **Try a different charger brick** — swap the wall adapter for another one. Some cheap chargers stop working without any visible sign.
3. **Try a different wall outlet** — it sounds silly, but sometimes the outlet is the problem. Test the outlet by plugging in a lamp or another device.
4. **Try wireless charging** — if your phone supports wireless charging (iPhone 8 and newer, most Samsung Galaxy and newer Androids), place it on a wireless pad. If it charges wirelessly but not with a cable, the charging port is definitely the problem.

![Different USB cables and charger bricks lined up on a desk](/images/guides/smartphones/different-chargers.jpg)

> **Tip:** Not all cables are equal. Some cheap cables can only carry data (not power), and some old cables only support slow charging. If your phone charges with one cable but not another, throw the bad one away — frayed or damaged cables can be a fire hazard.

**Common mistake:** Assuming a cable that "looks fine" is fine. Cables often break internally — the wires inside snap while the rubber coating looks perfect. If your phone charges only when the cable is bent at a certain angle, the cable is broken inside.

## Step 3: Force Restart Your Phone

Sometimes your phone's software gets stuck — the screen is off, it looks dead, but it's actually just frozen. A force restart (also called a hard reset) can wake it up. This is safe — it won't erase any of your data.

**iPhone with Face ID (iPhone X and newer):**

1. Quickly press and release the **volume up** button
2. Quickly press and release the **volume down** button
3. Press and **hold the right side button** until the Apple logo appears (this can take 10-15 seconds — keep holding!)
4. Release when you see the Apple logo

**iPhone with Home button (iPhone SE, iPhone 8 and older):**

1. Press and **hold both the top (or side) button AND the home button** at the same time
2. Keep holding — don't let go when the screen goes black
3. Release only when the Apple logo appears

**Most Android phones (Google Pixel, OnePlus, Motorola):**

1. Press and **hold the power button and the volume down button** at the same time
2. Keep holding for 10-20 seconds
3. Release when the screen lights up or you feel a vibration

**Samsung Galaxy phones:**

1. Press and **hold the power button and the volume down button** at the same time
2. Keep holding for 10-15 seconds
3. Release when you see the Samsung logo

![Hands holding a phone performing a force restart — fingers on the correct button combination](/images/guides/smartphones/force-restart-phone.jpg)

> **Warning:** If your phone was completely dead and you're now trying to turn it on after the force restart, leave it plugged in for at least 30 minutes first. A battery that's been at 0% for a while needs time before it can power the screen on.

## Step 4: Check for Software Charging Settings

Modern phones have battery management features that can sometimes interfere with normal charging — especially overnight.

**iPhone — Check these settings:**

1. Go to **Settings → Battery → Battery Health & Charging**
2. Look at **Maximum Capacity** — if it's below 80%, your battery is worn down and may need replacement
3. Check **Optimized Battery Charging** — this slows charging past 80% to protect battery health. It's normally fine, but if your phone won't charge past 80% and you need a full charge, you can temporarily turn it off
4. Check **Clean Energy Charging** (iPhones running iOS 16.1+) — this delays charging until cleaner energy is available. If your phone only charges at certain times, turn this off: **Settings → Battery → Battery Health & Charging → Clean Energy Charging → Off**

**Android — Check these settings:**

1. Go to **Settings → Battery** (or **Settings → Device Care → Battery** on Samsung)
2. Look for **Battery Protection**, **Protect battery**, or **Optimized charging** — these limit charging to 85% to extend battery life
3. If your phone stops at 85% and you need 100%, you can temporarily turn this off
4. On Samsung: **Settings → Device Care → Battery → Charging settings → Protect battery → Off**
5. On Google Pixel: **Settings → Battery → Charging optimization → turn off**

![Phone Settings screen showing Battery Health and Optimized Charging toggle](/images/guides/smartphones/battery-settings-software.jpg)

> **Tip:** Leaving battery protection features on is actually good for your phone's long-term health. Only turn them off temporarily when you know you'll need a full 100% charge (like before a long trip). Turn them back on afterward.

## Step 5: When to Get Professional Help

If you've tried all four steps above and your phone still won't charge, the problem is likely a hardware issue that needs a repair shop. Here's what to look for:

**Signs you need a repair shop:**
- Your phone charges wirelessly but never with a cable (damaged charging port)
- Your phone only works when plugged in and dies immediately when unplugged (dead battery)
- The charging port feels loose, wobbly, or you see bent or missing pins inside
- Your phone gets extremely hot while charging — hot enough that it's uncomfortable to hold
- You see a message saying "Liquid detected in Lightning connector" (iPhone) — and it doesn't go away after a day of drying out
- Your phone won't charge after being dropped in water or exposed to heavy rain

**What it'll cost (rough estimates, 2026):**
- Charging port repair: $50–$100 at a local repair shop ($150+ at Apple)
- Battery replacement: $60–$100 (Apple: $89–$99 depending on model)
- If under warranty: AppleCare+ or Samsung Care+ covers battery and charging port issues for a small deductible

![Phone being repaired at a repair shop — technician inspecting the charging port with a magnifying glass](/images/guides/smartphones/repair-shop-port.jpg)

> **Warning:** Do NOT attempt to replace a phone battery yourself unless you have proper tools and experience. Phone batteries are glued in and can catch fire or explode if punctured. This is one fix where paying a professional is worth every penny.

## Still Stuck?

- **Liquid detection warning won't go away?** iPhone users: the "Liquid detected in Lightning connector" warning can take 24+ hours to clear. Set your phone upright in a dry, ventilated spot (NOT rice — rice dust can get into the port). Android users: most Androids don't show a warning, but the phone may refuse to charge while the USB-C port is wet. Wait a few hours and try again.

- **Phone charges but extremely slowly?** Make sure you're using the charger that came with your phone (or an equally powerful one). Low-power chargers (like old iPhone 5W cubes or cheap gas-station chargers) can take 5-6 hours to charge a modern phone. Look for a charger labeled "PD" (Power Delivery) or "Quick Charge" for fast charging.

- **Phone is completely dead and won't even show the charging symbol?** Leave it plugged into a known-good charger for at least 1 hour without touching it. A phone that's been at 0% for days or weeks needs a long trickle charge before the screen can turn on. If nothing happens after 1 hour, the battery may be fully dead and need replacement.

- **Charging port is loose even after cleaning?** The port itself may be physically worn out from years of plugging and unplugging. If your phone supports wireless charging, you can switch to that full-time and avoid the port entirely. Otherwise, a repair shop can replace the charging port.

- **Phone says it's charging but percentage never goes up?** This usually means your phone is using more power than the charger is supplying. Close all apps (especially games, GPS navigation, and video streaming), turn on Airplane Mode, lower screen brightness to minimum, and try again. If it still won't gain charge, your charger is too weak or your battery is failing.
