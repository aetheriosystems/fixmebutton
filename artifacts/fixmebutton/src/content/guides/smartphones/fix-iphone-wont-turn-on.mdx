## Before You Start

You press the side button. Nothing. You plug it in. Nothing. You hold the button for 10 seconds. Still nothing — just a black screen staring back at you. Or maybe it's worse: the Apple logo appears, disappears, appears again, and your iPhone is stuck in an endless loop of trying and failing to start up.

This is scary. Your photos, messages, contacts, apps — they're all on that phone. But take a deep breath. In most cases, your iPhone isn't dead. It's just stuck, and you can get it unstuck without losing anything.

This guide walks you through five fixes, from the simplest (which works for most people) to the most thorough (which works for almost everyone). Start at Fix 1 and work your way through. Most people solve this by Fix 1 or Fix 3.

**What you'll need:**
- Your iPhone
- A Lightning or USB-C charging cable (the one that came with your iPhone is best)
- A wall charger (the little white brick — NOT a computer USB port for the first attempt)
- A computer (Mac or Windows PC) with the latest iTunes installed — only needed for Fix 3 and beyond
- About 20 minutes

> **Work through the fixes in order.** Each fix is more powerful than the last — but also more involved. The first two take under two minutes and fix the problem for the majority of people. Don't jump straight to Recovery Mode.

> **Before you start, try this quick check:** Is your iPhone screen completely black but you can feel it vibrate when you flip the silent switch (the little switch above the volume buttons)? If yes, your iPhone IS on — the screen is just not working. Skip to Fix 2 and make sure it's charged, but know that a completely black screen with a working phone behind it usually means a hardware problem with the display. You'll likely need a repair shop.

## Step 1: Force Restart Your iPhone

A force restart is different from a normal restart. When you normally turn your iPhone off (by holding the side button and sliding to power off), the phone shuts down gracefully — closing apps, saving data, and cleaning up. A force restart is more like pulling the plug and plugging it back in. It cuts power to the processor and forces a fresh boot from scratch.

Think of it like this: a normal restart is like putting your computer to sleep and waking it up. A force restart is like holding down the power button until it shuts off completely, then turning it back on. It's completely safe and it clears out the low-level software glitches that cause black screens and boot loops.

**For iPhone 8, iPhone X, iPhone 11, iPhone 12, iPhone 13, iPhone 14, iPhone 15, iPhone 16, and iPhone SE (2nd & 3rd gen):**

1. Press and **quickly release** the **Volume Up** button (on the left side of the phone)
2. Press and **quickly release** the **Volume Down** button (right below Volume Up)
3. Press and **hold** the **Side button** (on the right side of the phone — the one you use to lock the screen)
4. **Keep holding** the Side button even when you see the "slide to power off" slider appear on screen. DO NOT slide it — just ignore it and keep holding
5. Keep holding until the screen goes completely black and the **Apple logo** appears. This can take anywhere from 10 to 30 seconds — don't let go early
6. Once you see the Apple logo, release the Side button and let the iPhone boot up normally (this takes about 30-60 seconds)

> **The timing is important!** Step 3 must happen RIGHT after Step 2. Not half a second later — immediately. Press Up, release. Press Down, release. Then HOLD the Side button. If you pause between steps, the force restart doesn't trigger and you'll just end up taking a screenshot or calling Emergency SOS.

**For iPhone 7 and iPhone 7 Plus:**

1. Press and **hold** both the **Side button** (on the right) AND the **Volume Down button** (on the left) at the same time
2. Keep holding both buttons — ignore the "slide to power off" slider if it appears
3. When the Apple logo appears on the screen, release both buttons
4. Let the iPhone boot up

**For iPhone 6s, iPhone 6s Plus, iPhone SE (1st gen), and older models:**

1. Press and **hold** both the **Home button** (the round button on the front) AND the **Side/Top button** (on the right or top, depending on the model) at the same time
2. Keep holding until the screen goes black and the Apple logo appears
3. Release both buttons and let it boot up

![Close-up of hands performing the force restart sequence: pressing Volume Up, then Volume Down, then holding the Side button](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People press the Volume Up and Volume Down buttons at the same time, or they hold them down instead of quickly releasing. The correct sequence for modern iPhones is: CLICK and release Volume Up, CLICK and release Volume Down, then HOLD the Side button. It's a three-step sequence, not a simultaneous press. Also, many people let go of the Side button the moment they see the "slide to power off" slider — you must KEEP HOLDING through the slider until the Apple logo appears. If the slider appears and you keep holding, the screen will go black and the logo will appear — that's the force restart working.

> **Tip:** If your iPhone restarts but gets stuck on the Apple logo again, that's actually PROGRESS. It means the device CAN turn on, but the operating system is crashing during startup. This tells you the problem is software, not a dead battery or broken hardware. Move on to Fix 3.

## Step 2: Charge Your iPhone (and Check the Cable, Charger, and Port)

An iPhone with a completely drained battery won't turn on even for a force restart — there's literally no power to start the processor. And sometimes the problem isn't the phone at all — it's the cable, the charger, or gunk in the charging port that's preventing power from getting through.

**Fix 1 — Plug it in and wait**

1. Plug your iPhone into a **wall outlet** using the original Apple cable and charger (or a good-quality replacement)
2. Do NOT use a computer USB port — computer USB ports deliver much less power than a wall charger, and a deeply drained iPhone might not get enough juice from a computer to wake up
3. Do NOT use a wireless charger for this — wireless charging is slower, and if the phone is completely dead, it may not detect the wireless pad at all. Use a cable
4. Leave the iPhone plugged in and **do not touch it for at least 30 minutes**. Don't try to turn it on, don't unplug it, don't press buttons — just let it sit and absorb power
5. If the battery was completely dead, a lightning bolt icon and a red battery bar should appear on the screen within 5-15 minutes. If it does, the phone is charging — keep waiting until it has enough power to turn on (usually 15-30 minutes)
6. After 30 minutes, try turning it on by holding the Side button. If it boots up, great — but keep it plugged in until it charges to at least 50%

**Fix 2 — Try a different cable and charger**

1. Swap out the **charging cable** first — cables wear out faster than chargers. Use a cable that you KNOW works (try it on another device, or borrow one from a family member)
2. If a different cable doesn't work, swap out the **wall charger brick** next
3. Also try a **different wall outlet** — outlets can go bad, especially in older homes or if a circuit breaker has tripped
4. If nothing works with any combination of cables and chargers, the problem is in the phone — not the accessories. Move on to Fix 3

**Fix 3 — Clean the charging port**

Over months of use, pocket lint, dust, and debris get packed into the charging port at the bottom of your iPhone. Every time you plug in a cable, it compresses the gunk a little more. Eventually, the cable can't make a solid connection — it looks like it's plugged in all the way, but it's actually sitting on top of a layer of compacted lint.

1. Grab a **flashlight** and look into the charging port at the bottom of your iPhone
2. If you see dust, lint, or debris, grab a **wooden toothpick** (NOT a paperclip, NOT a SIM tool, NOT anything metal — metal can short the electrical contacts and permanently damage your iPhone)
3. Gently scrape along the bottom edge of the port, pulling lint UP and OUT. Go around all four sides. You'll be surprised how much comes out
4. Be gentle — you're scraping the flat bottom surface, not jamming into the thin connector pins in the middle
5. Blow gently into the port (or use compressed air from a can if you have it) to remove loose debris
6. Plug the cable in. It should now click into place firmly instead of feeling loose or spongy. If it doesn't click in all the way, there's still gunk in there — keep cleaning
7. Once the cable seats properly, plug it into the charger and wait for the charging indicator

![A close-up macro shot of an iPhone charging port with a wooden toothpick gently removing compacted pocket lint](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People use metal objects to clean their charging port — paperclips, SIM card ejector tools, sewing needles, even knives. This is incredibly dangerous. The charging port has live electrical contacts, and touching them with metal can short them out, causing permanent damage. Even if you don't short-circuit the phone immediately, metal tools can scratch the gold-plated contact pins, leading to unreliable charging that gets worse over time. Always use a wooden toothpick, a plastic dental pick, or compressed air. Also, people sometimes look at the port, see a little dust, and think "that couldn't be the problem." A layer of lint just half a millimeter thick is enough to prevent the cable from connecting — it doesn't take much.

> **Warning:** If your iPhone got wet (dropped in water, toilet, bathtub, pool, or exposed to heavy rain), DO NOT plug it in to charge. Even if the phone appears dry on the outside, water inside the charging port or internal components can cause a short circuit when electricity flows through it. If your iPhone has been exposed to liquid, skip this entire step and go straight to the "Still Stuck?" section at the end — specifically the part about liquid damage. Plugging in a wet iPhone can destroy it permanently.

## Step 3: Update iOS Using Recovery Mode (Keeps Your Data)

If a force restart didn't work and charging isn't the issue, the iPhone's operating system is likely corrupted. This can happen after a failed iOS update, a bad app install, a backup that went wrong, or a jailbreak attempt. The good news: you can reinstall iOS WITHOUT losing your data using Recovery Mode.

Recovery Mode is a special startup state built into every iPhone. When you put your iPhone into Recovery Mode and connect it to a computer, iTunes (or Finder on newer Macs) can download a fresh copy of iOS and install it over the existing system — like putting a new engine in a car without touching the seats, radio, or anything else inside. Your photos, messages, apps, and settings stay intact.

**Before you start — you need a computer:**

- **Mac:** Make sure you have the latest version of macOS. Recovery Mode uses Finder on macOS Catalina (10.15) and newer. On older Macs, it uses iTunes.
- **Windows PC:** Download and install the latest version of iTunes from [apple.com/itunes](https://www.apple.com/itunes). Do NOT use the Microsoft Store version — it sometimes doesn't recognize iPhones in Recovery Mode. Go directly to Apple's website.
- A USB cable to connect your iPhone to the computer. Preferably the original Apple cable.

**Fix 1 — Enter Recovery Mode**

The button sequence depends on your iPhone model:

**iPhone 8, iPhone X, iPhone 11, iPhone 12, iPhone 13, iPhone 14, iPhone 15, iPhone 16, iPhone SE (2nd & 3rd gen):**

1. Connect your iPhone to the computer with a USB cable
2. Quick-press and release **Volume Up**
3. Quick-press and release **Volume Down**
4. Press and **hold** the **Side button** — keep holding it, don't let go when the Apple logo appears
5. Keep holding through the Apple logo until you see a screen with a **computer icon and a Lightning cable pointing to it**. This is the Recovery Mode screen
6. Release the Side button when you see the Recovery Mode screen

**iPhone 7 and iPhone 7 Plus:**

1. Connect your iPhone to the computer
2. Press and **hold** both the **Side button** AND the **Volume Down button** at the same time
3. Keep holding through the Apple logo until the Recovery Mode screen (computer icon) appears
4. Release both buttons

**iPhone 6s and older, iPhone SE (1st gen):**

1. Connect your iPhone to the computer
2. Press and **hold** both the **Home button** AND the **Side/Top button** at the same time
3. Keep holding through the Apple logo until the Recovery Mode screen appears
4. Release both buttons

**Fix 2 — Update (not Restore) your iPhone**

1. On your computer, a window will pop up saying there's a problem with your iPhone and giving you two options: **Update** and **Restore**
2. Click **Update** (NOT Restore — Restore erases everything)
3. iTunes or Finder will download the latest version of iOS. This can take anywhere from 10 to 30 minutes depending on your internet speed — the iOS file is about 6-8 GB
4. If the download takes more than 15 minutes, your iPhone may exit Recovery Mode automatically (the screen goes black and the phone tries to boot normally). This is normal — just repeat the button sequence to put it back into Recovery Mode, and the download will resume where it left off
5. Once the download finishes, your computer will install iOS onto your iPhone. The iPhone screen will show an Apple logo with a progress bar underneath. DO NOT unplug the cable during this process
6. When the installation is complete, your iPhone will restart. It should boot up normally and take you to your lock screen with all your data intact
7. You may need to enter your Apple ID password after the restart — this is normal and part of Apple's security

![iTunes or Finder window showing an iPhone in Recovery Mode with the Update button highlighted and Restore button visible but not selected](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People see the Recovery Mode screen (the computer icon with a cable) and panic, thinking their iPhone is "dead" or in "DFU mode." Recovery Mode is a GOOD sign — it means your iPhone's hardware is working and it can still communicate with a computer. Also, some people click **Restore** instead of **Update** because they don't understand the difference. Update = reinstall iOS, keep your data. Restore = wipe everything, start from scratch. ALWAYS try Update first — you can always do a Restore later if the Update fails. And a critical mistake: unplugging the iPhone during the iOS installation because you think it's "stuck" or "taking too long." The progress bar often sits at the same spot for several minutes — this is normal. Unplugging during installation can corrupt the iOS and create a bigger problem.

> **Tip:** If you see an error message during the update, write down the exact error number (like "error 4013," "error 9," "error 14," or "error 53"). Each error code has a specific meaning. Google the error number along with "iPhone" to find the exact fix. Common ones: error 4013 usually means a hardware problem (often the charging port or battery connector), error 9 often means a bad USB cable or port, and error 14 often means a corrupted firmware file that requires a fresh download.

## Step 4: Restore Your iPhone Using Recovery Mode (Erases Data)

If the Update option in Step 3 didn't work — you tried it and got an error, or the iPhone went right back to the Apple logo loop after the update finished — the operating system damage is too deep for an in-place repair. You need to do a full Restore. This completely wipes your iPhone and installs a clean copy of iOS.

**⚠️ THIS ERASES EVERYTHING.** Your photos, messages, apps, contacts, notes, settings — everything. The phone comes back as if it just came out of the box.

**Before you start:**

Check if you have a backup. If you regularly back up to iCloud or your computer, you'll be able to get your stuff back after the restore.

- **Check for iCloud backup:** On another device, go to **Settings > [your name] > iCloud > iCloud Backup**. Look for your iPhone's last backup date. If it was recent (within the last few days or weeks), you can restore from iCloud after wiping the phone.
- **Check for computer backup:** If you've ever plugged your iPhone into your computer and let iTunes/Finder back it up, the backup is on that computer. You'll be given the option to "Restore from this backup" after the wipe.

If you have NO backup, you will lose your data permanently. The photos, messages, and everything else will be gone forever. Before proceeding, see the "Still Stuck?" section at the end for alternatives.

**Fix 1 — Perform the Restore**

1. Put your iPhone into Recovery Mode (same button sequence as Step 3)
2. Connect it to your computer
3. When the iTunes or Finder window pops up with the Update and Restore options, this time click **Restore**
4. A warning will appear telling you this erases all data. Click **Restore and Update** to confirm
5. Your computer will download the latest iOS (if it hasn't already from Step 3) and install a completely fresh copy onto your iPhone
6. The iPhone screen will show an Apple logo with a progress bar. This process takes 10-30 minutes. Do NOT unplug during this time
7. When it finishes, the iPhone will restart and show the **"Hello"** setup screen — the same screen you saw when you first got the phone
8. Follow the on-screen setup steps. When you reach the **Apps & Data** screen, choose **Restore from iCloud Backup** or **Restore from Mac or PC** to get your data back
9. The restore from backup can take a while (especially from iCloud) — leave your iPhone connected to WiFi and plugged into power until it finishes

![The iPhone setup screen displaying "Apps & Data" with options: Restore from iCloud Backup, Restore from Mac or PC, and Don't Transfer Apps & Data](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People do a Restore but then, during the setup process, accidentally select **"Don't Transfer Apps & Data"** instead of restoring from a backup. This sets the phone up as brand new without any of your old data. You CAN still restore from backup after this — go to **Settings > General > Transfer or Reset iPhone > Erase All Content and Settings** and start the setup again. But this erases the phone a second time and takes another 30+ minutes. Pay attention during setup and choose the right option. Also: if you restore from an iCloud backup, your phone needs to stay connected to WiFi the entire time. If your WiFi drops during the iCloud restore, the process stops and you'll need to erase and start over.

> **Warning:** If you use an Apple Watch paired to your iPhone and you do a Restore without unpairing the watch first, the watch may still think it's paired to the old (now-wiped) phone. This can cause problems re-pairing it. If possible, unpair your Apple Watch before restoring the iPhone: open the Watch app on your iPhone, tap **All Watches** at the top, tap the **(i)** next to your watch, and tap **Unpair Apple Watch**. If you can't access the Watch app because your iPhone won't turn on, you can also unpair directly from the watch: on the watch, go to **Settings > General > Reset > Erase All Content and Settings**. You'll need to enter your Apple ID password to turn off Activation Lock.

## Step 5: Restore Using DFU Mode (Deepest Software Fix)

If Recovery Mode didn't work — even a full Restore failed with an error — the software problem runs deeper than iOS itself. The issue may be in the iPhone's **firmware**, which is the low-level software that controls how the hardware boots up, before iOS even loads. DFU Mode (Device Firmware Update) lets your computer talk directly to the iPhone's hardware and overwrite EVERYTHING — firmware, iOS, all of it — with a completely fresh copy.

DFU Mode is the nuclear option. There is nothing deeper you can do yourself — after this, the only remaining option is Apple hardware repair. It works when nothing else does.

**Important:** The DFU Mode button sequence is harder to get right than Recovery Mode. Many people need 3-5 attempts before they succeed. Don't get frustrated — the timing is finicky.

**For iPhone 8, iPhone X, iPhone 11, iPhone 12, iPhone 13, iPhone 14, iPhone 15, iPhone 16, iPhone SE (2nd & 3rd gen):**

1. Connect your iPhone to the computer with a USB cable
2. Quick-press and release **Volume Up**
3. Quick-press and release **Volume Down**
4. Press and **hold** the **Side button** for about **10 seconds** — the screen will go completely black
5. While STILL holding the Side button, also press and **hold** the **Volume Down button**. Keep holding BOTH buttons for exactly **5 seconds**
6. After 5 seconds, **release the Side button** but **KEEP holding the Volume Down button** for about **10 more seconds**
7. If you did it correctly, the iPhone screen stays **completely black** (no Apple logo, no computer icon — nothing). But your computer will show a message that it detected an iPhone in recovery mode
8. If the screen shows ANYTHING — an Apple logo, a computer icon, anything — you accidentally entered Recovery Mode instead of DFU Mode. That's okay. Start over from Step 1 and try again with more precise timing

**For iPhone 7 and iPhone 7 Plus:**

1. Connect your iPhone to the computer
2. Press and **hold** both the **Side button** AND the **Volume Down button** for exactly **8 seconds**
3. After 8 seconds, **release the Side button** but **KEEP holding Volume Down** for about 10 more seconds
4. The screen should stay black, and the computer should detect the iPhone

**For iPhone 6s and older, iPhone SE (1st gen):**

1. Connect your iPhone to the computer
2. Press and **hold** both the **Home button** AND **Side/Top button** for exactly **8 seconds**
3. After 8 seconds, **release the Side/Top button** but **KEEP holding Home** for about 10 more seconds
4. The screen should stay black, and the computer should detect the iPhone

**Once in DFU Mode:**

1. On your computer, iTunes or Finder will show a message: "iTunes has detected an iPhone in recovery mode. You must restore this iPhone before it can be used."
2. Click **Restore iPhone** (or **OK**, then **Restore**)
3. Your computer will download the latest iOS firmware and perform a complete, deep restore
4. The process takes 15-30 minutes. Do NOT unplug during this time, even if the screen stays black the whole time — a black screen during DFU restore is NORMAL
5. When finished, your iPhone will restart and show the "Hello" setup screen
6. Restore your data from backup during setup

![A diagram showing the DFU Mode button timing sequence for modern iPhones: quick press Volume Up → quick press Volume Down → hold Side 10s → add Volume Down 5s → release Side, keep Volume Down](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People enter Recovery Mode by accident (the screen with the computer icon) and think they're in DFU Mode. They are NOT the same thing. Recovery Mode still loads the iPhone's bootloader — DFU Mode bypasses even the bootloader. You can tell the difference by looking at the iPhone screen: if there's ANYTHING on the screen (computer icon, Apple logo, anything at all), you're in Recovery Mode, not DFU Mode. DFU Mode = completely black screen, but the computer recognizes the phone. If you're trying to fix a problem that Recovery Mode couldn't fix, you MUST be in actual DFU Mode. Also: the timing of the button sequence is delicate. If you release the Side button too early or too late, you end up in Recovery Mode. It's completely normal to need multiple attempts — even experienced technicians sometimes need 3-4 tries. Use a timer app on another device if you need to.

> **Warning:** DFU Mode restore is even more destructive than Recovery Mode restore. Not only does it erase all your data, it also overwrites the device's firmware. If the DFU restore fails with an error, your iPhone has a hardware problem — there is nothing else you can do from home. The most common DFU error is "error 4013," which almost always means a hardware failure with the charging port flex cable, battery connector, or logic board. This needs professional repair.

## Still Stuck?

- **Force restart works temporarily but the iPhone dies again?** Your battery is probably failing. Go to **Settings > Battery > Battery Health & Charging**. If Maximum Capacity is below 80%, Apple officially recommends replacing the battery. A dying battery can't supply enough power to boot the phone reliably. Battery replacement costs $89-99 at Apple, or less at third-party repair shops. It takes about an hour and you don't lose any data.

- **iPhone got wet before this started happening?** Liquid damage is serious and time-sensitive. DO NOT plug the phone in to charge. DO NOT use rice (rice doesn't actually help and gets dust in the port). Instead: power the phone off if possible, place it in a dry, well-ventilated area for at least 24-48 hours, and then try turning it on. If it still won't start, internal corrosion has already damaged the components — you need professional repair. Liquid damage is NOT covered by Apple's standard warranty, but AppleCare+ does cover it with a service fee.

- **iPhone screen is black but you can hear sounds, feel vibrations, or Siri responds?** The screen itself is broken, but the phone is working. This means a hardware problem with the display — usually from a drop, impact, or pressure damage. You need a screen repair. Screen repair costs vary by model: $129-379 at Apple for newer iPhones without AppleCare+, or $29 with AppleCare+. Third-party shops are usually cheaper but may use lower-quality screens.

- **Recovery Mode Update failed with error 4013, error 9, or error 14?** Error 4013 nearly always means a hardware problem — sometimes just a loose internal connector (which a repair shop can reseat in 15 minutes), sometimes a failing logic board (which is expensive). Error 9 usually means a bad USB cable or port — try a brand-new cable, try a different USB port on your computer, or try a different computer entirely. Error 14 often means the iOS firmware file was corrupted during download — delete the downloaded firmware and try again. On Mac, the firmware is in `~/Library/iTunes/iPhone Software Updates`. On Windows, it's in `C:\Users\[YourName]\AppData\Roaming\Apple Computer\iTunes\iPhone Software Updates`. Delete the .ipsw file and let iTunes/Finder download a fresh copy.

- **Tried DFU restore and it also failed — what now?** Your iPhone has a hardware problem. The most common causes: a failed battery (won't hold enough charge to complete the restore process), a damaged charging port (loses connection during the restore), water damage (corroded internal components), or a logic board failure (the "brain" of the phone is damaged). An independent repair shop can diagnose which component is at fault. Apple Stores can run diagnostics for free and give you a repair estimate. If the repair cost is more than the phone is worth, consider selling it for parts and buying a replacement.

- **iPhone is under warranty or AppleCare+?** Don't waste your time with DIY fixes — take it to Apple. Apple's limited warranty covers manufacturing defects for 1 year from the purchase date. AppleCare+ extends this to 2 or 3 years and adds accidental damage coverage. Go to [checkcoverage.apple.com](https://checkcoverage.apple.com) and enter your iPhone's serial number to check your warranty status. If you can't access the serial number because the phone won't turn on, look at the original box, or check another Apple device signed into the same Apple ID under **Settings > [your name]** (scroll down to see your devices).

- **Need data recovery above all else?** If you have irreplaceable data on the iPhone (photos of a loved one who passed away, critical work documents, etc.) and no backup, stop trying restores and contact a professional data recovery service like DriveSavers or iFixit. These services cost $300-2000+ depending on the problem, but they have specialized tools that can recover data from iPhones with failed hardware. The longer you keep trying DIY fixes on a failing device, the lower your chances of professional recovery — every failed boot attempt can make the damage worse.
