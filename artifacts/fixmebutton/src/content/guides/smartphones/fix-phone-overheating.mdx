## Before You Start

Your phone feels hot in your hand — really hot. Maybe it shut itself off with a temperature warning, or maybe apps started lagging and stuttering. Phone overheating is scary because it feels like the device might get damaged, but in most cases, it's caused by something you can fix yourself in a few minutes.

A warm phone is normal during gaming, video calls, or GPS navigation. A *hot* phone — one that's uncomfortable to hold, shuts itself down, or gets hot when you're doing almost nothing — is a problem. This guide covers five fixes that work for iPhones, Samsung Galaxy, Google Pixel, and any other Android phone. Start with Fix 1 (it works immediately) and work your way down.

**What you'll need:**
- Your phone
- A cool, flat surface (a table, desk, or tile floor)
- Your phone charger (the official one that came in the box, if possible)

> **Warning:** If your phone is so hot that it's painful to touch, or if the back is bulging or the screen is lifting away from the body, put it down on a non-flammable surface (concrete, tile) away from anything that can catch fire, and DO NOT touch it. A bulging battery is a fire risk — contact the manufacturer or a repair shop immediately.

## Fix 1: Cool It Down Immediately (Emergency Steps)

If your phone is actively overheating right now, do these things immediately — in this order. Don't wait. Heat damages batteries permanently.

1. **Stop whatever you're doing.** Close the game, stop the video call, exit Google Maps. The fastest way to cool a phone is to stop making it work hard.
2. **Remove the phone case.** Cases trap heat like a winter coat. Take it off — even a thin case makes a big difference. Put the case aside for now.
3. **Turn on Airplane Mode.** Swipe down from the top-right (iPhone) or top (Android) to open Control Center or Quick Settings, and tap the airplane icon. This shuts off cellular, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and GPS — all things that generate heat. Leave Airplane Mode on for at least 5 minutes.
4. **Place the phone on a cool, flat surface.** A wooden table, tile floor, or granite countertop works great. Do NOT put it on a bed, couch cushion, pillow, or carpet — soft surfaces trap heat.
5. **Point a fan at it.** A regular desk fan or standing fan blowing room-temperature air is the safest way to cool a phone. Just set it up and wait.
6. **Lower the screen brightness.** Swipe open Control Center / Quick Settings and drag the brightness slider all the way down. The screen is one of the biggest heat generators in a phone.

![A phone lying on a cool tile floor with its case removed, a small fan blowing air across it](/images/guides/smartphones/cool-down-phone-fan.jpg)

> **Warning:** Never put your phone in the refrigerator, freezer, or in front of an air conditioning vent blowing cold air. Rapid temperature changes create condensation (water droplets) inside the phone, which can short-circuit the electronics and cause permanent damage. Room-temperature air from a fan is safe and effective.

**Common mistake:** Putting the phone in a pocket or bag to "let it rest." Enclosed spaces trap heat and make the problem worse. Always place an overheating phone on an open, hard surface with airflow.

## Fix 2: Find and Close the Rogue App

Sometimes a single app goes haywire — it gets stuck in a loop, uses 100% of the processor, and turns your phone into a pocket heater. This is called a "rogue app" or "runaway process," and it's one of the most common causes of sudden, unexplained overheating.

**iPhone — Check battery usage to find the culprit:**

1. Open **Settings → Battery**
2. Scroll down to **Battery Usage by App**
3. Look at the list. Tap **Show Activity** to see how much time each app spent running in the background
4. If you see an app using a huge percentage of battery (30%, 50%, or more) — especially one you haven't been using — that's your rogue app
5. Force-close it: swipe up from the bottom and pause (or double-click the Home button on older iPhones) to see open apps, then swipe the problem app up and off the screen
6. If the app keeps misbehaving, delete it and reinstall it from the App Store. Bad app data that causes overheating usually clears up with a fresh install

**Android — Check battery usage to find the culprit:**

1. Open **Settings → Battery → Battery Usage** (on Samsung: **Settings → Device Care → Battery → View Details**)
2. Look at the list of apps sorted by battery usage since the last full charge
3. If an app you barely used is at the top of the list, it's running wild in the background
4. Tap the app name and select **Force Stop** (on some phones: tap the app, then tap **Force Stop** at the bottom)
5. If the app keeps misbehaving after a force stop, uninstall it and reinstall from the Play Store — but first check the app's recent reviews. If lots of other people are complaining about overheating after a recent update, wait for the developer to fix it before reinstalling

![Phone Settings screen showing Battery Usage by App with one app using an unusually high percentage](/images/guides/smartphones/battery-usage-rogue-app.jpg)

> **Tip:** Social media apps (Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, Snapchat) and ride-sharing apps (Uber, Lyft) are common rogue-app offenders because they constantly refresh feeds, track location, and play auto-playing videos — all in the background, even when you're not using them. If one of these is your culprit, go into the app's own settings and turn off auto-play videos and background location access.

**Common mistake:** Force-closing ALL apps by swiping them all away. This doesn't help — in fact, it can make things worse because your phone has to work harder to re-launch apps from scratch. Only force-close the ONE app that's showing heavy background usage. Let the rest be.

## Fix 3: Check Your Charging Habits

A phone that overheats specifically while charging has a different problem — and it's usually fixable without buying anything new.

1. **Check your charging cable and brick.** Are you using the charger that came with your phone? Cheap, off-brand chargers (from gas stations, dollar stores, or random Amazon sellers) often lack proper voltage regulation and can send too much power to your phone, causing it to overheat. Use the original charger or a reputable brand (Anker, Belkin, Apple, Samsung).
2. **Don't use your phone while it charges.** Gaming or streaming video while plugged in forces the battery to charge AND discharge at the same time — that's a recipe for extreme heat. Let it charge undisturbed.
3. **Charge on a hard, flat surface — never on a bed, couch, or pillow.** Soft surfaces block the phone's ability to radiate heat. There are real cases of phones overheating under pillows and starting fires.
4. **Turn off fast charging if your phone supports it.** Fast charging generates more heat by design. If your phone consistently overheats while charging, try switching to regular-speed charging:

**iPhone — Turn off fast charging by using a slower charger:**
- Fast charging requires a USB-C to Lightning cable and a 20W or higher power adapter. If you use an older 5W cube charger (the small square one) and a regular USB-A to Lightning cable, your iPhone charges slower and cooler. Keep one of these older chargers around for overnight charging when speed doesn't matter.

**Samsung Galaxy — Turn off fast charging:**
1. Open **Settings → Device Care → Battery → Charging settings**
2. Turn off **Fast charging** and **Super fast charging**
3. Your phone will now charge at regular speed — slower, but much cooler

**Google Pixel — Turn off fast charging:**
1. Open **Settings → Battery → Charging optimization**
2. Turn off **Adaptive Charging** (this doesn't directly disable fast charging but slows the charge rate, which reduces heat)

![Phone plugged into a charger on a hard wooden desk — not on a soft surface](/images/guides/smartphones/phone-charging-hard-surface.jpg)

> **Tip:** If your phone gets extremely hot ONLY when wireless charging, the wireless charger itself might be the problem. Cheap wireless chargers can misalign the charging coils and waste energy as heat. Try a different wireless charger, or switch to wired charging and see if the problem goes away.

**Common mistake:** Assuming that because a charger fits, it's safe. USB-C and Lightning are standard ports, but the electronics inside the charger brick vary wildly in quality. A $5 charger from a convenience store might lack overheat protection, overcurrent protection, and voltage regulation — the three safety features that keep your phone from frying. If your phone gets hot during charging, the charger is suspect #1.

## Fix 4: Adjust Settings That Make Your Phone Work Too Hard

Some phone settings make the processor and screen work overtime — generating heat constantly, even when you're just scrolling through messages. Tweaking a few settings can drop your phone's temperature noticeably.

**Turn down screen brightness (biggest impact):**
- The screen is usually the #1 heat source in a phone, especially at maximum brightness
- Open Control Center (iPhone) or Quick Settings (Android) and drag the brightness slider to about 50%
- Turn on **Auto-Brightness**: Settings → Accessibility → Display & Text Size → Auto-Brightness (iPhone) or Settings → Display → Adaptive brightness (Android)

**Turn off background app refresh:**
- Apps refreshing in the background keep your processor working even when you're not using the phone

**iPhone:**
1. Open **Settings → General → Background App Refresh**
2. Turn it off entirely (toggle at the top), OR scroll through the list and turn it off for apps that don't need it (games, shopping apps, food delivery apps)
3. Keep it on for messaging apps if you need instant notifications

**Android:**
1. Open **Settings → Apps → See all apps**
2. Tap an app you don't need updating in the background
3. Tap **Mobile data & Wi-Fi → Background data → Off**
4. Repeat for other heavy apps
5. On Samsung: **Settings → Device Care → Battery → Background usage limits → put unused apps to sleep**

**Turn off unnecessary location services:**
- GPS is a major heat source. Many apps track your location constantly even when you're not using them.

**iPhone:**
1. Open **Settings → Privacy & Security → Location Services**
2. Scroll through the list — for apps that don't need your location all the time, change from **Always** to **While Using the App** or **Never**

**Android:**
1. Open **Settings → Location → App location permissions**
2. Tap each app and change from **Allow all the time** to **Allow only while using the app** or **Deny**

**Turn off animated wallpapers and reduce visual effects:**
- Live wallpapers, parallax effects, and other animations keep the graphics processor running constantly

**iPhone:**
1. Open **Settings → Accessibility → Motion**
2. Turn on **Reduce Motion**
3. Go back to Wallpaper settings and use a still image (not a Live Photo) as your wallpaper

**Android:**
1. Open **Settings → Accessibility → Visibility enhancements** (or **Display** on some phones)
2. Turn on **Remove animations** or **Reduce animations**
3. Change your wallpaper to a static image — avoid live wallpapers

![Phone Settings screen with brightness slider at 50% and Auto-Brightness toggled on](/images/guides/smartphones/screen-brightness-settings.jpg)

> **Tip:** If you use your phone for GPS navigation in the car, mount it on a vent clip — the AC air blowing on the back of the phone keeps it cool even during long trips. Avoid windshield mounts in direct sunlight, which bake the phone while it's already working hard running GPS.

**Common mistake:** Turning on Low Power Mode / Battery Saver and thinking that fixes overheating. These modes reduce background activity, which helps a little, but they don't address the root causes — the screen brightness, location tracking, and background refresh settings above. Use Low Power Mode as a temporary band-aid, not a permanent fix.

## Fix 5: Update Your Software and Check Battery Health

When none of the above fixes work, the problem is likely deeper — either a software bug that needs a system update, or a battery that's reached the end of its life.

**Update your operating system:**

Software bugs — including ones that cause overheating — get fixed in updates. Running an old version of iOS or Android means you're living with bugs that have already been patched.

**iPhone:**
1. Open **Settings → General → Software Update**
2. If an update is available, tap **Update Now** (plug your phone in first — updates need at least 50% battery or a charger connected)
3. After updating, use your phone normally for a day and see if the overheating stops

**Android (Google Pixel):**
1. Open **Settings → System → Software updates → System update**
2. Tap **Check for update**, then **Download and install** if available

**Samsung Galaxy:**
1. Open **Settings → Software update → Download and install**
2. Follow the prompts to install any available update

**Check battery health:**

A worn-out battery is less efficient — it wastes more energy as heat. If your phone is more than 2 years old and you've tried everything else, the battery may need replacing.

**iPhone:**
1. Open **Settings → Battery → Battery Health & Charging**
2. Look at **Maximum Capacity** — if it's below 80%, your battery is significantly degraded
3. You'll also see a message: "Your battery's health is significantly degraded. An Apple Authorized Service Provider can replace the battery..." — this means it's time for a new battery
4. Apple charges $89–$99 for battery replacement (prices vary by model). Third-party shops charge $50–$80. If you have AppleCare+, battery replacement is free when capacity drops below 80%

**Android:**
1. Android doesn't have a built-in battery health percentage like iPhone does
2. Instead, look for signs of a failing battery: the phone dies at 20% or 30% (won't stay on), battery percentage jumps around (goes from 50% to 10% instantly), the back is slightly bulging, or the phone gets hot even during light use
3. On Samsung phones, open the **Samsung Members** app, go to **Support → Phone diagnostics → Battery status** — this runs a basic battery check
4. Battery replacement at a repair shop typically costs $60–$100 for Android phones

![iPhone Settings showing Battery Health with Maximum Capacity at 79% and a service warning](/images/guides/smartphones/battery-health-service-warning.jpg)

> **Warning:** Do not attempt to replace a phone battery yourself unless you have proper tools and experience. Phone batteries are glued into the frame with strong adhesive, and puncturing a lithium-ion battery can cause it to catch fire or explode. This is one repair where paying a professional is worth every penny.

**Common mistake:** Thinking that because a software update "might cause problems," it's safer to skip it. The opposite is true. OS updates include thermal management improvements, bug fixes for known overheating issues, and security patches that block malware (which can secretly mine cryptocurrency on your phone, generating massive heat). Always install updates — they exist to fix problems, not create them.

## Still Stuck?

- **Phone only overheats outside in summer?** That's environmental — not a phone problem. Keep it in the shade, don't leave it in a hot car (even for 10 minutes — car interiors can hit 120°F+), and avoid using GPS or the camera for extended periods in direct sunlight. If you must use your phone outside on a hot day, a white or reflective phone case can help deflect heat.

- **Phone is hot AND the battery drains in under 2 hours?** This combination almost always means the battery is failing. A healthy phone battery should last most of a day with normal use. If you're charging 2-3 times a day AND the phone is hot, the battery needs replacement — no amount of settings tweaks will fix a physically worn-out battery.

- **Phone gets hot during phone calls specifically?** Holding the phone against your ear traps body heat, and the cellular radio works harder during calls. Use speakerphone or Bluetooth headphones for long calls to keep the phone away from your face and let it breathe.

- **Phone overheats and shuts off with a temperature warning screen?** This is a safety feature — the phone is protecting itself from permanent damage. When you see the warning (iPhones show "iPhone needs to cool down before you can use it," Androids may show a similar warning or just shut off), follow Fix 1 immediately. Wait until the phone feels cool to the touch before turning it back on. Repeated temperature-warning shutdowns will permanently damage the battery.

- **Tried everything and phone still overheats — is it malware?** It's possible, especially on Android. Malicious apps can secretly mine cryptocurrency (using your phone's processor at 100% around the clock), display hidden ads, or run background processes that you can't see. Install a reputable security app (Malwarebytes, Bitdefender, or AVG — all have free versions) and run a full scan. On iPhone, malware is much rarer but not impossible — a factory reset (Settings → General → Transfer or Reset iPhone → Erase All Content and Settings) will remove any malicious software. Back up your data first.
