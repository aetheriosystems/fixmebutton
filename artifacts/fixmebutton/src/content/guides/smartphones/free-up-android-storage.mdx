## Before You Start

Running out of storage on your Android phone? Photos, videos, and apps are usually the biggest space-hogs. This guide works for Samsung Galaxy, Google Pixel, OnePlus, Motorola, and most other Android phones.

To check how much space you have left: go to **Settings → Storage** (on Samsung, it's **Settings → Device Care → Storage**). You'll see a breakdown showing what's using your space.

![Screenshot of the Android Storage screen showing a colored bar with categories like Apps, Images, Videos, Audio, and System]

## Step 1: Delete Apps You Don't Use

Android phones can pile up unused apps over time. Some of them can be surprisingly large — games especially can take up 1-5 GB each.

1. Go to **Settings → Storage → Apps** (or **Settings → Apps**)
2. You'll see your apps listed by how much space they use, biggest first
3. Tap an app you don't use anymore
4. Tap **Uninstall**
5. Repeat for any other apps you haven't opened in months

> **Common Mistake:** Don't uninstall system apps like "Google Play Services," "Android System WebView," or "SIM Toolkit." These are part of Android and removing them can cause problems. Only uninstall apps you downloaded yourself from the Play Store.

If you're not sure about an app, look for the "Open" button — if it says "Enable" instead of "Uninstall," that means the app came with your phone and can only be disabled, not deleted.

## Step 2: Clear App Caches

Apps store temporary files called "cache" to load faster. Over time, cache files can build up and eat several gigabytes of space — and clearing them is totally safe.

1. Go to **Settings → Storage**
2. Look for a button or suggestion that says **Free Up Space** or **Clean Up** (most Android phones have this built in)
3. Tap it, and your phone will show temporary files you can delete
4. Check the boxes for cached data, then tap **Free Up** or **Delete**

If your phone doesn't have a built-in cleaner, you can clear cache for individual apps:

1. Go to **Settings → Apps**
2. Tap the app (start with Chrome, YouTube, Instagram, TikTok — these build up the most cache)
3. Tap **Storage & cache**
4. Tap **Clear Cache** (NOT "Clear Storage" or "Clear Data" — that would log you out)

![Screenshot of the App Info screen with Storage & cache option highlighted, then the Clear Cache button]

> **Common Mistake:** Tapping "Clear Storage" or "Clear Data" instead of "Clear Cache" will erase your login info, saved settings, and downloaded content inside that app. Only tap the button that says "Clear Cache."

## Step 3: Delete Large Files with Files by Google

Most Android phones come with a file manager app called **Files** (or **Files by Google**). It can find large files, duplicate photos, and unused downloads.

1. Open the **Files** app (if you don't have it, download **Files by Google** from the Play Store — it's free)
2. Tap the **Clean** button at the bottom
3. You'll see suggestions like:
   - **Delete old screenshots** (screenshots you took and never used again)
   - **Delete duplicate photos** (identical copies that waste space)
   - **Delete large files** (files over 100 MB you may have forgotten about)
   - **Delete downloaded files** (PDFs, ZIP files, APKs from your Downloads folder)
4. Tap each suggestion, review what it found, then tap **Delete**

![Screenshot of Files by Google showing the Clean tab with suggestions like Delete duplicate photos and Delete large files]

## Step 4: Back Up and Remove Photos and Videos

Photos and videos are almost always the biggest storage consumer. Instead of deleting them forever, back them up to Google Photos first.

1. Open the **Google Photos** app (comes pre-installed on most Android phones)
2. Tap your profile picture or initial in the top right corner
3. Tap **Photos settings → Backup**
4. Make sure **Backup** is turned on
5. Choose **Storage saver** quality (free unlimited-ish storage — see note below) or **Original quality** (counts toward your Google account storage)
6. Wait for your photos to finish backing up (you'll see a progress bar)
7. Once backed up, tap your profile picture again and select **Free up space**
8. Google Photos will find photos and videos already backed up and offer to delete them from your phone

![Google Photos app with the Free up space option showing how much space can be saved]

> **Note:** As of 2026, Google Photos no longer offers completely free unlimited storage. The "Storage saver" option compresses your photos (still looks great on a phone screen) and counts toward your free 15 GB of Google account storage. If you have a lot of photos, you may need to buy more storage — 100 GB costs $1.99/month.

## Step 5: Remove Downloaded Music, Podcasts, and Streaming Content

Streaming apps often download content so you can listen or watch offline. Those downloads eat up storage fast.

**Spotify:**
1. Open Spotify → **Your Library**
2. Tap the **Downloaded** filter at the top
3. Tap a downloaded album or playlist → tap the green download arrow to remove it

**YouTube / YouTube Music:**
1. Open YouTube → **Library → Downloads**
2. Tap the three-dot menu on any video → **Delete from device**

**Netflix:**
1. Open Netflix → **Downloads** tab
2. Tap the download icon on any show or movie → **Delete Download**
3. Or: tap **Smart Downloads** at the top and turn it off (this stops Netflix from auto-downloading new episodes)

**Podcasts (Google Podcasts or similar):**
1. Open your podcast app → **Downloads** or **Downloaded Episodes**
2. Delete episodes you've already listened to

![Screenshot collage showing download sections in Spotify, YouTube, and Netflix]

## Step 6: Empty the Trash in Photos and Files

When you delete photos or files on Android, they move to a Trash or Recycle Bin folder — and stay there for 30 days, still taking up space.

1. Open the **Google Photos** app
2. Tap **Library** (bottom right) → **Trash**
3. Tap the three-dot menu (top right) → **Empty trash**
4. Confirm by tapping **Delete**

Also check the Files app:
1. Open the **Files** app
2. Tap the hamburger menu (three lines, top left) → **Trash** or **Recycle bin**
3. Tap **Empty trash**

![Google Photos Trash screen with the Empty trash option highlighted]

> **Common Mistake:** Photos in the Trash still count against your storage until the trash is emptied or 30 days pass. If you're desperate for space right now, empty the trash manually instead of waiting.

## Step 7: Restart Your Phone

Sometimes Android misreports how much storage is available. A restart forces it to recalculate — and you may see a few hundred extra megabytes appear.

1. Press and hold the **power button** (and **volume down** on some phones) until the power menu appears
2. Tap **Restart**
3. Wait 30-60 seconds for your phone to fully restart

Go back to **Settings → Storage** — you may see slightly more free space than before.

## Still Stuck?

If you're still running out of space after all seven steps:

- **Consider a microSD card:** Many Android phones (especially Samsung Galaxy and Motorola) have a microSD card slot. You can buy a 128 GB card for around $15 and move photos, videos, and music to it. Go to **Settings → Storage → SD Card** after inserting the card, and look for an option to move files.
- **Use Google Photos more aggressively:** In Google Photos settings, enable **"Free up device storage"** auto-management so it regularly removes backed-up photos without you thinking about it.
- **Factory reset as a last resort:** If your phone has years of mystery files and nothing else works, you can factory reset it. **Warning:** This erases everything on the phone. Back up your photos, contacts, and messages first. Go to **Settings → System → Reset options → Erase all data (factory reset)**.
- **Your phone may just be too old:** If your phone has only 16 GB or 32 GB of storage and you've done everything above, it may be time for a phone with more storage. Most modern Android phones start at 128 GB.
