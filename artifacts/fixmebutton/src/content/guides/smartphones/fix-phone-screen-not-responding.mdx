## Before You Start

You tap an app and nothing happens. You swipe to unlock and the screen ignores your finger. Maybe only part of the screen works, or maybe it's completely frozen. An unresponsive touch screen is scary — it makes your phone feel broken and useless — but in most cases, it's a software glitch, dirt, or a bad screen protector, not a dead screen. You can fix it yourself.

This guide covers five fixes that work on iPhones, Samsung Galaxy, Google Pixel, and any other Android phone. Start with Fix 1 (it's the easiest and most likely culprit) and work your way down. You'll need your phone and a soft microfiber cloth — that's it for Fix 1.

**What you'll need:**
- Your phone
- A clean microfiber cloth (the kind for glasses or screens)
- A second phone or computer (to look up instructions while your phone is stuck, if you're reading this on the phone that's broken)
- If you have one: a second phone to test with for Fix 3

> **Warning:** If your screen is visibly cracked with sharp edges, or if the glass is lifting away from the body of the phone, do NOT press hard on it and do NOT use a wet cloth. Sharp glass edges can cut your fingers, and liquid can seep through the cracks into the phone's internal parts. Skip Fix 1 and go straight to Fix 3 — you probably have physical damage.

## Fix 1: Clean the Screen and Remove Your Screen Protector

Dirt, grease, sweat, and hand lotion build up an invisible film on your screen that blocks touch sensitivity. A screen protector — especially a cheap one, a cracked one, or one that's been on for more than a year — can also interfere with the touch digitizer (the layer inside your phone's screen that detects your fingers).

**Clean the screen properly:**

1. Turn off your phone first if you can (if the screen is partly working, use the power button). If the screen is completely dead, skip to step 3
2. Take a **dry microfiber cloth** — the kind that comes with glasses or screen cleaning kits. Do NOT use paper towels, toilet paper, or your shirt. Paper products are abrasive and scratch the screen's oleophobic coating (the invisible oil-repellent layer)
3. Wipe the screen firmly in small circles, covering the entire surface. Focus especially on the edges and the area around the front camera notch — oil and dirt collect there
4. If the screen still isn't responding, lightly dampen one corner of the microfiber cloth with a tiny amount of water (NOT soaking wet — just barely damp). Wipe the screen, then immediately dry it with the dry part of the cloth
5. Wash your hands. Oil and residue from your fingers transfer right back to the screen

**Remove your screen protector:**

If cleaning didn't fix it, your screen protector could be the culprit — even if it looks perfectly fine.

1. Carefully peel off the screen protector starting from one corner. Go slowly — cracked tempered glass protectors can splinter
2. Try using the phone without the protector. If the screen responds perfectly, the protector was the problem
3. Buy a new screen protector from a reputable brand. Cheap protectors from dollar stores or random online sellers often use low-quality adhesive and materials that block the electrical signal between your finger and the screen

**For sticky residue left behind by an old screen protector:**

1. Put a tiny drop of rubbing alcohol (isopropyl alcohol — 70% is fine) on the microfiber cloth, not directly on the screen
2. Wipe the residue off. The alcohol evaporates instantly and will not damage your screen
3. Never use nail polish remover, window cleaner (Windex), or household cleaning sprays — these contain ammonia and other chemicals that strip the screen's protective coating

![A hand using a microfiber cloth to clean a smartphone screen with circular motions](/images/guides/smartphones/cleaning-phone-screen.jpg)

> **Tip:** If your screen only stops responding when your hands are very dry or when you're wearing gloves, that's a different issue. Modern touch screens detect the tiny electrical charge in your skin. Dry skin and gloves block that signal. Try moisturizing your hands (then wiping off excess so you don't grease the screen) or buying gloves with touchscreen-compatible fingertips.

**Common mistake:** Pressing harder when the screen doesn't respond. Touch screens don't work by pressure — they work by detecting the electrical conductivity of your finger. Pressing harder doesn't make the signal stronger. It just risks cracking the screen. If a light tap doesn't register, the problem is something else — keep going through the fixes.

## Fix 2: Force Restart Your Phone (It's Different From a Normal Restart)

A "force restart" is NOT the same as turning your phone off and on. A normal restart shuts down software cleanly. A force restart cuts power to the processor and display controller at the hardware level, which can clear a stuck touch driver that a normal restart misses.

This is the most reliable fix for touch screens that suddenly stop working for no obvious reason. It's what Apple Store and Samsung repair techs try first.

**iPhone 8, iPhone SE (2nd and 3rd gen), iPhone X and newer (all Face ID models):**

1. Press and quickly **release** the **Volume Up** button (just click it, don't hold)
2. Press and quickly **release** the **Volume Down** button
3. Press and **hold** the **Side button** (the power button on the right side). Keep holding even when you see the "slide to power off" slider — ignore it and keep holding
4. Keep holding until the screen goes black, then the Apple logo appears (this takes 10–15 seconds)
5. Release the Side button and let your phone start normally

**iPhone 7 and iPhone 7 Plus:**

1. Press and **hold** the **Volume Down button** (left side) AND the **Sleep/Wake button** (right side) at the same time
2. Keep holding both buttons, ignoring any sliders that appear on screen
3. Release both buttons when the Apple logo appears (about 10 seconds)

**iPhone 6s, iPhone SE (1st gen), and older models with a physical Home button:**

1. Press and **hold** the **Home button** (the round button below the screen) AND the **Sleep/Wake button** (top edge or right side) at the same time
2. Keep holding both buttons, ignoring any sliders
3. Release both buttons when the Apple logo appears

**Google Pixel and most Android phones (OnePlus, Motorola, Nothing, etc.):**

1. Press and **hold** the **Power button** (and on some models, the **Volume Down button** at the same time) for at least 10–15 seconds
2. You may feel a vibration and see the screen go black, then the manufacturer logo will appear
3. Release the button(s) and let the phone restart normally

**Samsung Galaxy (most models since 2017):**

1. Press and **hold** the **Power button** (side key) and the **Volume Down button** at the same time
2. Keep holding both buttons for about 10–15 seconds
3. The screen will go black and the Samsung logo will appear — release both buttons

> **Note:** On newer Samsung Galaxy models (S22 and later), the "Power button" might be labeled "Side key" in some guides. It's the same button — the one you use to lock and unlock the phone. Just press and hold Volume Down + Side Key.

![Someone pressing the Volume Up and Side buttons on an iPhone, performing a force restart sequence](/images/guides/smartphones/force-restart-phone.jpg)

> **Warning:** Do NOT do a force restart if your phone is connected to a computer for a software update or data transfer. Cutting power suddenly during a data operation can corrupt files. Make sure no sync or update is in progress.

**Common mistake:** Letting go of the buttons too early. A force restart requires holding for a full 10–15 seconds — it feels longer than you expect. People often release the buttons when they see the "slide to power off" slider, which cancels the force restart. Ignore everything on the screen and keep holding until you see the logo.

## Fix 3: Check for Water Damage and Physical Damage

If the screen stopped working right after you dropped the phone, spilled liquid on it, or used it in heavy rain, you may be dealing with physical damage. This fix helps you figure out if the problem is hardware (needs a repair shop) vs. software (you can still fix it yourself).

**Check for water damage — Liquid Contact Indicator (LCI):**

Most phones have a small sticker inside the SIM card tray slot that changes color when it gets wet.

1. Use the SIM ejector tool (or a small paperclip) to pop out the SIM card tray
2. Shine a flashlight into the empty SIM tray slot and look at the inner wall
3. Find the LCI — it's a small dot or strip:
   - **iPhone:** Look for a small white/silver dot. If it's turned **red or pink**, water got inside
   - **Samsung Galaxy:** Look for a small white square with pink/purple X marks. If the X marks have bled or turned solid pink, water got inside
   - **Google Pixel:** Look for a small white sticker. If it's turned **pink or red**, water got inside

**What to do if water got inside (and the screen is still partly working):**

1. Power off the phone immediately — do not charge it, do not keep using it
2. Remove the SIM card tray and leave it out for airflow
3. Place the phone upright (charging port facing down) in a dry, well-ventilated area
4. If you have **silica gel packets** (the little "Do Not Eat" packets from shoe boxes and electronics packaging), place the phone in a sealed container or Ziploc bag with a handful of them. Silica gel actually absorbs moisture — unlike rice, which is mostly a myth
5. Wait at least **24 hours** before turning the phone back on
6. Do NOT use a hair dryer, microwave, or oven to speed up drying — heat damages the screen and battery
7. Do NOT put the phone in rice. Rice dust gets into ports and doesn't absorb moisture from inside the phone any better than open air

**Check for physical damage:**

1. Look at the screen closely under bright light. Do you see any hairline cracks, even tiny ones? A crack that doesn't look like much can still break the digitizer layer (the part of the screen that detects touch)
2. Press very gently around the edges of the screen with your fingertip. Does the screen move, click, or feel spongy? A screen that's separating from the body means the phone was dropped or bent
3. Check if only ONE part of the screen is unresponsive — for example, the top half works but the bottom half doesn't, or a single vertical or horizontal strip is dead. A dead zone that follows a straight line usually means the digitizer is physically cracked, even if the glass on top looks fine
4. Look at the display — are there any black spots, purple blobs, green lines, or flickering areas? These are signs the LCD or OLED panel underneath the glass is damaged

> **If you find physical damage:** The fixes in this guide won't help. You need a screen replacement. Apple charges $129–$379 depending on the iPhone model (iPhone SE is cheapest, Pro Max models are most expensive). For Android, prices range from $80 for older models to $300+ for the latest Samsung Galaxy Ultra or Google Pixel Pro. Authorized repair shops (Apple Store, Samsung Experience Store, uBreakiFix) are safest. Third-party mall kiosks often use low-quality screens that break again in months.

![A SIM card tray popped out of a phone, with a flashlight shining into the slot to check the Liquid Contact Indicator](/images/guides/smartphones/checking-water-damage-indicator.jpg)

> **Tip:** Even water-resistant phones (iPhone 7 and newer with IP67/IP68 rating, most Samsung Galaxy and Pixel phones) are only resistant to splashes and brief submersion — NOT to salt water, chlorinated pool water, soapy water, or steam. And water resistance weakens over time as seals age. A 2-year-old phone with an IP68 rating is not as waterproof as when it was new.

**Common mistake:** Assuming that because your phone is "water-resistant," water damage is impossible. The IP67/IP68 rating is tested in a lab with fresh water — not the ocean, not a pool with chlorine, not a hot shower with steam. Salt water corrodes the metal contacts inside the phone. Chlorine and soap break down the rubber seals. And the warranty (AppleCare, Samsung Care) does NOT cover water damage, even on water-resistant phones. If your phone got wet and the screen stopped working, you'll pay for the repair.

## Fix 4: Update Your Software and Adjust Touch Settings

If there's no dirt, no water damage, and no cracks, the problem is likely software. Operating system bugs can cause the touch screen to freeze, lag, or ignore input — and these bugs get fixed in updates. Both iOS and Android also have hidden touch sensitivity settings that can help.

**Update your operating system:**

If your screen is partly working (enough to navigate Settings), do this first. If the screen is completely frozen and you've already tried a force restart (Fix 2), skip to the "Update via computer" section below.

**iPhone (on the phone itself):**

1. Open **Settings → General → Software Update**
2. If an update is available, tap **Update Now**. You'll need at least 50% battery or to be plugged into power
3. If you can't tap the button because the screen isn't responding, plug your phone into a Mac or Windows PC and use Finder (Mac) or iTunes (Windows) to update

**Android (on the phone itself):**

1. Open **Settings → System → Software updates → System update** (on Samsung: **Settings → Software update → Download and install**)
2. Tap **Check for update**
3. If an update is available, download and install it

**Update via computer if the screen is too unresponsive:**

1. **iPhone:** Connect to a Mac or PC. On Mac, open **Finder** and click your iPhone in the sidebar. On Windows, open **iTunes** and click the iPhone icon. Click **Check for Update**, then **Update**
2. **Samsung Galaxy:** Download and install Samsung's **Smart Switch** app on a Windows PC or Mac. Connect your phone with a USB cable. Smart Switch will check for software updates
3. **Google Pixel:** Visit the **Android Flash Tool** (flash.android.com) in Chrome on a computer, connect your Pixel, and follow the on-screen instructions to install the latest update

**Adjust touch sensitivity settings:**

Some hidden accessibility settings can make an unresponsive screen work better — or accidentally make it worse if they were turned on by mistake.

**iPhone — Check Touch Accommodations:**

1. Open **Settings → Accessibility → Touch → Touch Accommodations**
2. If this is **ON** and your screen isn't responding, turn it **OFF** — these settings can interfere with normal touch detection when configured incorrectly
3. If it's OFF and your screen is sluggish, try turning it ON and adjusting **Hold Duration** to a shorter time. This makes the phone register your taps faster
4. Under the same Touch menu, check **Haptic Touch** (or 3D Touch on older iPhones). Set it to **Fast**. Slower settings can make the screen feel unresponsive

**Android — Check touch sensitivity and accessibility settings:**

1. Open **Settings → Display** and look for **Touch sensitivity** (on Samsung: **Settings → Display → Touch sensitivity**). Turn it **ON** — this increases the screen's sensitivity, especially useful if you have a screen protector installed
2. Open **Settings → Accessibility** and scroll through every category. Look for anything called **Touch & hold delay**, **Tap duration**, or **Interaction controls**. Set these to **Short** or **Off** — long delays can make the screen feel like it's not responding
3. On Google Pixel: **Settings → Accessibility → Timing controls → Touch & hold delay → Short**

![Phone Settings screen showing the Touch Accommodations menu with Hold Duration and other options](/images/guides/smartphones/touch-accommodations-settings.jpg)

> **Tip:** If your screen was working fine until you installed a specific app, that app might be the problem. Some poorly-coded apps (especially games, screen recorders, or "phone cleaner" apps) can interfere with the touch system. Boot your phone into **Safe Mode** to test: on most Androids, press and hold the Power button, then press and hold the "Power off" option until you see "Reboot to safe mode." On iPhone, there's no Safe Mode, but you can try uninstalling the most recently installed apps one by one.

**Common mistake:** Skipping software updates because "My phone was working fine before, so it can't be a software problem." Software bugs can appear suddenly — a background process crashes, a driver gets corrupted, or an app update introduces a conflict. The operating system doesn't need to have changed for a software bug to cause touch problems. Always update before assuming it's hardware.

## Fix 5: Reset All Settings (Last Software Fix Before a Repair Shop)

When everything above fails and there's no visible damage, the problem is likely deep in the phone's configuration files — corrupted settings that survived a force restart. Resetting all settings wipes these configuration files without deleting your personal data (photos, apps, messages, accounts). You will need to re-enter Wi-Fi passwords, re-pair Bluetooth devices, and reconfigure your wallpaper and accessibility preferences afterward.

**iPhone — Reset All Settings:**

1. Open **Settings → General → Transfer or Reset iPhone**
2. Tap **Reset**
3. Tap **Reset All Settings** (NOT "Erase All Content and Settings" — that wipes everything)
4. Enter your passcode when asked
5. Confirm by tapping **Reset All Settings** again
6. Your iPhone will restart. After restart, your home screen layout, wallpaper, Wi-Fi passwords, Bluetooth pairings, and Apple Pay cards will need to be set up again — but your apps, photos, and messages will all still be there
7. Test the touch screen

**Android — Reset All Settings:**

1. Open **Settings → System → Reset options** (on Samsung: **Settings → General management → Reset**)
2. Tap **Reset all settings** (NOT "Erase all data / Factory reset")
3. Enter your PIN, pattern, or password to confirm
4. Your phone will restart. After restart, reconnect to Wi-Fi, re-pair Bluetooth devices, and reconfigure any custom ringtones or notification sounds
5. Test the touch screen

**If Reset All Settings doesn't work — Factory Reset:**

A factory reset wipes your ENTIRE phone — apps, photos, messages, accounts, everything. Back up your data FIRST or you'll lose it permanently. Only do this if you're confident the problem is software (no cracks, no water, and the screen sometimes works).

**iPhone:**

1. Back up: connect to a computer and use Finder (Mac) or iTunes (Windows), OR back up to iCloud (Settings → [your name] → iCloud → iCloud Backup → Back Up Now)
2. After backup is complete: **Settings → General → Transfer or Reset iPhone → Erase All Content and Settings**
3. After the reset, your phone will start like new. Set it up and choose **Restore from iCloud Backup** or **Restore from Mac/PC**
4. Test the touch screen BEFORE restoring the backup — if it works perfectly on the fresh setup but breaks again after restoring, the problem is in your backup data, not the hardware

**Android:**

1. Back up: **Settings → Google → Backup → Back up now** (or **Settings → Accounts and backup → Back up data** on Samsung)
2. After backup is complete: **Settings → System → Reset options → Erase all data (factory reset)** (on Samsung: **Settings → General management → Reset → Factory data reset**)
3. After the reset, set up your phone and restore from the backup
4. As with iPhone: test the screen before restoring. If the problem comes back with the backup, the data was corrupted

> **If a factory reset doesn't fix the screen:** The problem is definitely hardware. You need a screen replacement. A phone repair shop can run a diagnostic that tests the digitizer to confirm. Screen replacements typically take 1-2 hours at a repair shop and cost $80-$380 depending on your phone model. Apple and Samsung stores also offer mail-in repair (3-5 business days). If your phone is under warranty and there's no physical or water damage, the repair may be free.

![Phone screen showing the "Reset All Settings" option — distinct from "Erase All Content and Settings"](/images/guides/smartphones/reset-all-settings-phone.jpg)

> **Warning:** If your phone is managed by a company, school, or organization (you see a "This iPhone is supervised" message or a work profile on Android), do NOT factory reset it. The organization's IT department may need to remove device management first, or the reset could lock you out of work apps and accounts permanently. Contact your IT department.

**Common mistake:** Choosing "Erase All Content and Settings" when you meant to choose "Reset All Settings." Read the buttons carefully. "Reset All Settings" keeps your data. "Erase All Content and Settings" wipes your phone clean. There is no undo for the wrong choice — and if you haven't backed up, your photos and messages are gone forever.

## Still Stuck?

- **Only part of the screen doesn't work (a strip, corner, or half)?** This is almost always physical damage to the digitizer, even if the glass looks fine. A drop or pressure (like sitting on the phone in a back pocket) can crack the digitizer layer underneath the glass. Screen replacement is the only fix. Take it to a repair shop.

- **Screen works sometimes but not others (intermittent)?** Intermittent problems are usually a loose internal connector. When you tap or move the phone, the connection between the screen and the motherboard briefly reconnects. A repair shop can open the phone and re-seat the connector — this is often a $30-$60 fix, much cheaper than a full screen replacement.

- **Screen stopped working after an iOS or Android update?** This is a known issue that happens occasionally. Check online forums (Reddit r/iPhone, r/Android, or Apple/Google support communities) for your specific phone model and software version. If it's a widespread bug reported by many users, the manufacturer will release a fix within days. In the meantime, a force restart (Fix 2) can be a temporary workaround each time it freezes.

- **Screen is black AND unresponsive (phone might be on but display is dead)?** The problem might not be the touch screen at all — it might be the display itself. Try this: call your phone from another phone. If it rings but the screen stays black, the display is dead, not the touch digitizer. If the screen lights up when you get a call, the display works but the touch layer doesn't. This distinction matters — tell the repair shop exactly what you observed.

- **Phone is under warranty or you have insurance?** AppleCare+ covers screen damage for a $29 deductible. Samsung Care+ covers it for $29 as well. Wireless carrier insurance (Verizon, AT&T, T-Mobile, etc.) typically charges $29-$99 for screen replacement depending on your plan. Check your coverage before paying full price at a repair shop. If your phone is less than 1 year old and has NO physical damage or water exposure, the standard manufacturer warranty may cover a defective touch screen for free.
