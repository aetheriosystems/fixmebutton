## Before You Start

You open your browser to check email, and suddenly a pop-up screams "YOUR COMPUTER IS INFECTED! CALL THIS NUMBER NOW!" Or every time you try to load a website, you get sent to a weird ad page instead. Or new tabs keep opening on their own with gambling or adult ads. These are all signs that something nasty has gotten into your browser — and good news: it's almost never a real virus. In most cases, it's a sneaky website you accidentally gave permission to send you notifications, a bad browser extension, or some adware that hitched a ride when you installed a free program.

This guide works for **Google Chrome** on Windows, Mac, Android, and iPhone — and the same steps apply to **Microsoft Edge** (which is built on the same engine). The fixes are arranged from easiest (and most common) to most thorough. Work through them in order — most people solve the problem by Step 2.

**What you'll need:**
- Your computer or phone
- About 15 minutes
- An internet connection (you may need to download a scanner in Step 5)

> **Important: Do NOT call the phone number on the pop-up.** Fake virus alerts that urge you to call a "Microsoft support" or "tech support" number are scams. Microsoft, Apple, and Google will NEVER show you a pop-up with a phone number to call. The scammers want you to call so they can charge you hundreds of dollars to "fix" a problem that doesn't exist, or worse, gain remote access to your computer to steal your data. Close the pop-up, ignore the number, and follow this guide.

> **Mac users:** The steps below use Chrome settings, which are identical on Mac and Windows. If the problem is happening in Safari instead of Chrome, the notification and extension steps are different. Search for "Safari pop-up ads fix" for Safari-specific instructions.

## Step 1: Turn Off Push Notifications From Bad Sites

This is the #1 cause of fake virus pop-ups and scam alerts. A website asks "Can we send you notifications?" — you click **Allow** without thinking — and now that site can push pop-ups to your desktop ANY time, even when your browser is closed. Scammers abuse this by sending fake virus warnings that look like system alerts.

**Fix 1 — Identify and block notification-spamming sites (Chrome on computer)**

1. Open **Chrome**.
2. Click the **three dots** (⋮) in the top-right corner, then click **Settings**.
3. On the left side, click **Privacy and security** → **Site Settings**.
4. Scroll down and click **Notifications** (under the "Permissions" section).
5. Here you'll see two lists:
   - **"Allowed to send notifications"** — these sites CAN push pop-ups to your screen. Look for anything suspicious: sites you don't remember visiting, URLs with weird names like "prize-winner-2025.top" or "antivirus-scan-alert.com," or anything that doesn't look like a real website.
   - **"Not allowed to send notifications"** — these sites are blocked.
6. For every suspicious site under "Allowed," click the **three dots** (⋮) next to it and select **Block** (or **Remove**).
7. If the list is long and you're not sure which site is the culprit, you can also block ALL sites at once: at the very top, under "Default behavior," choose **"Don't allow sites to send notifications."** This is the nuclear option, but it guarantees no site can send you notification pop-ups. You can always re-enable it later for sites you trust.

**Fix 2 — Block notifications on Android**

1. Open **Chrome** on your Android phone.
2. Tap the **three dots** (⋮) in the top-right corner, then **Settings**.
3. Tap **Site settings** → **Notifications**.
4. Under "Allowed," look for suspicious sites. Tap each one and toggle **Show notifications** to **OFF**.
5. To prevent this from happening again, scroll to the very top and tap **Notifications** → change it to **Blocked** (this blocks all sites from asking to send notifications).

**Fix 3 — Block notifications on iPhone/iPad**

1. On iPhone, Chrome notifications are actually controlled by the iOS system, not by Chrome directly. If you're seeing Chrome pop-ups on your iPhone, it's likely coming from a website you visited.
2. Open **Chrome**, tap the **three dots** (⋮) → **Settings** → **Content Settings** → **Block Pop-ups**. Make sure this is turned ON.
3. Also check **Settings → Notifications → Chrome** (in your iPhone's main Settings app, not inside Chrome) and turn OFF **Allow Notifications** if you're seeing unwanted alerts.

![Google Chrome Notifications settings page showing a list of "Allowed to send notifications" sites with suspicious URLs](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People click **Block** on the pop-up itself but never revoke the notification permission. The pop-up stops for that moment, but the site still has permission and will send another pop-up in a few hours or the next day. You must revoke the permission in Chrome Settings or the site keeps pushing pop-ups forever.

> **Tip:** If you see "McAfee," "Norton," or "Windows Defender" in a browser pop-up and you don't actually have that antivirus installed, it's 100% fake. Real antivirus programs don't deliver warnings through your browser's notification system — they show alerts in their own app window or in the Windows system tray.

## Step 2: Remove Suspicious or Unwanted Extensions

Browser extensions are small programs that add features to your browser — ad blockers, coupon finders, screenshot tools. But bad extensions (or good extensions that got bought by shady companies) can inject ads, redirect your searches, and track everything you do.

**Fix 1 — Remove suspicious extensions on computer**

1. Open **Chrome**.
2. Click the **three dots** (⋮) → **Extensions** → **Manage Extensions** (or just type `chrome://extensions` in the address bar and press Enter).
3. You'll see a list of every extension you've installed. Look for:
   - Extensions you don't remember installing
   - Extensions with generic names like "Coupon Saver," "PDF Converter," "Video Downloader," or "Shopping Helper"
   - Extensions whose names are just random letters or misspelled words
   - Extensions that claim to do something too good to be true (like "Free Netflix" or "Unlimited Robux")
4. For each suspicious extension, click **Remove**. Chrome will ask "Remove [extension name]?" — click **Remove** to confirm.
5. If you're not sure whether an extension is safe: toggle it OFF with the blue switch first. If the pop-ups stop, the extension was the problem — come back and remove it permanently.
6. Do NOT remove extensions you know are legitimate (like Google Docs Offline, LastPass, uBlock Origin, Grammarly — if you installed them yourself and trust them).

**Fix 2 — Remove suspicious extensions on Android**

1. Chrome extensions don't run on Android Chrome — so if your Android phone has pop-ups, skip this step. The problem is either a notification permission (Step 1) or a bad app on your phone (see Step 4 for phones).

![Chrome Extensions management page showing several extensions with the Remove button visible](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People delete an extension but don't realize the extension installed a separate program on their computer. If the pop-ups come back after deleting the extension, check for unwanted programs on your computer (Step 4). Some extensions are just the browser-side half of a program that also installed itself on your system.

> **Tip:** A good rule of thumb: if you have more than 10 extensions installed and you can't remember what half of them do, you have too many. Remove everything you don't actively use and recognize. A clean browser is a safe browser. You can always reinstall an extension later if you miss it.

## Step 3: Reset Chrome Settings and Clear Browsing Data

If Steps 1 and 2 didn't work — or if your homepage keeps changing, your search engine got swapped to something weird like "Search Marquis" or "Yahoo" when you normally use Google, or new tabs keep opening on their own — it's time for a deeper clean. Resetting Chrome wipes out any settings the bad software changed, without deleting your bookmarks or passwords.

**Fix 1 — Reset Chrome settings to their original defaults**

1. Open **Chrome**.
2. Click the **three dots** (⋮) → **Settings**.
3. On the left menu, click **Reset settings** (it's at the very bottom — you may need to scroll).
4. Click **Restore settings to their original defaults**.
5. A warning box will appear explaining what this does: it resets your startup page, new tab page, search engine, and pinned tabs. It also disables all extensions (you can re-enable the ones you trust later) and clears temporary data like cookies. It does NOT delete your bookmarks, history, or saved passwords.
6. Click **Reset settings** to confirm.
7. Chrome will restart. After it reopens, go through and re-enable only the extensions you trust (⋮ → Extensions → Manage Extensions → toggle ON for each trusted extension).

**Fix 2 — Clear your browsing data (cache, cookies, and site data)**

Sometimes bad sites store data in your browser that keeps the pop-ups coming back even after resetting settings.

1. Open **Chrome**.
2. Click the **three dots** (⋮) → **Delete browsing data** (or press **Ctrl+Shift+Delete** on Windows, **Command+Shift+Delete** on Mac).
3. In the pop-up window, click the **Advanced** tab.
4. Set the **Time range** dropdown to **All time**.
5. Make sure these three boxes are checked: **Browsing history**, **Cookies and other site data**, and **Cached images and files**. (Leave **Passwords and other sign-in data** UNCHECKED — no need to log out of every website.)
6. Click **Delete data**.
7. Close and reopen Chrome. Some sites will load a bit slower the first time (they need to re-download images), but any tracking cookies or scripts left behind by bad sites will be gone.

![Chrome Reset Settings page showing the "Restore settings to their original defaults" button](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People reset Chrome settings but forget to clear cookies and cached data. If a bad website stored a tracking cookie that triggers pop-ups, resetting settings alone won't touch it. Always do BOTH: reset settings AND clear browsing data for "All time."

> **Tip:** After resetting Chrome, your homepage and search engine should go back to Google. If they change back to something else within a few hours or the next day, you still have an unwanted program running on your computer. Go to Step 4.

## Step 4: Remove Unwanted Programs From Your Computer or Phone

Pop-ups and redirects that survive a browser reset are being fed by a program installed on your device. These are often "potentially unwanted programs" (PUPs) that came bundled with free software — you installed a free screen recorder or PDF tool and accidentally agreed to install extra "offers" along with it. They're not technically viruses, but they're not doing you any favors either.

**Fix 1 — Uninstall suspicious programs (Windows)**

1. Click the **Start** button, then click the **Settings** gear icon (or press **Windows key + I**).
2. Click **Apps** → **Installed apps** (on Windows 11) or **Apps & features** (on Windows 10).
3. Scroll through the list or use the search box. Look for anything that:
   - You don't remember installing
   - Has a generic name like "PC Cleaner," "Driver Updater," "Search Protect," "Shopping Helper," "Coupon Printer," or "Weather Toolbar"
   - Was installed around the same date the pop-ups started
   - Has a publisher name you don't recognize or that looks fake
4. Click the **three dots** next to any suspicious program and select **Uninstall**. Follow the prompts.
5. If a program won't uninstall normally (it says "still running" or the Uninstall button does nothing), restart your computer in Safe Mode and try again. To enter Safe Mode on Windows 11: hold Shift while clicking Restart → Troubleshoot → Advanced Options → Startup Settings → Restart → press 4.
6. Pay special attention to programs with names like **WebDiscover**, **Search Manager**, **Yontoo**, **Conduit**, or **MySearchDial** — these are well-known browser hijackers.

**Fix 2 — Remove suspicious programs (Mac)**

1. Open **Finder**.
2. In the left sidebar, click **Applications**.
3. Look for any app you don't recognize or didn't intentionally install. Names like "MacKeeper," "CleanMyMac" (unless you intentionally bought it), "Search Manager," or anything with "toolbar" or "coupon" in the name are suspicious.
4. Drag the suspicious app to the **Trash** (or right-click → **Move to Trash**).
5. Right-click the **Trash** icon in your Dock and select **Empty Trash**.
6. Also check **System Settings → General → Login Items** (on newer macOS) or **System Preferences → Users & Groups → Login Items** (on older macOS). Remove anything suspicious from the list of programs that start automatically when you log in.

**Fix 3 — Remove bad apps (Android phone)**

1. Open your Android phone's **Settings** app.
2. Tap **Apps** (or **Apps & notifications** → **See all apps**).
3. Scroll through the list. Look for apps you don't remember installing — especially apps without proper icons, apps with generic names, or apps installed around the same time the pop-ups started.
4. Tap the suspicious app, then tap **Uninstall**.
5. If the Uninstall button is grayed out, the app may have Device Administrator permissions. Go to **Settings → Security → Device admin apps** (or **Settings → Biometrics and security → Other security settings → Device admin apps**), find the app, and toggle it OFF. Then go back and uninstall it.
6. After uninstalling suspicious apps, open the **Google Play Store**, tap your profile picture → **Play Protect** → **Settings** (gear icon) → turn ON **Scan apps with Play Protect**. This helps catch bad apps before they cause trouble.

**Fix 4 — Remove bad apps (iPhone/iPad)**

iPhones are generally less vulnerable to unwanted programs, but Safari pop-ups and calendar spam can still happen.

1. Go to **Settings → Safari** and tap **Clear History and Website Data**. Confirm.
2. Check **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**. If you see an unknown profile (especially one from a website you visited or a free streaming app), tap it and select **Remove Profile**.
3. If you see spam events in your Calendar app, you may have accidentally subscribed to a spam calendar. Go to **Settings → Calendar → Accounts** and look for any "Subscribed Calendars" you don't recognize. Tap it and select **Delete Account**.
4. Also check your installed apps — delete any you don't recognize or no longer use.

![Windows Settings "Installed apps" page showing a list of programs with an "Uninstall" option visible for a suspicious app](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People uninstall the suspicious program but don't restart their computer or browser afterward. Some unwanted programs leave behind running processes that only stop on restart. After uninstalling, restart your computer and then open Chrome to check if pop-ups are gone.

> **Tip:** Many unwanted programs were installed alongside legitimate free software. When you install free programs in the future, choose "**Custom installation**" or "**Advanced installation**" instead of "Express" or "Recommended" — this lets you see and uncheck any bundled "offers" before they're installed. Never just click "Next, Next, Next" through an installer.

## Step 5: Run a Malware Scan (Windows and Android)

If you've done Steps 1–4 and still see pop-ups or redirects, there may be actual malware on your device — a program designed to steal information, mine cryptocurrency, or serve ads. This step covers free, trusted scanners that can find and remove what might be hiding.

**Fix 1 — Run a full scan with Microsoft Defender (Windows 10 and 11, free, built-in)**

1. Click the **Start** button, type **Windows Security**, and open it.
2. Click **Virus & threat protection**.
3. Under "Current threats," click **Scan options**.
4. Select **Full scan** (this checks every file on your computer — it takes 30 minutes to an hour or more, but it's thorough) and click **Scan now**.
5. Wait for the scan to complete. If Microsoft Defender finds anything, it will give you options to quarantine or remove the threats. Click **Start actions** and follow the prompts.
6. After the scan and cleanup, restart your computer.

**Fix 2 — Run a second scan with Malwarebytes (Windows and Mac, free)**

Microsoft Defender is good, but no single scanner catches everything. Malwarebytes is a free second-opinion scanner that specializes in adware, browser hijackers, and PUPs — exactly the kind of things that cause pop-ups.

1. Go to **https://www.malwarebytes.com** and download the free version. (Do NOT download Malwarebytes from any other site — fake versions exist.)
2. Install and open Malwarebytes. Skip the premium trial if offered (click "No thanks" or close the offer — the free scanner is all you need).
3. Click the **Scan** button and let it run. It usually takes 5–15 minutes.
4. If Malwarebytes finds threats, click **Quarantine** to remove them. Restart your computer when prompted.
5. You can uninstall Malwarebytes afterward if you don't want to keep it — or leave it installed for occasional manual scans.

**Fix 3 — Scan your Android phone with Play Protect**

1. Open the **Google Play Store** app.
2. Tap your **profile picture** (top-right) → **Play Protect**.
3. Tap **Scan**. Google checks all your installed apps for malicious behavior.
4. If Play Protect flags anything, follow the prompts to remove the bad app.
5. For a deeper scan, install **Malwarebytes** for Android (free from the Play Store) and run a scan.

**Fix 4 — If nothing works: The nuclear option**

If scans find nothing and pop-ups persist, your Chrome profile may be too damaged to fix. This is rare but possible.

1. **Back up your bookmarks:** In Chrome, click ⋮ → **Bookmarks and lists** → **Bookmark Manager** → ⋮ (inside the Bookmark Manager) → **Export bookmarks**. Save the file somewhere safe.
2. **Back up your passwords:** Make sure Chrome Sync is on (⋮ → Settings → You and Google → Sync and Google services → Manage what you sync → make sure Passwords is ON). Your passwords will sync to your Google account and come back when you sign in again.
3. **Sign out of Chrome:** ⋮ → Settings → click **Turn off** next to your name (under "You and Google"). In the box that appears, check "Clear bookmarks, history, passwords, and more from this device" — this removes the entire Chrome profile from your computer.
4. **Sign back in:** Open Chrome again and sign into your Google account. Your bookmarks and passwords will sync back. Reinstall only trusted extensions.
5. For the nuclear option on Android: Go to **Settings → Apps → Chrome → Storage** and tap **Clear storage** (this resets Chrome completely). Then sign back in.

![Windows Security Virus and Threat Protection page showing scan options with "Full scan" selected](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People download "PC cleaner" or "virus removal" tools advertised in pop-ups or search results. These are often fake or harmful programs themselves. Only download security tools from the official website: Microsoft Defender is already on your Windows computer, and Malwarebytes should only be downloaded from **malwarebytes.com**. Never call a phone number from a pop-up, and never give remote access to anyone who contacted you first.

> **Tip:** Even after cleaning everything, you may still see pop-ups on one specific website. That means the problem isn't on your computer — the website itself is serving malicious pop-ups. Close the tab and don't visit that site again. If it's a site you need (like a school or work portal), tell the site owner — their site may have been hacked.

## Still Stuck?

- **Pop-ups only appear on one specific website, and nowhere else?** The website itself is the problem — not your computer. The site may be running scam ads or has been compromised. Stop visiting the site. If it's a site you rely on (a school portal, a work intranet, a shopping site), contact the site owner and let them know their site is showing malicious pop-ups.

- **Pop-ups appear even when Chrome isn't open?** On Windows, this usually means a program installed on your computer is generating the pop-ups directly. Go back to Step 4 and look harder for unwanted programs. Check your system tray (the area near the clock in the bottom-right corner of your screen) for any icons you don't recognize — right-click them and look for exit/uninstall options. Also check **Task Manager** (press Ctrl+Shift+Esc) → **Startup apps** tab and disable anything suspicious.

- **Using a work or school computer and can't change settings?** Your IT department may have locked down browser settings. Contact your IT help desk, describe the pop-ups you're seeing, and ask them to remove the notification permissions and scan for malware. Don't try to bypass IT restrictions — your organization has them for security reasons.

- **On iPhone and pop-ups keep coming even after clearing Safari?** Check your iPhone's **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management** for any unknown configuration profiles. Delete them. Also check if any installed app has permission to show notifications (Settings → Notifications — look for apps you don't recognize with notifications enabled).

- **Want to prevent this from happening again?** Install an ad blocker. **uBlock Origin** is the most trusted ad blocker and it's free. It blocks not just ads but also known malicious domains that serve pop-ups and fake virus alerts. On desktop Chrome: go to the Chrome Web Store, search for "uBlock Origin," and install it. On mobile, consider switching to **Firefox** on Android (which supports uBlock Origin) or **Brave** browser on iPhone (which has built-in ad blocking). A good ad blocker prevents most pop-ups before they ever appear.

- **Think your passwords might have been stolen?** If you entered any passwords while your browser was infected, change them immediately — from a clean device, not from the infected one. Go to each important account (email, banking, social media) and change the password. If you use the same password on multiple sites, change those too. Consider using a password manager so each site has a unique password. Enabling **two-factor authentication** (2FA) on your most important accounts also adds a layer of protection even if a password does get stolen.
