## Before You Start

You type a website address into Safari and press Enter — but nothing happens. The page stays blank. Or it loads halfway and stops. Or you see messages like **"Safari Can't Open the Page,"** **"A problem repeatedly occurred,"** or **"This webpage was reloaded because a problem occurred."** Meanwhile, the same website loads instantly in Chrome or on your phone. Frustrating, right?

Safari is the default browser on every Mac, iPhone, and iPad, and when it stops loading pages, it feels like the internet is broken — even though it's not. The good news: Safari page-loading problems are almost always caused by corrupted cached data, a misbehaving extension, a privacy setting that's blocking too much, or a VPN interfering. These are all fixable without buying anything or calling Apple Support.

This guide walks you through the five most effective fixes, ordered from the quickest to the most thorough. Most people have Safari working again by Fix 2 or 3.

**What you'll need:**
- Your Mac, iPhone, or iPad
- Safari (it's already installed — no download needed)
- About 15 minutes
- Your website passwords handy (some fixes sign you out of websites)

> **Work through the fixes in order.** Each one solves the problem for many users — you probably won't need all five. If a fix doesn't work, move to the next. The fixes at the end (Fix 4 and 5) are stronger but take more steps.

## Fix 1: Quick Checks — Reload, Restart, and Update

Before digging into settings, rule out the simplest causes. These checks take under two minutes and fix about one-quarter of all Safari loading problems.

**Fix 1a — Reload the page (the right way):**

A simple reload often fixes a page that glitched while loading. But a regular reload reuses some of the cached data that might be causing the problem.

1. While on the blank or broken page, click the **View** menu at the top of your screen, then choose **Reload Page** — or press **Command-R** on your keyboard.
2. If that doesn't help, do a **hard reload** instead: hold down the **Option (⌥) key** on your keyboard, then click **View > Reload Page From Origin**. On iPhone or iPad, tap the **reload arrow** in the address bar (the circular arrow icon on the right side of the address field).

**Fix 1b — Quit Safari completely and reopen it:**

Safari sometimes gets stuck in a bad state where background processes freeze. Closing the window doesn't fully quit Safari.

1. **On Mac:** Click the **Safari** menu (top-left, next to the Apple logo) and choose **Quit Safari**. Or press **Command-Q**. Then wait 10 seconds and reopen Safari from the Dock or Applications folder.
2. **On iPhone/iPad:** Swipe up from the bottom of the screen (or double-click the Home button on older devices) to see your open apps. Find Safari and swipe it up and off the screen to close it. Then tap the Safari icon to reopen it.

**Fix 1c — Restart your device:**

A restart clears temporary system glitches that can affect Safari.

1. **On Mac:** Click the **Apple menu ()** in the top-left corner and choose **Restart**.
2. **On iPhone/iPad:** Hold down the power button and either volume button until the "slide to power off" slider appears. Slide to turn off. Wait 30 seconds, then hold the power button to turn it back on.

**Fix 1d — Update macOS or iOS:**

Safari is built into the operating system — it updates when your device updates. An outdated version may have bugs that Apple already fixed.

1. **On Mac:** Apple menu () > **System Settings** > **General** > **Software Update**. If an update is available, click **Update Now**.
2. **On iPhone/iPad:** **Settings** > **General** > **Software Update**. If an update is available, tap **Download and Install**.

![The Safari menu on Mac with "Quit Safari" highlighted](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People click the red X button in the corner of the Safari window and think they've quit Safari. On a Mac, that only closes the window — the Safari app keeps running (you can see the black dot under the Safari icon in the Dock). You must use **Safari > Quit Safari** or **Command-Q** to fully shut it down and give it a fresh start. On iPhone, people tap the home screen and assume Safari closed — it's still running in the background. Always swipe it away in the app switcher.

## Fix 2: Clear Safari's History, Cache, and Website Data

Safari stores copies of websites on your device so they load faster next time. This is called the **cache**. It also saves cookies (small files websites use to remember you) and browsing history. Over time, this stored data can become corrupted, bloated, or outdated — and instead of speeding things up, it prevents pages from loading. This is the single most common fix for Safari loading problems.

**Fix 2a — Test in a Private Window first:**

Before clearing everything, test whether stored data is actually the problem. A private window doesn't use your history, cache, or cookies.

1. **On Mac:** Click **File > New Private Window** or press **Shift-Command-N**.
2. **On iPhone/iPad:** Tap the **tabs button** (two overlapping squares) in the bottom-right, then tap **Private** (or the private tab group icon), then tap the **+ (plus)** button to open a private tab.
3. Try loading the broken website in this private window.

**If the website loads in the private window:** Your saved data is the problem. Proceed with Fix 2b to clear it.
**If it still doesn't load:** The problem is not your cache — skip to Fix 3.

**Fix 2b — Clear history and website data (Mac):**

> **Warning:** Clearing history and website data signs you out of most websites and removes your browsing history. Make sure you know your passwords before continuing. Your bookmarks and saved passwords (in iCloud Keychain) are NOT affected.

1. In Safari, click **Safari** menu (top-left) > **Settings** (or **Preferences** on older macOS).
2. Click the **Privacy** tab.
3. Click the **Manage Website Data…** button.
4. A list of every website that has stored data on your Mac appears. You can:
   - **Remove a single site:** Type the website's name in the search bar, select it, and click **Remove**.
   - **Remove everything:** Click **Remove All**, then confirm by clicking **Remove Now**.
5. Click **Done** to close the window.
6. Now, still in Safari Settings, go to the **Advanced** tab. At the bottom, check the box for **"Show features for web developers"** (or "Show Develop menu in menu bar").
7. A new **Develop** menu appears in the top menu bar. Click **Develop > Empty Caches**.
8. Quit Safari (Command-Q) and reopen it. Test your website.

**Fix 2c — Clear history and website data (iPhone/iPad):**

1. Open the **Settings** app (the gray gear icon), not Safari itself.
2. Scroll down and tap **Safari**.
3. Scroll down and tap **Clear History and Website Data**.
4. A popup asks you to confirm. Tap **Clear History and Data**.
5. This clears browsing history, cookies, and cache across all your devices signed into the same iCloud account. Safari restarts fresh.

![The Safari Privacy settings showing the Manage Website Data button on Mac](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People clear their cache and then panic when websites load slightly slower the first time. This is normal and temporary — Safari is rebuilding its cache from scratch. After visiting each site once, it'll be back to full speed, but now without the corrupted data that was breaking things. Also, people sometimes only clear their history (through the **History > Clear History** menu) and wonder why the problem persists. Clearing history alone does NOT remove the cache or cookies — you need to use the **Manage Website Data** and **Empty Caches** options for a proper cleanup.

## Fix 3: Turn Off Safari Extensions and Check Content Blockers

Safari extensions add extra features to your browser — ad blockers, password managers, coupon finders, grammar checkers, and more. But extensions can also interfere with how websites load. An ad blocker might accidentally block parts of a page that aren't ads. A password manager might conflict with a website's login form. And some extensions become incompatible after a macOS or iOS update.

The fix is simple: turn everything off, test, and then turn things back on one at a time to find the troublemaker.

**Fix 3a — Disable extensions on Mac:**

1. In Safari, click **Safari > Settings** (or Preferences) > **Extensions** tab.
2. You'll see a list of all installed extensions on the left, each with a checkbox. **Uncheck every extension** to turn them all off.
3. Quit Safari (Command-Q) and reopen it.
4. Try loading the broken website. Does it work now?
   - **If yes:** One of your extensions was causing the problem. Go back to the Extensions tab, turn on extensions **one at a time**, testing the broken website after each one. When the website breaks again, you've found the culprit. Either remove it (select it and click **Uninstall**) or check if it has an update available.
   - **If no:** Extensions aren't the problem. Turn them back on and move to Fix 3b.

**Fix 3b — Check content blockers on iPhone/iPad:**

On iPhone and iPad, content blockers work differently than Mac extensions — they're separate apps that Safari uses.

1. Open the **Settings** app > **Safari** > **Extensions**.
2. Tap each extension listed and turn off its toggle (or tap "Turn Off" next to "Allow Extension").
3. Go back to Safari Settings, scroll down, and look for **Content Blockers** (under "Settings for Websites"). Make sure they're all toggled off for now.
4. Close Safari completely (swipe it away in the app switcher) and reopen it. Test your website.

**Fix 3c — Check per-website settings on Mac:**

Safari lets you set custom permissions for individual websites. A website might be blocked from loading because Safari's per-site settings have content blockers, pop-up blockers, or auto-play restrictions turned on for that specific site.

1. With Safari open, click **Safari > Settings > Websites** tab.
2. On the left sidebar, look at these sections one by one: **Content Blockers**, **Pop-up Windows**, **Auto-Play**, and **Page Zoom**.
3. In each section, find the website you're having trouble with in the list on the right. Check its permission — make sure it's set to **Allow** or **Off** (for content blockers, set to **Off**).
4. Also check the **Privacy** tab (Safari > Settings > Privacy) — make sure **"Hide IP address from trackers"** and **"Prevent cross-site tracking"** aren't breaking the site. You can temporarily uncheck them to test.

![The Safari Extensions tab on Mac showing installed extensions with checkboxes to enable or disable](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People install ad blockers and content blockers, forget about them, and then can't figure out why websites look broken or won't load at all. Ad blockers don't just block ads — they can also block videos, images, login forms, and entire sections of websites. A website that shows a blank white page but works in Chrome is almost always an ad blocker or content blocker issue. Also, note that Safari on Mac can have both traditional extensions AND content blocker apps — you need to check both. And on iPhone, the content blocker might be its own app (like 1Blocker or AdGuard) — you need to open that app and turn it off, not just check Safari settings.

## Fix 4: Check iCloud Private Relay, VPN, and Network Settings

Apple's **iCloud Private Relay** (included with any paid iCloud+ subscription) routes your internet traffic through two separate relays to hide your IP address. It's a great privacy feature — but it sometimes blocks websites that don't work well with relayed traffic. Similarly, VPN apps (Virtual Private Networks) can interfere with page loading, especially for banking, shopping, or streaming sites that check your location. And custom DNS settings or proxy configurations can redirect traffic in ways that break Safari.

**Fix 4a — Bypass iCloud Private Relay for the broken website:**

1. **On Mac:** While on the broken page, click the **View** menu in the top menu bar. If you see **"Reload and Show IP Address,"** that means Private Relay is active and might be the problem. Click it — Safari reloads the page without Private Relay for this one site.
2. **On iPhone/iPad:** Tap the **AA** button (or the reader icon) in the left side of the address bar, then tap **"Show IP Address"** or **"Reload Without Content Blockers."**
3. If the website now loads, Private Relay is the issue. You can either keep using the "Show IP Address" option for that specific site, or turn off Private Relay entirely:
   - **Mac:** Apple menu () > **System Settings** > [your name at top] > **iCloud** > **Private Relay** > **Turn Off**.
   - **iPhone/iPad:** **Settings** > [your name] > **iCloud** > **Private Relay** > turn it off.

**Fix 4b — Turn off your VPN temporarily:**

If you use a VPN app (NordVPN, ExpressVPN, Surfshark, ProtonVPN, etc.), it can cause websites to block you, load slowly, or not load at all.

1. Open your VPN app and disconnect.
2. Also check if your VPN has a system-wide setting — on Mac, go to **System Settings > VPN & Filters** and make sure no VPN configuration is active. On iPhone/iPad, go to **Settings > VPN** and make sure it says "Not Connected."
3. Test the website in Safari. If it now loads, your VPN was causing the problem. Try connecting to a different VPN server location, or add the broken website to your VPN's "split tunneling" or "bypass" list if the app supports it.

**Fix 4c — Reset DNS cache and check proxy settings (Mac only):**

A corrupted DNS cache can make Safari unable to find websites even though your internet connection is fine. DNS (Domain Name System) is like the phonebook of the internet — it translates website names (like "google.com") into IP addresses that computers understand.

1. Open the **Terminal** app (find it in Applications > Utilities, or search with Spotlight by pressing Command-Space and typing "Terminal").
2. Type this command exactly and press Enter:
   ```
   sudo dscacheutil -flushcache; sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder
   ```
3. You'll be asked for your Mac's login password. Type it (the cursor won't move or show dots — that's normal) and press Enter.
4. Now check for proxy settings: Apple menu () > **System Settings > Network > Wi-Fi** (or your active connection) > **Details > Proxies**. Make sure no proxy is turned on unless you specifically set one up (most home users shouldn't have any proxies enabled).

![The Mac System Settings showing the iCloud Private Relay option](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People subscribe to iCloud+ for extra storage and don't realize Private Relay turned on automatically. It's enabled by default for iCloud+ subscribers on both Mac and iPhone/iPad. If Safari suddenly stops loading certain websites after you upgraded your iCloud storage plan, Private Relay is very likely the cause. Also, many VPN apps set themselves to "Connect on Demand" or "Always On" — meaning they reconnect automatically when you restart your device. If you disconnect your VPN and Safari works, but then it breaks again the next day, your VPN probably reconnected itself. Check the VPN app's settings for an auto-connect option and turn it off.

## Fix 5: Reset Safari to Factory Settings (Nuclear Option)

When none of the simpler fixes work, the problem is likely a corrupted Safari preferences file or a deeply hidden misconfiguration. The solution used by Apple Support Community experts — confirmed by over 100 users on Apple's forums — is to completely delete Safari's settings folder and let your Mac rebuild it from scratch.

> **Warning:** This fix is safe but thorough. It removes ALL Safari settings, history, bookmarks, extensions, and website data. You MUST export your bookmarks first if you want to keep them. Your saved passwords in iCloud Keychain are NOT affected — they'll still be there after the reset.

**Fix 5a — Export your bookmarks (Mac only):**

1. In Safari, click **File > Export > Bookmarks…**
2. Choose a location you'll remember (like your Desktop or Documents folder) and click **Save**. This creates an HTML file with all your bookmarks.

**Fix 5b — Delete Safari's preferences folder (Mac only):**

1. Quit Safari completely (Safari > Quit Safari, or Command-Q).
2. Open **Finder** (click the Finder icon in the Dock — the blue smiley face).
3. From the menu bar at the top, click **Go**. Now **hold down the Option (⌥) key** on your keyboard. A new hidden menu item called **Library** appears. Click it.
4. Inside the Library folder, find the folder named **Safari**.
5. Right-click the **Safari** folder and choose **Move to Trash** (or drag it to the Trash in the Dock).
6. **Restart your Mac:** Apple menu () > **Restart**.
7. After restarting, open Safari. It creates a brand-new Safari folder with default settings — like you just got a new Mac.
8. **Re-import your bookmarks:** Click **File > Import From > Bookmarks HTML File…** and select the bookmarks file you saved in Fix 5a.
9. Test your broken website. It should now load.

**Fix 5c — If you're on iPhone or iPad (no folder to delete):**

On iOS, you can't delete Safari's settings folder, but you can do the closest equivalent — reset all Safari data and settings:

1. **Settings > Safari > Clear History and Website Data** (as in Fix 2c).
2. Also in Safari settings, scroll to the bottom and tap **Advanced > Website Data > Remove All Website Data**.
3. Go to **Settings > General > Transfer or Reset iPhone/iPad > Reset** (at the bottom). Tap **Reset Network Settings**. This clears WiFi passwords, VPN settings, and network configurations — but your personal data, photos, and apps are safe.
4. Restart your device and test Safari.

![The Finder Go menu on Mac with the Option key pressed, revealing the hidden Library folder](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People see the word "Library" and think of the Library folder that's visible in their home folder (the one with Music, Pictures, and Documents folders). That's not the right one. The Safari settings live in `~/Library/Safari` — a HIDDEN Library folder that you can only access by holding Option while clicking the Go menu. If you go to the regular Library or try to find it in your home folder without the Option trick, you won't find it. Also, people sometimes trash the Safari application itself (from the Applications folder) instead of the preferences folder — don't do that. The Safari app is part of macOS and can't easily be reinstalled. You're deleting the settings FOLDER, not the app.

## Still Stuck?

- **Safari doesn't load pages but Chrome and Firefox work fine?** The problem is definitely with Safari, not your internet. After trying all five fixes, the last resort on Mac is to create a new user account to test: Apple menu () > **System Settings > Users & Groups > Add User**. Log into the new account and try Safari there. If Safari works in the new account, your original account has a deeper system-level issue. Apple Support can help with that.

- **Safari loads some websites but not others?** The specific websites might be incompatible with Safari. Try updating Safari by installing the latest macOS/iOS update (Fix 1d). You can also report the issue to Apple: in Safari, click the **Page Menu button** (the icon on the left side of the address bar that looks like a rectangle with lines) and choose **"Report a Website Issue."**

- **Safari was working fine until a recent macOS or iOS update?** Updates sometimes introduce bugs that Apple fixes in follow-up releases. Check r/Safari on Reddit or the Apple Support Communities to see if others are reporting the same issue. If it's a known bug, Apple typically releases a fix within days or weeks.

- **Safari shows "Safari Can't Open the Page because it can't establish a secure connection"?** This often means the website's security certificate has expired, or your device's date and time are wrong. Check your date and time: **System Settings > General > Date & Time** (Mac) or **Settings > General > Date & Time** (iPhone/iPad). Make sure "Set automatically" is turned ON.

- **Tried everything and Safari still won't work?** As a temporary workaround while you wait for a fix, download an alternative browser like Google Chrome, Firefox, or Brave from their official websites. They work independently of Safari and won't be affected by Safari-specific issues. Many people use an alternative browser for a few days until an Apple update resolves the Safari problem.
