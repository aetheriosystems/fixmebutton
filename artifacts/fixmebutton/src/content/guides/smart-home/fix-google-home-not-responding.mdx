## Before You Start

You say "Hey Google" and nothing happens — no glowing dots, no voice response, no action. Or maybe the four colored dots spin for a moment, then your Nest Hub goes silent. It's frustrating when the smart speaker you rely on for timers, music, weather, and smart home control suddenly stops listening.

The good news: Google Home and Nest devices are reliable hardware, and most "not responding" problems come down to a muted microphone, a flaky WiFi connection, or a software glitch that clears up with a restart. This guide walks you through the fixes in order, from the 10-second checks to the nuclear option. Most people solve the problem by Step 2.

**What you'll need:**
- Your Google Home or Nest device
- The Google Home app on your phone (free from the App Store or Google Play)
- Your WiFi password (you might need to reconnect)
- About 10 minutes

> **Work through the steps in order.** The first two steps fix the problem for the vast majority of people. Save the factory reset (Step 5) for last — it erases all your settings.

## Step 1: Check the Microphone Switch and Quick Basics

Before you dive into deeper fixes, check the three things that people overlook constantly. These take 30 seconds and solve a surprising number of cases.

**Fix 1 — Is the microphone muted?**

Every Google Home and Nest device has a physical microphone switch. If the mic is muted, the device CANNOT hear you — no matter how loudly you say "Hey Google."

1. Look at your device. If you see **four orange/amber LED dots** on top of a speaker, or an **orange light** on the back of a Nest Hub — the microphone is muted.
2. Find the physical microphone switch:
   - **Nest Hub and Nest Hub Max:** On the back of the screen, near the top edge, there's a sliding switch. Slide it so the orange color is NOT visible — that means the mic is ON.
   - **Nest Audio:** On the back, near the power cord, there's a switch. Flip it so you don't see orange.
   - **Nest Mini (2nd gen) and Google Home Mini (1st gen):** On the side, there's a sliding switch. Slide it so orange is hidden.
   - **Google Home (original):** On the back, press the microphone mute button. The LED ring on top will show orange when muted.
3. When you unmute, the device will announce "The mic is on." If you hear that, you know the speaker is working — try "Hey Google" again.
4. When you mute, it says "The mic is muted." Make sure you DON'T hear that when you try to use it.

> **Common mistake:** People bump the microphone switch while cleaning, dusting, or moving the device. On the Nest Hub, the switch is on the back — easy to accidentally slide when you're adjusting the screen angle. Always check the mic switch FIRST. If you see ANY orange light on your device, the mic is off and that's your problem.

**Fix 2 — Is "Hey Google" the right wake word?**

Unlike Alexa, Google devices only respond to "Hey Google" or "Ok Google" — but there's a setting that can affect this.

1. Open the **Google Home app** on your phone.
2. Tap **Devices** at the bottom (or find your device on the home screen).
3. Touch and hold your device's tile, then tap the **Settings gear** ⚙️ in the top right.
4. Tap **Audio** → **"Hey Google" sensitivity**.
5. Make sure the sensitivity isn't turned all the way down. If it is, the device basically ignores the wake word. Move the slider to the middle or higher.
6. If your device supports it, you can also check that **Voice Match** is set up under **Recognition & sharing** — Voice Match lets your device recognize YOUR voice specifically.

> **Common mistake:** People don't realize Google devices have a sensitivity setting for "Hey Google." If someone in your household accidentally set sensitivity to the lowest level (or a guest was playing with settings), the device will almost never respond to the wake word. This setting is buried under Audio settings and most people never find it.

**Fix 3 — Is the volume set too low to hear the response?**

Google might be responding — you just can't hear it.

1. Say "Hey Google, set volume to 50%" to set a middle volume level.
2. Or use the physical volume buttons: on Nest Hub, they're on the top-right edge of the screen. On speakers, tap the volume up area on top.
3. The LED dots on the speaker should light up white to show the volume change.

## Step 2: Power Cycle Your Google Device

A power cycle — unplugging and plugging back in — clears temporary software glitches that build up over days or weeks of uptime. This fixes the problem for most people.

**Fix 1 — Unplug, wait, and plug back in**

1. Unplug the device from the **wall outlet** — not just from the back of the device. The power adapter can hold a small charge.
2. Wait **30 full seconds**. Count slowly — don't rush.
3. Plug it back into the wall outlet.
4. On a Nest Hub, the screen will show the Google "G" logo and then boot up (about 45-60 seconds).
5. On a speaker, the LED lights will pulse white during startup, then go dark when ready.
6. Once the device is fully booted (give it a full minute), try "Hey Google."

**Fix 2 — Restart your WiFi router too**

If your Google device lost its WiFi connection, power cycling the router at the same time helps both devices reconnect cleanly.

1. Unplug your **Google/Nest device** from the wall.
2. Unplug your **WiFi router** from the wall.
3. Wait 30 seconds.
4. Plug the router back in and wait **2 full minutes** for it to fully restart.
5. Now plug your Google device back in.
6. Wait for it to boot up and reconnect (up to 2 minutes — the device will announce when it's reconnected, or the Nest Hub screen will show the home screen).

> **Common mistake:** People unplug the cable from the back of the device but leave the power adapter plugged into the wall. The power brick holds a small residual charge — not much, but enough that a 2-second disconnect doesn't fully reset the device. Always unplug from the WALL OUTLET. Also, do NOT just flip the power strip switch — many smart power strips have surge protection capacitors that hold charge. Physically pull the plug.

## Step 3: Fix Your WiFi Connection

Google Home and Nest devices need a stable internet connection for almost everything — even basic commands like setting a timer rely on Google's cloud servers. If your WiFi is weak or the device disconnected, it won't respond.

**Fix 1 — Check if the device is online**

1. Open the **Google Home app** on your phone.
2. Find your device on the home screen or tap **Devices** at the bottom.
3. If the device shows **"Offline"** or has a greyed-out tile, it's not connected to WiFi.
4. If the device responds to commands through the Google Home app (tap the device and use the on-screen controls), but NOT to voice, the WiFi is fine — the issue is with the microphone or wake word. Go back to Step 1.
5. If the device IS offline, move to the next fix.

**Fix 2 — Move the device closer to the router**

WiFi weakens through walls, floors, and distance. A device on the edge of its range will connect intermittently or not at all.

1. Temporarily move the Google device to the **same room** as your WiFi router.
2. Power cycle it (unplug 30 seconds, plug back in).
3. If it works in that room, the original spot has a weak WiFi signal.
4. Permanent solutions: move the device to a better spot, move the router to a more central location, or get a mesh WiFi system / WiFi extender (starting around $25).

**Fix 3 — Reconnect to WiFi through the Google Home app**

If your Google device lost its WiFi settings (happens after router changes, password changes, or power outages):

1. Open the **Google Home app**.
2. When the app detects an offline device, it usually shows a prompt to help you reconnect. Tap the prompt.
3. If no prompt appears: tap **Devices** → find the offline device → tap its tile → it should show "Device is offline" with a reconnect option.
4. The app will guide you through reconnecting — your device will broadcast a temporary setup signal.
5. Select your WiFi network and enter the password.
6. **Important:** Most Google Home and Nest devices only support **2.4 GHz WiFi**. If your router has both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz networks with the same name (combined SSID), the device should connect automatically. But if they're separate networks with different names, make sure you connect to the 2.4 GHz one.

> **Common mistake:** If you recently changed your WiFi password or got a new router, your Google device is still trying to connect with the OLD credentials. Every smart device in your house needs to be updated individually. Go into the Google Home app and reconnect each device. Also, Google Nest devices do NOT support 5 GHz WiFi — only 2.4 GHz. If you set up a new router and disabled 2.4 GHz or renamed only the 2.4 GHz band, your Google devices can't connect at all.

## Step 4: Check the Google Home App and Account Settings

Sometimes the problem isn't the hardware — it's a setting in the app, a linked account issue, or a software update that needs to finish.

**Fix 1 — Make sure the device is linked to the right Google Account**

Your Google device responds to the account it was set up with. If it's on the wrong account or was set up by someone else, it won't respond to your voice commands properly.

1. Open the **Google Home app**.
2. Tap your **profile picture or initial** in the top right corner.
3. Check which Google account is signed in — this should be YOUR primary account.
4. Go back to the home screen and find your device. If the device was set up under a DIFFERENT account, you won't see it here.
5. If the device was set up by a family member or previous owner, they need to remove it from their Google Home app, or you need to factory reset it (Step 5) and set it up fresh on your account.

**Fix 2 — Check for and install software updates**

Google devices update automatically when idle, but sometimes an update gets stuck.

1. Open the **Google Home app**.
2. Touch and hold your device's tile → tap **Settings** ⚙️ → **Device information**.
3. Look for **Software version** or **Cast firmware version**. Note the current version.
4. There's no manual "Update now" button — but you can force an update check by rebooting the device (Step 2) and leaving it idle for 15–30 minutes.
5. Make sure the device is plugged in, connected to WiFi, and NOT playing music or in use. The update happens during idle time.

**Fix 3 — Check Google's service status**

Sometimes the problem isn't your device at all — it's a Google server outage.

1. Go to the [Google Workspace Status Dashboard](https://www.google.com/appsstatus) or search "Is Google Home down" on your phone.
2. Check sites like [DownDetector](https://downdetector.com) and search "Google Home."
3. If you see a spike in outage reports, Google's servers are having issues — wait it out. No amount of resetting your device will fix a server-side problem.

> **Common mistake:** People spend an hour factory resetting devices and reinstalling apps because Google Assistant isn't responding — only to discover Google is having a widespread outage. Always check DownDetector BEFORE you factory reset anything. It takes 30 seconds and saves you an hour of unnecessary troubleshooting.

## Step 5: Factory Reset the Device

If nothing above worked, a factory reset wipes everything and returns the device to its out-of-the-box state. This is also required for used devices that are still linked to the previous owner's account.

> **⚠️ WARNING: A factory reset erases ALL your settings — WiFi, linked accounts, Voice Match, smart home connections, routines, alarms, and speaker groups. You will have to set the device up from scratch like it's brand new. Only do this as a last resort.**

**For Nest Hub and Nest Hub Max (devices with screens):**

1. Make sure the device is plugged in.
2. On the back of the Nest Hub, find both **volume buttons** (they're at the top-right edge on the back).
3. Press and **hold both volume buttons at the same time** for about **10 seconds**.
4. The screen will show a warning and countdown. KEEP HOLDING until the countdown finishes.
5. The device will announce it's resetting and restart. The screen will show the Google "G" logo.
6. Once it boots up, the screen will say "Get the Google Home app" — it's ready for setup.
7. Open the Google Home app → tap the **+** button → **Set up device** → **New device** and follow the instructions.

**For Nest Mini, Google Home Mini, Nest Audio, and Google Home (speakers without screens):**

1. Make sure the device is plugged in.
2. First, **turn the microphone OFF** using the physical switch. The LED lights will turn orange.
3. Now press and **hold the top center** of the device (on Nest Audio and Nest Mini, it's the fabric-covered area near the top; on the original Google Home, it's the flat top surface).
4. Keep holding. After about **5 seconds**, the LED lights will start flashing orange. CONTINUE holding.
5. After about **10–15 seconds total**, the device will announce it's resetting, and the lights will go through a sequence.
6. Release the top. The device will restart and enter setup mode.
7. Open the Google Home app → **+** → **Set up device** → **New device** and follow the instructions.

> **Common mistake:** Not holding the buttons/top long enough. On the Nest Hub, people press both volume buttons for 3-5 seconds, see the countdown, and release — but the countdown has to finish, which takes 10 full seconds. On speakers, people skip the "mute first" step. You MUST mute the microphone before holding the top, or the factory reset won't trigger. If you're just holding and nothing is happening, check that the mic is muted (orange lights = muted).

**After the factory reset:**
- Set up the device in the Google Home app like it's brand new.
- Connect it to your WiFi.
- Link it to your Google Account.
- Set up Voice Match (if you want personalized responses).
- Add it back to any speaker groups you had.

## Still Stuck?

- **Device works right after a restart but stops responding again later?** Your device may be on the edge of your WiFi range. Even if it connects initially, a weak signal causes it to drop off over time. Try moving it closer to the router or add a WiFi extender.

- **Other Google devices in the house work fine, just this one?** Try swapping locations between the broken device and a working one. If the broken device still doesn't work in the new spot, the device itself may be failing. If the working device now has problems in the old spot, that location has a WiFi dead zone.

- **Google Assistant responds but gives wrong answers or makes errors?** This is usually a WiFi speed issue — voice processing happens in the cloud and needs a stable connection. Run a speed test on your phone near the device (go to speedtest.net). You need at least 1 Mbps for basic commands and 3-5 Mbps for music streaming.

- **Nest Hub screen works but voice commands don't?** If the touchscreen responds but "Hey Google" doesn't, the microphone is almost certainly muted. Check the physical switch on the back. If it's NOT muted, the microphone hardware may have failed — especially common on older Nest Hubs (1st gen) that are 4+ years old.

- **Device says "Something went wrong, try again in a few seconds"?** This is almost always a temporary Google server issue. Wait 5–10 minutes and try again. If it persists for hours, check DownDetector for Google Home outages.

- **Bought a used Google device that won't respond?** The device is almost certainly still linked to the previous owner's account. You MUST factory reset it (Step 5) and set it up fresh on your own Google Account. A device still linked to someone else's account will ignore your commands entirely.

- **Still nothing after a factory reset?** The device may have a hardware failure — most commonly the microphone array, WiFi chip, or power system. If it's more than 3-4 years old, replacement may be more cost-effective than repair. A new Nest Mini costs around $30-40 when on sale.
