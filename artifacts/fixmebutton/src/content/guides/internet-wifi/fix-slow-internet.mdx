## Before You Start

Slow internet can be caused by many things — your router placement, too many devices connected, or even your internet provider. This guide walks you through the most common fixes, from easiest to most involved.

**What you'll need:**
- Access to your WiFi router
- Your WiFi password (usually printed on a sticker on the router)

## Step 1: Run a Speed Test

First, let's find out how slow your internet actually is. This gives you a baseline to compare against after trying fixes.

Open a web browser and go to **speedtest.net**. Tap or click the big "Go" button. Wait about 30 seconds for the test to complete.

Write down your **download speed** (the bigger number) and **upload speed** (the smaller number). These are measured in Mbps.

> **What's normal?** For streaming Netflix: 5-25 Mbps. For video calls: 3-10 Mbps. For browsing: 1-5 Mbps. If your number is below these, something's wrong.

## Step 2: Restart Your Router and Modem

This fixes more internet problems than anything else. It clears temporary glitches and refreshes your connection.

1. Unplug the power cable from both your **router** and **modem** (they might be the same box, or two separate boxes)
2. Wait 60 seconds — really wait, don't skip this
3. Plug the **modem** back in first (if they're separate). Wait for all the lights to stop blinking
4. Plug the **router** back in. Wait 2 minutes for it to fully restart
5. Run another speed test at speedtest.net. Did it improve?

## Step 3: Move Closer to Your Router

WiFi gets weaker the farther you are from the router, and walls/floors block the signal.

- Move your device to the same room as the router and test again
- If the speed improves dramatically, your issue is **router placement**
- For a permanent fix: place the router in a central, elevated location — not in a closet, not on the floor, not behind the TV

## Step 4: Disconnect Unused Devices

Every device connected to your WiFi takes a slice of your internet speed. Devices you forgot about — old phones, smart speakers, security cameras — might be hogging bandwidth.

1. Look at your router (or its app) to see what's connected
2. Disconnect anything you're not actively using
3. Run the speed test again

## Step 5: Switch to 5 GHz WiFi

Most modern routers broadcast two networks: **2.4 GHz** (slower, longer range) and **5 GHz** (faster, shorter range).

1. On your device, open WiFi settings
2. Look for your network name followed by "5G" or "5GHz" — for example, "MyNetwork-5G"
3. Connect to that one instead
4. Run the speed test. The 5 GHz network is almost always faster if you're close enough to the router.

## Step 6: Check for Background Downloads

Your computer or phone might be downloading updates, syncing photos, or backing up files without you knowing.

- **Windows**: Open Task Manager (Ctrl+Shift+Esc) → Processes tab → sort by Network
- **Mac**: Open Activity Monitor (search in Spotlight) → Network tab
- **iPhone/Android**: Check App Store / Google Play for active downloads

If something is using a lot of network, pause or cancel it and test again.

## Step 7: Update Your Router's Firmware

Router manufacturers release updates that can fix bugs and improve performance.

1. Find your router's brand and model number (printed on the bottom or back)
2. Open a browser and type your router's address — usually **192.168.0.1** or **192.168.1.1**
3. Log in. The default username and password are often "admin" and "password" (check the sticker on your router)
4. Look for a section called "Firmware Update," "Router Update," or "Administration"
5. Follow the on-screen instructions to check for and install updates

> **Warning:** Don't unplug the router during a firmware update. It'll restart on its own when done.

## Step 8: Contact Your Internet Provider

If none of the above helped, the problem might be on your provider's end.

1. Call your internet provider's support number (Comcast/Xfinity, Spectrum, AT&T, Verizon, etc.)
2. Tell them: your download speed, what you've tried already (restarting the router, etc.), and when the problem started
3. Ask them to: run a line test, check for outages in your area, and confirm you're getting the speed you're paying for

They can often fix problems on their end or send a technician if needed.

## Still Stuck?

If your internet is still slow after all these steps:

- Consider a **mesh WiFi system** (like Google WiFi, Eero, or TP-Link Deco) — these use multiple units to blanket your home in strong WiFi
- Try an **Ethernet cable** directly from your router to your computer. If wired is fast but wireless is slow, the issue is WiFi interference
- Check if your internet plan is fast enough. If you're paying for 25 Mbps, that might simply not be enough for a family with multiple devices streaming at once
