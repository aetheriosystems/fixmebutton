## Before You Start

You're expecting an important email — a password reset, a receipt, a message from a friend — but it never shows up in your Gmail inbox. People tell you they sent it, but you can't find it anywhere. This is one of the most common Gmail problems, and in most cases, the emails aren't actually lost — they're just hidden, blocked, or being turned away because your account storage is full.

This guide walks you through the five most common reasons Gmail stops receiving emails, ordered from quickest fix to more involved solutions. Most people solve this by Step 1 or Step 2.

**What you'll need:**
- Your Gmail email address and password
- A phone or computer with internet access
- About 15 minutes

> **Work through the steps in order.** Each step fixes the problem for a large number of people — you probably won't need all five. But if one doesn't work, move to the next.

## Step 1: Check Your Spam, Trash, and All Mail Folders

Gmail automatically sorts incoming messages. Sometimes it puts real emails in the wrong folder — especially messages from new senders, emails with attachments, or messages that look similar to ones you've deleted before. Before changing any settings, make sure the missing email isn't just sitting in another folder.

**Fix 1 — Check the Spam folder**

1. Open Gmail on your phone or computer
2. On a **computer**: look at the left sidebar. If you don't see **Spam**, click **More** at the bottom of the sidebar to expand hidden folders, then click **Spam**
3. On an **iPhone or Android**: tap the **☰ (three horizontal lines)** menu icon in the top-left corner, then scroll down and tap **Spam**
4. Look through the list for the missing email
5. If you find it, open the email and click or tap **Report not spam** (this moves it to your inbox and teaches Gmail not to mark future messages from this sender as spam)

> **Tip:** Gmail automatically deletes emails that have been in Spam for more than 30 days. If the email was sent more than a month ago, it may be gone for good.

**Fix 2 — Check the Trash folder**

1. In the left sidebar (computer) or under the **☰** menu (phone), find and open **Trash** (sometimes called **Bin**)
2. Look for the missing email
3. If you find it, open the email and click or tap **Move to inbox** (the arrow icon pointing into a tray)
4. If the email isn't there either, try Fix 3

**Fix 3 — Check All Mail**

Sometimes emails get "archived" by accident — a single swipe on a phone can archive an email without you noticing. Archived emails disappear from your inbox but are NOT deleted. They live in a folder called All Mail.

1. On a **computer**: click **All Mail** in the left sidebar (if you don't see it, click **More** first)
2. On an **iPhone or Android**: tap **☰** → scroll down and tap **All Mail**
3. Search for the email using the search bar at the top. Try searching by the sender's name or a word you know was in the subject line
4. If you find it, open the email and click or tap **Move to inbox** to bring it back

![Gmail left sidebar showing Spam, Trash, and All Mail folders highlighted](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People search their inbox using Gmail's search bar and find nothing, then assume the email was never received. The search bar at the top of Gmail searches ALL folders by default — but if you're viewing a specific folder (like Inbox), results only show emails in that folder. Always check **All Mail** or use the dropdown arrow in the search bar and make sure "All Mail" is selected under "Search."

## Step 2: Free Up Your Google Storage Space

This is the **number one reason** Gmail stops receiving emails. Every Google account comes with 15 GB of free storage shared across Gmail, Google Drive, and Google Photos. When that storage fills up, Gmail cannot accept new messages — senders will get a "bounce back" error saying the email couldn't be delivered, but YOU won't see any warning.

Emails with attachments (PDFs, photos, videos, documents) are the biggest storage eaters. An email with a 10 MB attachment takes up 10 MB of your Google storage.

**Fix 4 — Check your storage and clear space**

**On a computer:**

1. Go to **drive.google.com** and look at the storage bar at the bottom of the left sidebar. It shows how much of your 15 GB is used
2. If the bar is red or nearly full, click it to open your **Storage details** page
3. You'll see what's using your space. Click **Gmail** under "Clean up by service"
4. You'll see a list of emails with large attachments, sorted by size. Check the box next to emails you don't need and click **Delete** (the trash icon)
5. After deleting, go to Gmail → **Trash** in the left sidebar → click **Empty Trash now** at the top. Deleted emails stay in Trash for 30 days and still count toward your storage until you permanently delete them
6. Wait 5-10 minutes, then ask someone to send you a test email to confirm it works

**On an iPhone or Android:**

1. Open the **Gmail app** and tap your **profile picture** (top-right corner)
2. Tap **Manage your Google Account** → scroll to the **Storage** section and tap **Manage storage**
3. Tap **Clean up space** → under "Clean up by service," tap **Gmail**
4. Select emails with large attachments by tapping the checkbox next to each one, then tap the **trash icon (🗑️)** at the top
5. Go back to Gmail → tap **☰** → **Trash** → tap **Empty Trash now**
6. Wait 5-10 minutes and have someone send you a test email

![Google storage management page showing the Gmail cleanup section with large attachments listed](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People delete thousands of emails to free up space but forget to **empty the Trash**. Gmail keeps deleted emails in Trash for 30 days, and they still count against your storage during that time. If you delete 2 GB of emails but don't empty Trash, your storage doesn't go down at all. Always finish with "Empty Trash now."

> **Warning:** Deleting emails from Trash is permanent. Once you empty Trash, those emails are gone forever and cannot be recovered — even by Google support. Double-check that you're not deleting anything important before you empty.

## Step 3: Check Your Gmail Filters and Blocked Addresses

Gmail lets you create "filters" — automatic rules that tell Gmail what to do with incoming emails (skip the inbox, delete them, apply a label, forward them, etc.). Sometimes a filter you created long ago — or one that was set up accidentally — is hiding or deleting your incoming emails without you realizing it.

You might also have accidentally **blocked** the sender. When you block someone in Gmail, their emails go straight to Spam.

**Fix 5 — Review and clean up your filters**

**On a computer (filters can only be managed from a computer or desktop mode):**

1. Open Gmail and click the **gear icon (⚙)** in the top-right corner → click **See all settings**
2. Click the **Filters and Blocked Addresses** tab at the top
3. You'll see a list of all your active filters. Read through each one carefully
4. Look for any filter that includes the words **"Delete it"** or **"Skip the Inbox (Archive it)"** — these are the most likely culprits
5. To remove a bad filter: click **delete** (the trash icon) next to it, then click **OK** to confirm
6. To edit a filter instead: click **edit** next to the filter, change the action, and click **Update filter**

**Check for blocked addresses:**

1. In the same **Filters and Blocked Addresses** tab, scroll down past the filter list
2. You'll see a section called **"The following email addresses are blocked"**
3. If the person you're expecting email from is in this list, click **Unblock** next to their address
4. After unblocking, ask them to resend the email — blocked messages that were already sent cannot be recovered

![Gmail settings showing the Filters and Blocked Addresses tab with a "Delete it" filter highlighted](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People create filters to organize their inbox (like "all emails from this store go to a Shopping label") and forget about them. Months later, they wonder why emails are missing. Filters don't send you a notification when they act on an email — they just silently do their job. Review your filters every few months, especially if you suddenly stop getting emails from a particular sender.

> **Tip:** If you can't log into Gmail on a computer, open Gmail in your phone's web browser (Chrome or Safari). In the browser's menu, tap **Request Desktop Site** — this lets you access the full Gmail settings page, including filters, from your phone.

## Step 4: Turn Off Email Forwarding

Gmail can automatically forward your incoming emails to another email address. This is useful if you want all your Gmail to show up in your work email, for example. But if forwarding is turned on by accident — or if it was set up by someone else who had access to your account — your emails may be arriving and immediately leaving to another inbox. You'd never know because they never land in your Gmail.

**Fix 6 — Disable email forwarding**

1. On a **computer**, open Gmail and click the **gear icon (⚙)** → **See all settings**
2. Click the **Forwarding and POP/IMAP** tab at the top
3. At the top of this tab, look for the **Forwarding** section
4. If it says **"Forward a copy of incoming mail to [some email address]"**, that's your problem. Select **Disable forwarding** instead
5. Scroll to the bottom of the page and click **Save Changes**
6. Ask someone to send you a test email to confirm the fix worked

**Also check your POP/IMAP settings (on the same tab):**

While you're on this tab, scroll down and make sure these are enabled:
- Under **POP Download**: select **Enable POP for all mail** (or "Enable POP for mail that arrives from now on" if you prefer)
- Under **IMAP Access**: select **Enable IMAP**
- Click **Save Changes**

POP and IMAP are the technologies that let email apps (like Apple Mail, Outlook, or your phone's built-in mail app) read your Gmail. If these are disabled, third-party email apps can't fetch your messages — and in some cases, this can interfere with message delivery.

![Gmail settings Forwarding and POP/IMAP tab showing the Disable forwarding option](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People see the **Forwarding and POP/IMAP** tab and assume it's only for advanced users, so they skip it. But forwarding is a single toggle that anyone could have turned on by accident while exploring settings. Always check this tab — it takes 20 seconds and has solved the problem for thousands of Gmail users. Also check that the forwarding address isn't one you don't recognize — if it is, your account may have been accessed by someone else. Change your password immediately.

> **Warning:** If you see a forwarding address you don't recognize, your account may be compromised. After disabling forwarding, go to **Settings → See all settings → Accounts and Import** → **Change password** and update your password right away. Also check **Google Account Security** (myaccount.google.com/security) for any unfamiliar devices signed into your account.

## Step 5: Sign Out and Sign Back In (Plus Update the App)

Sometimes Gmail's connection to Google's servers gets stuck — especially on phones. Your app shows old emails but can't pull in new ones. Signing out forces a fresh sync, and updating the app fixes bugs that cause syncing to break.

**Fix 7 — Sign out and back in on a computer**

1. In Gmail, click your **profile picture** (top-right corner)
2. Click **Sign out** (or **Sign out of all accounts** if you have multiple Google accounts signed in)
3. Close your browser completely, then reopen it
4. Go to **gmail.com** and sign back in with your email and password
5. Check if new emails appear

**Fix 8 — Remove and re-add your account on iPhone or Android**

On a phone, you can't just "sign out" of a Google account from the Gmail app — you have to remove the entire account from your device and add it back. Don't worry, your emails aren't deleted when you do this.

**iPhone:**
1. Open the Gmail app and tap your **profile picture** (top-right) → tap **Manage accounts on this device**
2. Tap the **switch (toggle)** next to your account to turn it off → tap **Done**
3. Now tap your profile picture again → tap **Add another account** → select **Google**
4. Sign in with your email and password. Gmail will resync all your emails from Google's servers

**Android:**
1. Open your phone's **Settings** app → **Accounts** (or **Accounts and backup** → **Manage accounts**)
2. Tap your Google account (the one with the Gmail address)
3. Tap **Remove account** → confirm by tapping **Remove account** again
4. Go back to **Settings** → **Accounts** → **Add account** → select **Google**
5. Sign in with your email and password. Open the Gmail app and let it sync

**Fix 9 — Update the Gmail app (phone only)**

An outdated version of the Gmail app can have bugs that stop new emails from appearing:

1. **iPhone:** Open the **App Store** → tap your profile picture (top-right) → scroll down and look for Gmail under available updates → tap **Update**. If you don't see it, search for "Gmail" in the App Store and tap **Update**
2. **Android:** Open the **Google Play Store** → tap your profile picture (top-right) → tap **Manage apps & device** → tap **Updates available** → find Gmail and tap **Update**

After updating, open Gmail and pull down from the top of the screen to manually refresh your inbox.

![Gmail app showing the profile picture menu with Manage accounts option](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** People remove their Google account from their phone and panic, thinking all their emails will be deleted. Your emails live on Google's servers, not on your phone. Removing the account from your device only removes the local copy — when you sign back in, Gmail downloads everything again from the cloud. You won't lose a single email.

> **Tip:** On Android, removing your Google account also signs you out of other Google apps (YouTube, Google Maps, Google Drive). You'll need to sign back into those too after re-adding your account. If that sounds annoying, try Fix 9 (updating the app) first — it's faster and often fixes the same sync problems.

## Still Stuck?

- **Tried everything and still not receiving emails from ONE specific sender?** Ask them to check if THEY received a "bounce back" error message. If your storage was full or your account had a problem, Gmail would have sent an error to the sender. That error message often contains the exact reason the email couldn't be delivered.
- **Emails arrive hours or days late?** This is different from "not receiving at all." Check your internet connection — Gmail needs a stable connection to sync in real time. On a phone, make sure background data isn't restricted for Gmail: **Settings → Apps → Gmail → Mobile data & Wi-Fi → enable Background data**. Also check that **Battery optimization** isn't set to "Optimized" for Gmail — this can pause syncing to save battery.
- **Using a third-party email app (Apple Mail, Outlook, Thunderbird) instead of the Gmail app?** Make sure POP/IMAP is enabled (see Step 4). Then remove and re-add your Gmail account in the third-party app. In Apple Mail on iPhone, go to **Settings → Mail → Accounts → your Gmail account → Delete Account**, then add it back.
- **Think your account may have been hacked?** Go to **myaccount.google.com/security** and check the "Your devices" section for anything you don't recognize. Also check "Recent security activity" for logins from unfamiliar locations. If you see anything suspicious, use the **Security Checkup** tool on that page to secure your account.
- **Storage is full but you don't want to delete anything?** You can buy more Google storage (Google One) starting at $1.99/month for 100 GB. Go to **one.google.com** to see plans. This instantly fixes the "not receiving emails" problem without deleting anything.
- **Nothing works and the email is urgent?** Try using a different email address entirely — create a free account at Gmail, Yahoo, or Outlook — and ask the sender to resend the email to that new address. This is a quick workaround while you continue troubleshooting your main account.
