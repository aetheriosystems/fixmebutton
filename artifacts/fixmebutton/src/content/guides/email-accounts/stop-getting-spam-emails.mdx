## Before You Start

You open your inbox and it's drowning in junk. Dozens — maybe hundreds — of emails you never asked for: "You've Won!", "Claim Your Prize!", "Your Package Is Waiting," newsletters from companies you don't remember signing up for, and sketchy messages from strangers. Your important emails are buried under a mountain of spam.

You're not alone. Over 45% of all email sent worldwide is spam. But you don't have to just accept it. This guide shows you how to clean up your current inbox AND how to stop new spam from arriving. Think of it like cleaning a messy room: first you throw out the trash (Steps 1-2), then you set up systems to keep it clean (Steps 3-5).

**What you'll need:**
- Access to your email account (on a computer is easiest)
- About 15 minutes
- Patience — you won't fix this overnight, but each step makes a big difference

> **Work through the steps in order.** Steps 1-3 give you immediate relief. Steps 4-5 prevent the problem from coming back. Do them all for the best result.

## Step 1: Unsubscribe from Legitimate Newsletters and Mailing Lists

Not all spam is malicious. A lot of inbox clutter comes from real companies you gave your email to — stores you shopped at, apps you signed up for, websites you created an account on. These companies send newsletters, promotions, and "weekly deals" that clutter your inbox. The good news: legitimate companies are required by law (in most countries) to include an Unsubscribe link.

**Fix 1 — Unsubscribe using the built-in tools**

**In Gmail (on a computer):**

1. Open Gmail and find a newsletter or promotional email you don't want anymore
2. Look for the **Unsubscribe** link. Gmail often shows a blue **"Unsubscribe"** button at the very top of the email, right next to the sender's name. Click it
3. Click **Unsubscribe** again in the popup to confirm
4. Gmail will unsubscribe you from that sender's list. Future emails from them should stop within a few days

**In Yahoo Mail:**

1. Open the unwanted email
2. Look for the **Unsubscribe** link, usually at the bottom of the email in small print. Click it
3. Follow the instructions on the sender's website (usually just clicking "Confirm Unsubscribe")

**In Outlook/Hotmail:**

1. Open the unwanted email
2. Look at the top of the email. If Outlook detects an unsubscribe option, you'll see a banner that says **"Unsubscribe"** — click it
3. If there's no banner, scroll to the bottom of the email and look for the **Unsubscribe** link in small text

**On iPhone or Android:**

1. Open the unwanted email in your mail app
2. Scroll all the way to the bottom — past the signature, past the social media icons, past the legal fine print
3. Look for a link that says **"Unsubscribe,"** **"Manage Preferences,"** or **"Update your email preferences"**
4. Tap the link. It opens a webpage where you can unsubscribe or choose to receive fewer emails

> **Common mistake:** People mark newsletters as "Spam" or "Junk" instead of unsubscribing. This hurts YOU more than the sender. When you mark a legitimate company's email as spam, your email provider learns that emails from that company are unwanted — which can cause emails you DO want from them (order confirmations, receipts, password resets) to also go to spam. Always try Unsubscribe first. Only mark as spam if there's no Unsubscribe option.

> **Warning:** Never click Unsubscribe links in emails that look suspicious or are from senders you don't recognize at all (e.g., "freeprize123@random-site.xyz"). Scammers use fake Unsubscribe links to confirm your email address is active, which leads to MORE spam. Only unsubscribe from companies you recognize and have actually interacted with.

## Step 2: Block Persistent Senders

Some senders ignore unsubscribe requests, or their emails are straight-up spam with no working unsubscribe link. For these, use your email provider's Block feature. Blocking sends all future emails from that sender directly to your Spam folder — you'll never see them in your inbox again.

**Fix 2 — Block senders in Gmail**

1. Open the unwanted email
2. Click the **three vertical dots (⋮)** in the top-right corner of the email (next to the reply arrow)
3. Click **Block "[sender name]"** from the dropdown menu
4. Gmail asks for confirmation: click **Block** again
5. All future emails from this sender go straight to Spam. Past emails from them stay in your inbox unless you delete them manually

**Fix 3 — Block senders in Yahoo Mail**

1. Open the unwanted email
2. Click the **three horizontal dots (⋯)** at the top of the email
3. Click **Block senders** from the menu
4. Click **OK** to confirm
5. Future emails from this address are automatically sent to Spam

**Fix 4 — Block senders in Outlook/Hotmail**

1. Open the unwanted email
2. Click the **three horizontal dots (⋯)** in the top toolbar
3. Click **Block** → **Block sender**
4. Click **OK** to confirm
5. The sender is added to your blocked list. Future emails go to Junk Email

**Fix 5 — Block senders on iPhone Mail app**

1. Open the unwanted email
2. Tap the sender's name at the top of the email
3. On the contact card that appears, tap **Block this Contact**
4. Confirm by tapping **Block this Contact** again

> **Tip:** Blocking works best for spam that comes from the SAME email address repeatedly. If spam keeps coming from different addresses (e.g., spam123@abc.com, spam456@abc.com), blocking individual addresses won't help much. In that case, move to Step 3 (Filtering) or report it as spam so your provider's automated filters learn to catch it.

## Step 3: Report Spam to Train Your Email Filter

Every major email provider uses automated spam filters — computer programs that analyze incoming emails and decide whether they're spam. These filters learn from what YOU mark as spam. The more you use the "Report Spam" button correctly, the better your filter gets at catching junk before it reaches your inbox.

**Fix 6 — Report spam (not just delete it)**

1. In your inbox, **select the checkbox** next to each spam email (on a computer) or **tap and hold** the email (on a phone)
2. **Do NOT just delete the emails.** Deleting doesn't teach the filter anything — it just removes the email from your view
3. Instead, click or tap the **Report Spam** button:
   - **Gmail:** The **!** (exclamation mark) icon, or the **"Report spam"** button at the top
   - **Yahoo Mail:** The **shield icon (🛡️)** or **"Spam"** button
   - **Outlook:** The **"Junk"** dropdown → **"Junk"** (or **"Report as junk"**)
   - **iPhone Mail:** Tap the **flag icon (🚩)** at the bottom → **Move to Junk**
4. The email moves to your Spam/Junk folder, AND your provider's filter learns that similar emails are unwanted
5. Do this for ALL obvious spam in your inbox — batch-select multiple emails at once to save time

> **Common mistake:** People delete spam emails without reporting them first. This is like sweeping dirt under a rug — the dirt is still there, and more keeps coming. Reporting spam teaches the filter to catch it automatically next time. Spend 30 seconds reporting before you delete, and you'll see less spam within a week.

## Step 4: Create Filters to Automatically Sort and Delete Spam

Filters (also called "Rules") are automatic instructions that tell your email provider what to do with certain messages. You can create filters that automatically move spam to the trash, label it, or delete it — without you ever seeing it.

**Fix 7 — Create a filter in Gmail**

Gmail's filters are the most powerful free spam-fighting tool available. Here's how to set one up:

1. On a **computer**, open Gmail and click the **search bar** at the top (don't type anything yet — just click it)
2. Click the **slider icon (⚙)** on the right side of the search bar to open "Advanced search"
3. Fill in the fields that match your spam. For example:
   - **From:** Enter a common spam word like `@` followed by a suspicious domain (e.g., `@spam-offers.xyz`)
   - **Subject:** Enter common spam phrases like `"You have won"` or `"Claim your prize"`
   - **Has the words:** Enter words that always appear in the spam you get (e.g., `"free trial" "act now" "limited time"`)
   - **Doesn't have:** If you want to be safe, exclude words from real emails (e.g., enter your friends' email addresses or the name of your workplace)
4. Click **Create filter** (blue button at the bottom of the advanced search form)
5. Now choose what Gmail should do with matching emails:
   - ✅ Check **"Delete it"** — this sends matching emails straight to Trash. Use this ONLY if you're 100% sure no real emails will match
   - ✅ Check **"Skip the Inbox (Archive it)"** — emails skip your inbox but stay in All Mail in case you need to find them later. This is safer than "Delete it"
   - ✅ Check **"Apply the label"** and create a label like "Possible Spam" to collect these emails in one place for review
6. Click **Create filter** to save it

**Example filter that works well:**
- **Has the words:** `"unsubscribe" "click here" "act now"`
- **Doesn't have:** `@gmail.com @yahoo.com @outlook.com`
- **Action:** Skip the Inbox, Apply label "Bulk Mail"

This filter catches most marketing emails (which all contain "unsubscribe") but ignores emails from real people (Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook addresses).

> **Warning:** Be careful with the "Delete it" filter action. If your filter is too broad, it could delete real emails from friends, your bank, or your job — and you'll never see them. Start with "Skip the Inbox" and review the filtered emails for a week. If no real emails get caught, then switch to "Delete it."

## Step 5: Protect Your Email Address from Future Spam

The best way to stop spam is to prevent spammers from getting your email address in the first place. Every time you type your email into a website, sign up for a "free" offer, or post it publicly, it can end up on spam lists.

**Fix 8 — Stop giving out your real email address**

Follow these rules to keep your inbox clean:

1. **Never post your email address publicly.** Don't put it in social media bios, forum posts, blog comments, or Craigslist ads. Spammers use automated programs (called "harvesters") that scan the web 24/7 looking for email addresses
2. **Use a "throwaway" email for sign-ups.** When a website requires an email to access a free download, enter a contest, or view content, don't use your main email. Create a free second email address (another Gmail or Outlook account) specifically for sign-ups and promotions. Check it once a week and delete everything
3. **Read before you check boxes.** When signing up for anything online, look for small pre-checked boxes that say things like "Yes, send me promotional emails" or "I agree to receive newsletters from our partners." Uncheck them BEFORE submitting the form
4. **Use email aliases if your provider supports them.** Gmail has a built-in feature: add a `+` sign and any word to your address, and it still arrives in your inbox. For example, if your email is `jane@gmail.com`, you can use `jane+shopping@gmail.com` when buying things online. If that address gets spammed, you know exactly who sold your email — and you can create a filter to block everything sent to `jane+shopping@gmail.com`
5. **Don't reply to spam or click "Remove me" links in obvious spam.** Legitimate companies honor unsubscribe requests. Scammers do not — clicking their links tells them your email is active and valuable, leading to MORE spam

> **Common mistake:** People use the same email address for EVERYTHING — banking, social media, online shopping, forum sign-ups, newsletter subscriptions, contest entries. When (not if) one of those services has a data breach or sells your email to advertisers, your main inbox gets flooded. Use separate emails for important stuff (banking, work, family) and non-important stuff (shopping, forums, freebies).

## Still Stuck?

- **Spam is getting worse, not better?** It can take 1-2 weeks of consistent reporting and blocking before you notice a real difference. Spam filters need time to learn. Keep reporting spam every day, and the volume WILL go down.
- **Receiving spam from YOURSELF?** This is called "spoofing" — spammers fake the "From" address to look like it came from you. Don't worry, they don't have access to your account. Report it as spam and move on. If you're worried, change your password for peace of mind.
- **Getting hundreds of spam emails per day?** Your email may have been part of a recent data breach. Check **haveibeenpwned.com** — enter your email address and it tells you if your information was leaked. If it was, seriously consider creating a brand new email address and switching your important accounts (bank, work, family) to the new one.
- **Spam emails include your real name or password?** This is scary but common. It usually means your information was in a data breach, and scammers are using old passwords to scare you. Change your password immediately — especially if the email includes a password you still use anywhere.
- **Want a completely fresh start?** Create a new email address, set up strong spam filters from day one, and ONLY give it to people and services you trust. Gradually move your important accounts to the new address over a few weeks. This is the nuclear option, but it works.
