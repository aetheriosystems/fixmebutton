## Before You Start

You pop your AirPods in your ears, expecting to hear your music or jump on a call — and nothing happens. Maybe the popup doesn't appear on your iPhone. Maybe one AirPod is dead silent while the other works perfectly. Or maybe they were connected five minutes ago and now they've vanished from your Bluetooth list entirely.

AirPods problems are frustrating because they seem to appear out of nowhere. The good news: the vast majority of AirPods issues — connectivity problems, one side not working, charging hiccups — are software or grime problems, not hardware failures. You almost never need to buy a replacement.

This guide covers all AirPods models (1st generation through the latest AirPods 4 and AirPods Pro 3), plus Beats earbuds and most generic Bluetooth earbuds. Work through the steps in order — Step 2 alone fixes the problem for about half of all people.

**What you'll need:**
- Your AirPods or Bluetooth earbuds and their charging case
- Your iPhone, iPad, or Mac (the device you're trying to connect to)
- A Lightning or USB-C charging cable (for wired charging)
- A soft, dry cloth or microfiber cloth
- A cotton swab or soft-bristled brush (like a clean toothbrush)
- About 20 minutes

> **Work through the steps in order.** Start with Step 1 — dead batteries are the #1 cause and take one minute to check. You probably won't need all six steps. If your AirPods were working fine until a recent iOS update, jump to Step 6.

## Step 1: Check the Battery and Make Sure Your AirPods Are Charged

AirPods have tiny batteries that can drain even when they're sitting in the case — especially if the case itself is dead. A completely dead AirPod won't connect, won't make a sound, and won't even show up in Bluetooth settings. This is the single most common reason AirPods "stop working" and it's also the easiest to fix.

### Fix 1 — Check the battery level on your iPhone

1. With your AirPods inside the charging case, open the lid and hold the case close to your iPhone (within 2–3 inches).
2. A popup should appear on your iPhone screen showing the battery percentage for both AirPods and the case. If it doesn't appear:
   - Make sure Bluetooth is ON (Settings → Bluetooth).
   - Make sure your AirPods have been paired with this iPhone before (the popup only works with previously paired AirPods).
3. If one AirPod shows "0%" or doesn't appear at all in the popup, that specific AirPod is completely dead — it needs charging before it can connect.
4. If the case shows "0%" or a red lightning bolt, the case battery is dead — plug it in.

### Fix 2 — Check the status light on the case

1. Open the lid of the charging case (with AirPods inside) and look at the status light:
   - **Green:** The case and/or AirPods are fully charged.
   - **Amber (solid):** The case has less than one full charge remaining, or the AirPods are charging.
   - **Amber (flashing):** Your AirPods need to be set up again (skip to Step 5).
   - **White (flashing):** Your AirPods are in pairing mode and ready to connect.
   - **No light at all:** The case battery is completely dead. Plug it in.
2. If your case has no light at all, connect it to a charger using a Lightning or USB-C cable (depending on your model). Wait at least 15 minutes before trying again. Even a few minutes of charging can give you enough power to test whether the AirPods are working.
3. On AirPods 4 and AirPods Pro 2/3 with the latest firmware, the status light may briefly pulse amber when the AirPods are less than 95% charged. This is normal — it doesn't mean anything is wrong.

### Fix 3 — Clean the charging contacts inside the case

If your AirPods are sitting in the case but not charging, the problem is often dirty charging contacts. Earwax, dust, and pocket lint build up on the metal contacts at the bottom of the case and prevent the AirPods from making a good electrical connection.

1. Look inside the charging case at the bottom of each AirPod slot. You'll see small metal contacts at the very bottom — these are the charging pins.
2. Use a dry cotton swab to gently clean around the metal contacts. Do NOT use any liquid — moisture inside the charging case can cause a short circuit and permanently damage it.
3. Check the bottom of each AirPod — there's a small metal ring at the tip. Wipe each AirPod's stem with a soft, dry microfiber cloth.
4. Put the AirPods back in the case and close the lid. Wait 30 seconds, then open the lid and check the status light. If it turns on now, the contacts were dirty — you've solved the charging problem.
5. For stubborn grime, use a soft-bristled brush (like a clean, dry toothbrush) to gently brush the contacts. Compressed air (the kind sold for cleaning electronics) can also help blow out debris. Hold the can upright and use short bursts — tilting the can sprays liquid propellant that can damage the case.

> **Common mistake:** People clean the outside of their AirPods and the case, but never the charging contacts inside. These contacts transfer power from the case to the AirPods — if they're even slightly dirty, the AirPods can sit in the case all night and still be dead in the morning. Clean the contacts once a month as preventative maintenance.

## Step 2: Make Your iPhone "Forget" Your AirPods and Reconnect Them

Sometimes the Bluetooth connection between your iPhone and your AirPods gets into a confused state. Your iPhone thinks it's connected, your AirPods think they're connected, but no audio plays and the popup doesn't appear. The fix is to erase the pairing and start fresh — this clears any glitched Bluetooth handshake.

### iPhone and iPad

1. Go to **Settings → Bluetooth**.
2. Find your AirPods in the **My Devices** list. They may appear as "AirPods," "AirPods Pro," or whatever you named them (e.g., "Alex's AirPods").
3. Tap the **blue "i" (info) icon** in a circle next to your AirPods' name.
4. Scroll down and tap **Forget This Device**.
5. Tap **Forget Device** again to confirm. Your AirPods are now completely unpaired from this iPhone.
6. Turn Bluetooth OFF completely: go to **Settings → Bluetooth** and toggle the switch to OFF. Wait 10 seconds, then toggle it back ON.
7. Now put both AirPods in their charging case, close the lid, and wait 30 seconds.
8. Open the lid of the case (keep the AirPods inside).
9. For AirPods 4 and AirPods Pro 3: double-tap the front of the case until the status light flashes white.
10. For all other AirPods models: press and hold the **setup button** on the back of the case until the status light flashes white (about 15 seconds).
11. Hold the case (with the lid open) next to your iPhone. A setup animation should appear on your iPhone screen.
12. Tap **Connect**, then follow the on-screen steps. If you use Siri, it will ask you to set up "Hey Siri." If you have AirPods Pro, it will walk you through noise control settings.
13. If the popup doesn't appear: unlock your iPhone, go to the Home Screen, and open the case lid again. The popup only appears when the iPhone is unlocked and on the Home Screen (not inside an app).

### Mac

1. Click the **Apple menu ()** → **System Settings** → **Bluetooth**.
2. Find your AirPods in the device list, click the **"i" (info) icon** next to them, then click **Forget This Device**.
3. Confirm, then turn Bluetooth off and back on.
4. Follow steps 7–13 above to reconnect.

### Android and other devices

1. Go to **Settings → Bluetooth** (or **Connections → Bluetooth** on Samsung).
2. Tap the gear icon next to your AirPods in the paired devices list, then tap **Forget** or **Unpair**.
3. Turn Bluetooth off and back on.
4. Put AirPods in the case, open the lid, press and hold the setup button on the back until the light flashes white.
5. Your AirPods should appear in the "Available devices" list — tap to pair them.

> **Common mistake:** People turn Bluetooth off in Control Center (swipe down from the top-right corner) and assume that fully turns Bluetooth off. It doesn't — Control Center only disconnects current devices. The Bluetooth radio keeps running, and the glitched connection may survive. To truly reset the Bluetooth connection, you MUST turn it off in the Settings app (Settings → Bluetooth → toggle OFF), not Control Center.

> **Tip:** If you have multiple Apple devices signed into the same iCloud account (iPhone, iPad, Mac), your AirPods automatically sync the pairing across all of them. After you reconnect to your iPhone, they should automatically appear on your iPad and Mac too. If they don't, repeat the "Forget This Device" steps on those devices individually.

## Step 3: Fix One AirPod Not Working (Audio Imbalance and Dirt)

When one AirPod is silent while the other works perfectly, the most common causes are (in order): dirty speaker grilles, an off-center audio balance setting, or a firmware glitch. Before you assume the silent AirPod is "broken," try these fixes.

### Fix 1 — Clean the speaker grilles thoroughly

Earwax, sweat, and skin oils gradually clog the tiny speaker grilles on each AirPod. When one grille gets more clogged than the other, that AirPod sounds quieter — and if it's completely blocked, it can go silent entirely.

1. Inspect both AirPods under good light. Look at the main speaker grille (the large oval opening) and the small secondary grilles on the sides and top. If one looks dull, waxy, or discolored compared to the other, it's clogged.
2. Use a dry cotton swab to gently clean around the main speaker grille. Don't push debris INTO the grille — wipe across the surface.
3. For the small secondary grilles (the tiny mesh openings on the sides), use a soft-bristled dry brush (a clean toothbrush works perfectly). Gently brush across the mesh in one direction — don't scrub back and forth, which can push debris deeper.
4. **Blu-Tack trick (the most effective cleaning method):** Take a small piece of Blu-Tack (or any reusable adhesive putty — available at office supply stores, hardware stores, or Amazon for a few dollars). Warm it up by kneading it in your fingers for 30 seconds. Press it firmly against the speaker grille, then peel it off quickly. The putty pulls earwax and debris out of the tiny holes without leaving residue. Repeat 2–3 times on each AirPod. This trick is widely recommended on Reddit and YouTube and works better than any other cleaning method.
5. For AirPods Pro: the silicone ear tips also trap debris. Remove the ear tips (gently pull them off — they're designed to be removable), rinse them with fresh water (no soap), dry them thoroughly with a lint-free cloth, and snap them back on. Make sure they click into place — an improperly seated ear tip can cause one side to sound quieter.
6. Test the audio after cleaning. On iPhone, open an app like Music or Spotify and play a song. Listen closely — is the previously silent AirPod now producing sound?

> **Warning:** Never use liquids (water, alcohol, cleaning spray) directly on your AirPods. AirPods are NOT waterproof — they're only water-resistant (sweat and splashes). Liquids can seep inside and permanently damage the internal components. If you must use a slightly damp cloth, wring it out until it's barely moist, and avoid all openings and grilles.

### Fix 2 — Check the iPhone's audio balance setting

Your iPhone has an accessibility setting that shifts audio volume between the left and right channels. If this slider got moved all the way to one side — often accidentally, or by someone else who used your phone — one AirPod will be completely silent even though both are working perfectly.

1. Go to **Settings → Accessibility → Audio & Visual**.
2. Look for the **Balance** slider under the "Hearing" section. It's a left-to-right slider with "L" on the left and "R" on the right.
3. If the slider is NOT in the exact center, audio is being sent unevenly to your AirPods. Drag the slider to the exact center position. (It should snap to the center — you'll feel a slight haptic bump on newer iPhones.)
4. Play audio to test. If the previously silent AirPod now works, the balance slider was the culprit.
5. Also check **Mono Audio** on the same page. If Mono Audio is ON, your iPhone is combining left and right channels into one — which can cause odd behavior with some audio sources. Turn it OFF for normal stereo playback, or leave it ON if you're hard of hearing in one ear and want the same audio in both AirPods.

> **Common mistake:** People spend 30 minutes resetting their AirPods when the problem is simply the audio balance slider pushed all the way to one side. This slider is buried in Accessibility settings where most people never look. If you share your iPhone with kids or someone else, this setting gets bumped accidentally more often than you'd think.

### Fix 3 — Switch ears and test

This is a quick diagnostic trick that tells you whether the problem is the AirPod or your ear:

1. Take the silent AirPod and put it in the OPPOSITE ear (the one where the working AirPod normally goes).
2. Take the working AirPod and put it in the ear where the silent one normally goes.
3. Play music.
4. If the silence MOVES to the other ear (the previously working ear), the problem is that specific AirPod — it's dirty or has a hardware issue.
5. If the silence stays in the SAME ear (the ear, not the AirPod), the problem is with YOUR EAR — you may have earwax buildup, an ear infection, or hearing loss in that ear. See a doctor or audiologist. This is more common than people realize and is often discovered exactly this way.

> **Tip:** If the ear-swap test confirms one AirPod is the problem and cleaning doesn't fix it, try swapping just the silent AirPod into the opposite ear but leave the working one out entirely. If the silent AirPod produces sound — even quietly — when it's the only one in your ear, the problem was likely just a temporary firmware glitch fixed by removing and reinserting it.

## Step 4: Check for Bluetooth Interference and Connection Conflicts

AirPods use Bluetooth, and Bluetooth can get stepped on by other wireless signals, other devices, and even your own body. If your AirPods keep cutting out, stuttering, or disconnecting randomly — especially in specific locations — the problem is likely interference.

### Fix 1 — Move away from sources of Bluetooth and Wi-Fi interference

Bluetooth operates in the same 2.4 GHz frequency band as Wi-Fi, microwave ovens, some baby monitors, cordless phones, and even some fluorescent lights. If your AirPods cut out every time you walk into a particular room, that room has an interference source.

1. Step away from any running microwave oven (seriously — microwaves blast 2.4 GHz interference and are notorious for cutting out Bluetooth audio while they're running).
2. Move away from Wi-Fi routers and mesh Wi-Fi nodes — these broadcast on overlapping frequencies. Try being at least 6–10 feet away.
3. If you're near large metal objects (filing cabinets, refrigerators, metal desks), move away. Metal reflects and scatters Bluetooth signals.
4. If you're in a dense urban area or an apartment building with dozens of nearby Wi-Fi networks, Bluetooth interference is unavoidable. In these environments, try switching your router to use 5 GHz Wi-Fi for your phone (Bluetooth only uses 2.4 GHz, so moving your phone's Wi-Fi to 5 GHz reduces competition on the 2.4 GHz band).

### Fix 2 — Check for device connection conflicts

AirPods can only actively stream audio from ONE device at a time, but they maintain simultaneous connections to multiple Apple devices for quick switching. Sometimes this automatic switching gets confused — your AirPods keep jumping between your iPhone, iPad, and Mac, causing audio to cut out.

1. If you have multiple Apple devices nearby, try turning Bluetooth OFF on the ones you're NOT actively using. For example, if you're listening on your iPhone, turn Bluetooth off on your iPad and Mac (Settings → Bluetooth → toggle OFF).
2. After listening, you can turn Bluetooth back on — the conflict should be resolved.
3. To prevent this permanently: on your iPhone, go to **Settings → Bluetooth → tap the "i" next to your AirPods → Connect to This iPhone → change from "Automatically" to "When Last Connected to This iPhone."** This stops automatic switching to that device. Do this on each device except the one you use most for your AirPods.

### Fix 3 — The body-block effect (keep your phone close)

Bluetooth signals are weak — they barely penetrate the human body, which is mostly water and absorbs 2.4 GHz signals. If your phone is in your back pocket or across the room, and you turn your head, your own body can block the signal between the phone and the AirPod on the opposite side.

1. Keep your iPhone on the same side of your body as your AirPods, or in a front pocket rather than a back pocket.
2. If you're exercising and your phone is in an armband, wear it on the same arm as the AirPod that cuts out.
3. If you're at a desk, keep your iPhone within 3–4 feet with a clear line of sight to your ears.
4. AirPods Pro 2 and Pro 3 have improved Bluetooth range compared to older models, but the body-block effect still applies.

> **Common mistake:** People assume Bluetooth has the same range as Wi-Fi — it doesn't. Bluetooth's effective range is typically 30 feet in open air, but with walls, bodies, and interference, it can drop to 10 feet or less. If your AirPods cut out when you walk to the other end of your apartment, that's normal — Bluetooth isn't designed for whole-house coverage.

> **Tip:** If your AirPods consistently cut out in the same spot (like a specific corner of your office), that spot is an interference dead zone. The fix is usually to move your Wi-Fi router to a different location, switch your phone to 5 GHz Wi-Fi, or simply avoid taking calls in that spot.

## Step 5: Factory Reset Your AirPods

If you've tried everything above and your AirPods still won't connect, one side is silent, or they keep disconnecting, a full factory reset is the next step. Resetting erases all pairing information and custom settings, returning the AirPods to the state they were in when you first took them out of the box. Custom settings like your double-tap action, press-and-hold settings, ear tip fit test results, and personalized spatial audio setup will be erased — but they're quick to set up again.

### For AirPods 1, 2, 3 and AirPods Pro 1, 2

1. Put both AirPods in the charging case. Close the lid and wait 30 seconds.
2. On your iPhone or iPad: go to **Settings → Bluetooth**, tap the blue **"i" (info) icon** next to your AirPods, then tap **Forget This Device**. Tap again to confirm.
3. If your AirPods also appear on your other Apple devices (iPad, Mac, Apple Watch), "Forget" them on those devices too — otherwise they may try to reconnect and interfere with the reset.
4. Open the lid of the charging case (keep AirPods inside).
5. Press and hold the **setup button** on the back of the case for about 15 seconds. The status light on the front of the case will flash amber, then flash white. As soon as it flashes white, release the button.
6. Hold the case (with the lid open) close to your iPhone. A setup animation should appear.
7. Tap **Connect** and follow the on-screen steps. Your AirPods are now factory reset and freshly paired.

### For AirPods 4 and AirPods Pro 3

These newer models have a different reset procedure — there's no physical setup button. Instead, you double-tap the front of the case:

1. Put both AirPods in the charging case. Close the lid and wait 30 seconds.
2. On your iPhone: **Settings → Bluetooth → tap the "i" next to your AirPods → Forget This Device → confirm**.
3. Open the lid of the charging case.
4. Double-tap the front of the case (near the status light) while the status light is on.
5. Double-tap again when the status light flashes white.
6. Double-tap a third time when the status light flashes faster.
7. After the third double-tap, the status light will flash amber, then flash white. Your AirPods are now reset.
8. Hold the case close to your iPhone and follow the setup animation.

### What to do if the status light NEVER flashes white during reset

1. Close the case lid and wait 20 seconds.
2. Open the lid and try the reset sequence again.
3. If the light still doesn't flash white after three attempts, plug the case into a charger and let it charge for at least 15 minutes (even if you think it has power). Some AirPods models won't enter reset mode if the case battery is too low.
4. After charging, try the reset sequence again.
5. If the light STILL doesn't flash white, the case itself may have a hardware issue. You can try using a different charging case (if you have access to one — AirPods cases are cross-compatible within the same generation) to determine if the problem is the AirPods or the case.

### After the reset: restore your settings

Once your AirPods are reconnected, you'll need to redo any custom settings:

1. Go to **Settings → Bluetooth → tap the "i" next to your AirPods**.
2. Set your preferred double-tap or press-and-hold actions (Siri, Play/Pause, noise control, etc.).
3. For AirPods Pro: do the **Ear Tip Fit Test** (Settings → Bluetooth → tap "i" next to AirPods → Ear Tip Fit Test) to check the seal and find the right ear tip size.
4. For AirPods with Personalized Spatial Audio: redo the spatial audio setup when prompted.

> **Common mistake:** People do the reset sequence but skip "Forget This Device" on their iPhone first. If you don't "Forget" the AirPods before resetting, your iPhone still remembers the OLD pairing information and can get confused when the AirPods come back with factory-fresh identity. The reset may appear to work but the same problems come back. Always "Forget This Device" BEFORE the physical reset.

> **Warning:** If you use your AirPods with a non-Apple device (Android phone, Windows PC, Nintendo Switch, etc.), you'll need to re-pair them manually on each of those devices after a factory reset. The reset erases ALL pairings, not just the Apple ones.

## Step 6: Update iOS and AirPods Firmware

AirPods run firmware — software installed on the AirPods themselves — and Apple releases updates to fix bugs, improve connectivity, and add new features. Firmware updates happen silently in the background, but if your AirPods or iPhone haven't updated in a while, you may be running old firmware with known bugs. After major iOS releases (like iOS 26), there are often widespread reports of AirPods connectivity problems that get fixed by a firmware update.

### Check your AirPods firmware version

1. Connect your AirPods to your iPhone (put them in your ears or open the case lid).
2. Go to **Settings → Bluetooth → tap the blue "i" icon** next to your AirPods.
3. Scroll all the way down to the **About** section. Look for **Version** — this is your AirPods firmware version. It'll be something like "7A305," "8B25," or similar.
4. Write down this number. Then check Apple's official AirPods firmware page (search "Apple AirPods firmware versions" on Google) to see if you're on the latest version.

### How to force a firmware update

There's no "Update" button for AirPods firmware. Apple pushes updates automatically, but only when specific conditions are met. Here's how to create those conditions:

1. Put both AirPods in the charging case.
2. Connect the charging case to a power source using a Lightning or USB-C cable. (The case MUST be plugged in — firmware updates won't install on battery power alone.)
3. Place your iPhone or iPad (connected to Wi-Fi) within a few inches of the AirPods case.
4. Leave everything alone for at least 30 minutes. The iPhone will detect the available firmware update and transfer it to the AirPods automatically.
5. After 30 minutes, check the firmware version again (Settings → Bluetooth → tap "i" next to AirPods → About → Version). If it changed, the update installed.
6. If the version didn't change, try again but leave everything overnight. Some firmware updates take longer or require the AirPods to be fully charged.

### Update iOS on your iPhone

AirPods depend on your iPhone for the initial connection and firmware transfer. If your iPhone is running an outdated iOS version, AirPods connectivity can break:

1. Go to **Settings → General → Software Update**.
2. If an update is available, tap **Download and Install**. This requires at least 50% battery or a charger.
3. iOS updates can take 20–60 minutes. Make sure you're on Wi-Fi during the download.
4. After updating, restart your iPhone (press and hold the side button and either volume button until "slide to power off" appears, then turn it back on).
5. Reconnect your AirPods using the steps in Step 2, then check for a firmware update as described above.

### Known issues with specific iOS versions

Apple's community forums and Reddit (r/airpods, r/iPhone, r/iOS) often light up with AirPods connectivity complaints right after major iOS updates. Common post-update problems include:

- **Automatic Ear Detection not working:** AirPods don't automatically play/pause when you put them in or take them out. Go to **Settings → Bluetooth → tap "i" next to AirPods → toggle Automatic Ear Detection OFF, wait 10 seconds, toggle it back ON**.
- **AirPods connecting to the wrong device:** If your AirPods keep connecting to your iPad when you want them on your iPhone, go to **Settings → Bluetooth → tap "i" next to AirPods → Connect to This iPhone** and change it to **"When Last Connected to This iPhone"** on your iPad and Mac.
- **Spatial Audio not working:** If personalized spatial audio stops working after an update, go to **Settings → Bluetooth → tap "i" next to AirPods → Personalized Spatial Audio → redo the setup**.

> **Common mistake:** People assume AirPods firmware updates are instant, like app updates. They're not — firmware updates are slow, silent, and conditional. The case MUST be plugged into power, your iPhone MUST be nearby and on Wi-Fi, and the AirPods MUST be inside the case with the lid closed. If any of those conditions aren't met, the update won't install. If you've been having connectivity problems for weeks and haven't intentionally done a firmware update (by plugging in the case next to your iPhone for 30+ minutes), you're probably running outdated firmware.

## Still Stuck?

- **One AirPod is still silent after cleaning and resetting?** The silent AirPod may have a hardware fault — the speaker driver, battery, or Bluetooth antenna inside it may have failed. Individual AirPods can be replaced through Apple: a single replacement AirPod costs $69–$89 depending on the model (AirPods Pro replacement earbuds cost more than standard AirPods). You don't need to buy a whole new set. Visit an Apple Store or [contact Apple Support](https://support.apple.com/airpods/repair-service) for a replacement. If your AirPods are under warranty or you have AppleCare+ for Headphones, the replacement may be free or heavily discounted.

- **AirPods case not charging at all?** Try a different charging cable and power adapter — cables fail more often than cases. Also check the charging port for lint (use a wooden toothpick — never metal — to gently clean out any pocket lint compressed inside the Lightning or USB-C port). If you have a wireless charging case model, try wireless charging (Qi or Apple Watch charger) as an alternative — if wireless charging works but wired doesn't, the port is dirty or damaged.

- **AirPods keep disconnecting from a non-Apple device?** AirPods work with Android, Windows, and other Bluetooth devices, but they don't get the same seamless experience. Try: (1) "Forget" the AirPods on all Apple devices before pairing with the non-Apple device. (2) Turn off Bluetooth on your Apple devices when using AirPods with a non-Apple device. (3) On Android, go to **Settings → Connected devices → [your AirPods] → toggle off "HD Audio"** (or "AAC") — some Android phones have compatibility issues with AAC over Bluetooth.

- **AirPods Max specific issues?** AirPods Max use a different connection system with an H1 or H2 chip in each ear cup. If your AirPods Max won't connect: (1) Charge them for at least 5 minutes using the Lightning or USB-C cable. (2) Put them in the Smart Case — this triggers ultra-low-power mode and a reset of the internal connection. (3) Press and hold BOTH the Digital Crown AND the noise control button for 15 seconds until the LED flashes amber, then white. This is the factory reset for AirPods Max.

- **Still having persistent problems with all your Apple devices?** The issue may be your iCloud account. If your AirPods misbehave on every device you own, sign out of iCloud on one device (Settings → [your name] → Sign Out), then sign back in. This refreshes the Bluetooth pairing sync across your Apple ID. After signing back in, reconnect your AirPods. This is an aggressive step — you'll need to re-download Apple Music songs and re-add Apple Pay cards — but it fixes deeply embedded syncing issues.

- **Considering replacing your AirPods?** AirPods typically last 2–3 years before battery degradation becomes noticeable (shorter listening time, case needs frequent charging). If your AirPods are more than 3 years old and you've tried every step in this guide, battery aging is the most likely culprit — the tiny batteries simply can't hold enough charge anymore. New AirPods start at $129 (AirPods 4), AirPods Pro 3 at $249, AirPods Max at $549. Apple offers trade-in credit for old AirPods in some regions.
