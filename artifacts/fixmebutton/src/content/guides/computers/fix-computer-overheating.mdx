## Before You Start

Your computer is hot to the touch, the fans are screaming, and it might even shut itself off without warning. Overheating is one of the most common computer problems — and it can lead to permanent damage if ignored. But the good news is that most overheating problems are fixable at home with no special tools.

This guide walks you through five fixes, starting with what to do right now and moving to longer-term solutions that prevent overheating from coming back.

**What you'll need:**
- Your computer
- A can of compressed air (costs about $5 at any office supply or electronics store)
- A screwdriver (for desktop computers only)
- About 20 minutes

> **Work through the steps in order.** The first step helps you cool things down immediately. The later steps stop overheating from happening again.

## Step 1: Emergency Cooling — What to Do Right Now

If your computer is burning hot or keeps shutting down on its own, start here. These actions cool things down fast.

**Fix 1 — Shut down and let it rest**

1. Save any work you can. If the computer is so hot it's unresponsive, skip this — your data may already be at risk.
2. Shut the computer down normally: **Start → Power → Shut down** (Windows) or **Apple menu → Shut Down** (Mac).
3. If it won't respond, press and **hold the power button** for about 10 seconds until the screen goes black.
4. Unplug the charger (for laptops).
5. Wait at least **15–30 minutes**. Give the computer time to cool all the way down to room temperature.
6. While waiting, check: is the computer on a hard, flat surface? If it's on a bed, blanket, pillow, or carpet, move it. Soft surfaces block the air vents on the bottom and sides.

> **Common mistake:** Turning the computer back on after only 2–3 minutes. The inside components stay hot much longer than the outside case feels. Wait the full 15–30 minutes — otherwise you're just trapping the heat inside again.

**Fix 2 — Check if the fans are even spinning**

1. After the cool-down period, turn the computer back on.
2. Listen closely near the vents. You should hear a gentle whirring sound.
3. **Windows:** Place your hand near the vents (usually on the side or back of a laptop, or back of a desktop). You should feel warm air blowing out.
4. **Mac:** MacBooks vent through the hinge area (between the keyboard and screen) and sometimes the sides. Feel for air movement.
5. If you hear **nothing** — no fan sound at all — a fan may be dead or clogged. Skip to Step 3 (cleaning).
6. If you hear **grinding, clicking, or rattling** sounds, a fan is failing and needs replacement. Skip to "Still Stuck?" at the bottom.

## Step 2: Find Out What's Making Your Computer Hot

Your computer gets hot because something is working it too hard. Let's find the culprit.

**Fix 1 — Check CPU and memory usage (Windows)**

1. Press **Ctrl + Shift + Esc** to open Task Manager.
2. If you see a simple list, click **More details** at the bottom.
3. Click the **CPU** column header to sort by CPU usage, highest first.
4. Look at the top of the list. Any program using more than **70–80% CPU** for more than a few seconds is a problem.
5. Common culprits: web browsers with many tabs open, video editing software, games, antivirus scans, and Windows Update running in the background.
6. Right-click any high-CPU program you don't need and click **End task**.

![Windows Task Manager sorted by CPU usage, showing a program using over 80% CPU](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 1 — Check CPU usage (Mac)**

1. Open **Activity Monitor** (press **Cmd + Space**, type "Activity Monitor", and press Enter).
2. Click the **CPU** tab at the top.
3. Click the **% CPU** column header to sort, highest first.
4. Look for any process using a high percentage of CPU. Web browsers, "kernel_task" (this is normal if it's high — it's macOS trying to cool things down), and media apps are common heat sources.
5. Select a high-CPU app you don't need and click the **X button** (top-left) to quit it.

![Mac Activity Monitor showing high CPU usage from a web browser](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 2 — Close unneeded browser tabs**

Web browsers are notorious for eating up CPU and making computers hot. Each open tab — especially ones running videos, animations, or ads — adds to the heat.

1. Count your open tabs. More than 10–15? Close the ones you're not using.
2. Bookmark pages you want to save instead of keeping them open.
3. In Chrome or Edge, press **Shift + Esc** to open the browser's own Task Manager. It shows which specific tabs and extensions are using the most CPU. Close or end the worst offenders.

> **Common mistake:** "But I need all 40 tabs open!" — Each tab with auto-playing video, animated ads, or heavy websites (like Google Docs, social media feeds, and news sites) uses CPU even when you're not looking at it. Install an extension like "OneTab" or "Tab Suspender" that pauses inactive tabs to save CPU and reduce heat.

**Fix 3 — During hot weather, give your computer a break**

1. If the room temperature is above 85°F (29°C), your computer's cooling system has to work much harder. There's only so much it can do if the air around it is already hot.
2. Use a fan to blow air across your workspace — this helps both you and your computer.
3. Avoid using heavy programs (games, video rendering, large file transfers) during the hottest part of the day.
4. For laptops especially, consider using a laptop cooling pad — a flat platform with built-in fans that blows cool air up into the laptop's bottom vents. They cost $15–$30 online or at electronics stores.

## Step 3: Clean Dust Out of Vents and Fans

Dust is the number one cause of overheating. Over months and years, dust builds up inside your computer like a fuzzy blanket, trapping heat. Cleaning it out is the single most effective fix.

> **WARNING:** Always turn off and UNPLUG your computer before cleaning. For laptops, also remove the battery if it's removable. Never use a vacuum cleaner — it creates static electricity that can destroy computer parts. Use only compressed air.

**Fix 1 — Clean laptop vents (no disassembly)**

1. Turn off and unplug the laptop.
2. Find the vents — they're usually on the bottom, sides, and sometimes near the hinge.
3. Hold the can of compressed air **upright** (tipping it sprays freezing liquid, which can damage parts).
4. Spray short bursts (1–2 seconds each) into each vent from about 3 inches away. You'll likely see dust puff out.
5. Do this outside or near an open window — dust will fly everywhere.
6. Pay special attention to the exhaust vent (where hot air comes out) — this one clogs the most.

**Fix 2 — Clean desktop fans and interior (with adult help)**

1. Turn off, unplug, and open the side panel of your desktop case. Most cases have thumbscrews on the back; some need a screwdriver.
2. Before touching anything inside, touch a metal part of the case to discharge static electricity from your body.
3. Use compressed air to blow dust off the fans, the metal heatsinks (the finned metal blocks), and out of the power supply vents.
4. **Hold fans still with your finger** while blowing compressed air at them. Letting them spin freely can damage the bearings.
5. Blow dust out of the case from the inside toward the outside.
6. Close the case, plug everything back in, and turn on.

![A person using a can of compressed air to clean dust from a desktop computer's interior fans](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 3 — Clean MacBook vents**

MacBooks are harder to open, so focus on external vent cleaning:
1. Turn off and unplug your MacBook.
2. Find the vents. On newer MacBooks, they're in the hinge area between the keyboard and screen. On older models, they're also on the sides and bottom.
3. Spray compressed air into the vents with short bursts.
4. Also clean the keyboard — dust can enter through the keys on some models.
5. If the fans still run loudly after cleaning, a professional cleaning (where the bottom case is removed) may be needed. Apple Stores offer this service.

> **Common mistake:** Using a vacuum cleaner to "suck out" dust instead of compressed air to blow it out. Vacuums create static electricity that can fry sensitive computer parts. A $5 can of compressed air is much safer. Also: never blow into the computer with your mouth — the moisture in your breath can cause corrosion.

## Step 4: Adjust Power Settings to Reduce Heat

Your computer's power settings control how hard it works. Changing a few settings can dramatically lower heat without you noticing much difference in everyday use.

**Fix 1 — Change power plan (Windows)**

1. Click **Start** and type **Power Plan**, then click **Choose a power plan**.
2. You'll see options like "Balanced," "Power saver," and "High performance."
3. If "High performance" is selected, switch to **Balanced** or **Power saver**. High performance forces your CPU to run at maximum speed all the time, generating constant heat.
4. Click **Change plan settings** next to your chosen plan, then **Change advanced power settings**.
5. Scroll to **Processor power management** → **Maximum processor state**.
6. Change it from 100% to **99%** (on battery and plugged in). This one change prevents the CPU from going into turbo boost mode, which generates the most heat. You'll barely notice the difference day-to-day.
7. Click **Apply** and **OK**.

![Windows Power Options showing the advanced power settings with processor power management](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 2 — Reduce energy use (Mac)**

1. Click the **Apple menu** → **System Settings** → **Battery**.
2. Under **Low Power Mode**, set it to **Always** (or "On Battery Only" if you only overheat when unplugged). Low Power Mode reduces background activity and slightly lowers performance, which keeps the Mac cooler.
3. Also click **Options** (or the ⓘ button) and enable **Optimized battery charging** — this doesn't directly cool, but a healthier battery generates less heat.
4. Under **System Settings** → **Displays**, reduce brightness to about 70% or lower. The screen backlight is a major heat source on laptops.

**Fix 3 — Reduce startup programs**

Programs that launch automatically when you turn on your computer run all day, even if you never use them. Each one adds a little heat.

**Windows:**
1. Press **Ctrl + Shift + Esc** to open Task Manager.
2. Click the **Startup** tab (you may need to click "More details" first).
3. Look at the list. Right-click any program you don't need at startup and select **Disable**. Safe ones to disable: Spotify, Steam, Discord, Adobe helpers, OneDrive (if you don't use it).
4. Don't disable your antivirus or anything from Microsoft/Apple.

**Mac:**
1. Click the **Apple menu** → **System Settings** → **General** → **Login Items & Extensions**.
2. Look under "Open at Login." Select any program you don't need at startup and click the **minus (–) button**.
3. Safe to remove: music apps, chat apps, cloud sync tools you rarely use, and helper apps for printers or accessories.

> **Common mistake:** Disabling ALL startup programs, including important ones like antivirus, graphics drivers, or system utilities. Only disable programs you recognize and know you don't need. If you're unsure about something, leave it alone.

## Step 5: Scan for Malware and Update Your System

Malware (viruses and other bad software) can secretly run in the background, using your computer's power and generating heat. Outdated software can also cause your computer to work harder than it should.

**Fix 1 — Run a malware scan (Windows)**

1. If you already have antivirus software (Windows Security, AVG, Avast, Malwarebytes), open it and run a **Full Scan** — not a Quick Scan. A full scan checks every file and takes 30 minutes to a few hours.
2. To use the built-in Windows Security:
   - Click **Start** → **Settings** → **Privacy & Security** → **Windows Security** → **Virus & threat protection**.
   - Click **Scan options** → **Full scan** → **Scan now**.
3. Let the scan complete. If it finds anything, follow the prompts to remove or quarantine the threats. Then restart your computer.

![Windows Security showing the virus scan options with Full Scan selected](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 2 — Check for malware (Mac)**

Macs are less likely to get malware than Windows PCs, but it still happens — especially "adware" and "cryptojackers" that secretly use your Mac's power for someone else's profit.

1. Open **Activity Monitor** (Cmd + Space, type "Activity Monitor").
2. Click the **CPU** tab and look for any process using a lot of CPU that has a strange name or you don't recognize.
3. If you find something suspicious, search its name online to check if it's malware.
4. For a thorough scan, download the free version of **Malwarebytes for Mac** (malwarebytes.com). Install it and run a scan — it's trusted, widely used, and the free version is enough.

**Fix 3 — Install system updates**

Updates often include fixes for the exact problems that cause overheating.

**Windows:**
1. Open **Settings** → **Windows Update**.
2. Click **Check for updates** and install everything available.
3. Also check for driver updates: right-click **Start** → **Device Manager** → expand **Firmware**, right-click any item → **Update driver** → **Search automatically**.

**Mac:**
1. Click **Apple menu** → **System Settings** → **General** → **Software Update**.
2. If any updates are available, click **Update Now**.
3. Also open the **App Store** and click **Updates** in the sidebar — update all apps. Old app versions can have bugs that cause high CPU usage.

> **Common mistake:** Putting off updates for weeks or months because "they take too long." Each skipped update increases the chance that your computer is running with bugs that make it work harder and run hotter. Set updates to install automatically overnight when you're not using the computer.

## Still Stuck?

- **Computer still overheats after cleaning?** The thermal paste between the CPU/GPU and the heatsink may have dried out. This is the gray paste that transfers heat from the chips to the metal cooling fins. Over 3–5 years, it dries and cracks. Reapplying it requires disassembly — if you're not experienced, take it to a repair shop (usually $30–$60 for the service).

- **Fans are loud even when the computer is idle?** Check the temperature in your room. If the room is above 80°F (27°C), the computer will struggle no matter what. Also, check that a background program isn't running — open Task Manager or Activity Monitor and sort by CPU.

- **Laptop overheats only when charging?** This is partly normal — charging generates heat. But if it's extreme, try a different charger. A failing charger can send the wrong voltage, making the battery and charging circuits overheat. Also, avoid using the laptop while it's charging if possible.

- **Computer shuts down immediately when doing heavy tasks like gaming or video editing?** This is a safety shutdown — the CPU or GPU is hitting its maximum safe temperature. Clean dust first (Step 3), then consider whether your computer can actually handle the task. Entry-level laptops and older desktops may simply not be powerful enough for modern games or 4K video editing.

- **None of these fixes helped?** The problem may be a failing fan, a damaged heatsink, or a faulty temperature sensor. These are hardware problems that need a repair shop. If your computer is under warranty, contact the manufacturer — overheating is usually covered.
