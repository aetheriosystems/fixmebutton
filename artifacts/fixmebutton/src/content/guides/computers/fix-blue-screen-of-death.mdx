## Before You Start

The Blue Screen of Death (BSOD) — that scary blue screen with a sad face and a wall of white text — means Windows hit a problem it couldn't recover from. Your computer restarts, and you might lose unsaved work. But here's the good news: the blue screen itself tells you exactly what went wrong, and most BSODs are fixable without paying for repairs.

**What you'll need:**
- Your Windows computer (even if it keeps restarting — there are workarounds)
- A phone or another device to look up error codes (in case your screen won't stay on long enough)

> **Don't panic!** A single blue screen is rarely a sign of permanent damage. If it only happened once and your computer is working fine now, you can skip to Step 5 (Windows Updates) to prevent future crashes. If it keeps happening, work through every step below in order.

> **Mac users:** Macs don't get the Blue Screen of Death — instead they show a **kernel panic** message (a gray screen with "Your computer restarted because of a problem" in multiple languages). The troubleshooting steps are different. Search for "Mac kernel panic fix" or contact Apple Support.

## Step 1: Write Down the Stop Code

Every blue screen displays a **stop code** — a short phrase in ALL_CAPS_WITH_UNDERSCORES near the bottom of the screen. This code is the single most important clue for fixing your problem.

**What to look for:**
When the blue screen appears, find the text that looks like `DRIVER_IRQL_NOT_LESS_OR_EQUAL` or `MEMORY_MANAGEMENT` or `CRITICAL_PROCESS_DIED`. Write it down or take a photo with your phone before the computer restarts.

**Common stop codes and what they usually mean:**

| Stop Code | Most Likely Cause |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **MEMORY_MANAGEMENT** | Faulty RAM or a driver writing to the wrong memory location |
| **DRIVER_IRQL_NOT_LESS_OR_EQUAL** | A driver tried to access memory it shouldn't — usually graphics, network, or audio drivers |
| **CRITICAL_PROCESS_DIED** | A core Windows process crashed — often from corrupted system files |
| **PAGE_FAULT_IN_NONPAGED_AREA** | Windows couldn't find data it expected in memory — RAM issues or corrupted files |
| **SYSTEM_SERVICE_EXCEPTION** | A driver or system service caused an exception — commonly graphics drivers |
| **VIDEO_TDR_FAILURE** | Your graphics card driver crashed and couldn't recover |
| **WHEA_UNCORRECTABLE_ERROR** | A hardware-level error — often overheating, failing CPU, or power issues |
| **INACCESSIBLE_BOOT_DEVICE** | Windows can't read your hard drive or SSD during startup |
| **DPC_WATCHDOG_VIOLATION** | A driver took too long to respond — often SSD or storage drivers |
| **KMODE_EXCEPTION_NOT_HANDLED** | A kernel-mode program (usually a driver) caused a crash |

> **If your computer restarts too fast** to read the code: restart your computer, and as soon as it turns on, repeatedly press **F8** (or hold **Shift** while clicking Restart). This opens the Advanced Startup menu. Go to **Troubleshoot → Advanced Options → Startup Settings → Restart**, then press **F8** for "Disable automatic restart on system failure." Now the blue screen will stay on screen until you manually restart.

![A close-up of a BSOD screen highlighting the stop code text near the bottom](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** Restarting over and over without ever writing down the stop code. Without that code, you're guessing. The stop code narrows down the problem from "anything" to "probably a graphics driver" or "probably faulty RAM." Always write it down first.

## Step 2: Disconnect All External Devices

New or faulty hardware is one of the most common BSOD triggers. Before diving into software fixes, rule out the easy stuff.

**Fix 1 — Unplug everything except the essentials**
1. Shut down your computer completely (hold the power button for 10 seconds if it won't shut down normally)
2. Unplug EVERY external device: USB drives, printers, webcams, external hard drives, game controllers, microphones, drawing tablets — everything
3. Leave only your keyboard and mouse plugged in
4. Turn the computer back on

**Fix 2 — Remove any recently added internal hardware**
If you installed new RAM, a new graphics card, or any other internal component right before the BSODs started:
1. Turn off and unplug the computer
2. Remove the new hardware
3. Try starting the computer without it

If the BSODs stop after unplugging something, that device or its driver is the problem. Plug devices back in one at a time to find the culprit, then update that device's driver (see Step 4).

![A person unplugging USB devices from the side of a laptop](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** Forgetting about a tiny Bluetooth adapter or a USB hub that's buried behind the desk. These count as external devices. Unplug literally everything except your main keyboard and mouse.

## Step 3: Boot Into Safe Mode

Safe Mode starts Windows with only the bare-minimum drivers and programs. If your computer works fine in Safe Mode but blue-screens in normal mode, you know a third-party driver or program is the cause.

**How to enter Safe Mode (when Windows still boots):**
1. Click the **Start** button, then click the **Power** icon
2. Hold down the **Shift** key on your keyboard and click **Restart**
3. After your computer restarts, you'll see a blue "Choose an option" screen
4. Click **Troubleshoot → Advanced Options → Startup Settings → Restart**
5. After restarting, press **4** or **F4** for regular Safe Mode (press **5** or **F5** for Safe Mode with Networking if you need internet)

**How to enter Safe Mode (when Windows won't boot at all):**
1. Turn on your computer — as soon as you see the Windows logo, press and hold the power button until it shuts off
2. Repeat this two more times (turn on, force off when logo appears)
3. On the third startup, Windows will display "Preparing Automatic Repair" and then the "Choose an option" screen
4. Follow **Troubleshoot → Advanced Options → Startup Settings → Restart**, then press **4** or **F4**

![The Windows "Choose an option" blue screen with Troubleshoot and other options](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Tip:** In Safe Mode, your screen will look different — lower resolution, black desktop background, and the words "Safe Mode" in all four corners. This is normal! Don't panic — just move on to Step 4.

## Step 4: Update or Roll Back Problem Drivers

Outdated, corrupted, or incompatible drivers are the #1 cause of blue screens. This step covers three ways to fix them.

**Fix 1 — Update your graphics driver (the most common culprit)**
1. In Safe Mode, right-click the **Start** button and select **Device Manager**
2. Expand the **Display adapters** category
3. Right-click your graphics card (NVIDIA, AMD, or Intel) and select **Update driver**
4. Choose **Search automatically for drivers**
5. Let Windows find and install the latest driver, then restart your computer

**Fix 2 — Roll back a recently updated driver**
If the BSODs started right after you updated a driver, rolling back to the previous version may fix it:
1. In **Device Manager**, right-click the problem device
2. Select **Properties → Driver** tab
3. Click **Roll Back Driver** (if the button is grayed out, there's no previous version to roll back to)
4. Follow the prompts and restart

**Fix 3 — Check for devices with warning symbols**
In Device Manager, look for any device with a **yellow triangle with an exclamation mark** (!). This means Windows is having trouble with that device:
1. Right-click the flagged device
2. Select **Update driver → Search automatically for drivers**
3. If updating doesn't work, try **Uninstall device**, then restart — Windows will attempt to reinstall the driver automatically

![Windows Device Manager showing a device with a yellow warning triangle next to it](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** Only checking "Display adapters" and ignoring other categories. Network adapters, audio devices, and storage controllers can all cause blue screens. Look for warning symbols across ALL categories in Device Manager.

## Step 5: Repair Corrupted System Files

Corrupted Windows system files can cause repeated blue screens. Windows has two built-in tools that can find and fix these files automatically.

**Run these commands in order — DISM first, then SFC:**

1. Click **Start**, type **cmd**, right-click **Command Prompt**, and select **Run as administrator**. Click **Yes** on the pop-up.
2. First, run DISM to repair the system image (this needs an internet connection):
   ```
   DISM.exe /Online /Cleanup-image /Restorehealth
   ```
3. Wait patiently — DISM may appear to stall at 62.3% or another percentage for several minutes. This is completely normal. Do not close the window. The whole process can take 10–30 minutes.
4. After DISM finishes, run the System File Checker:
   ```
   sfc /scannow
   ```
5. Wait for SFC to complete (5–15 minutes). When it finishes, it will tell you one of three things:
   - **"Windows Resource Protection did not find any integrity violations"** — Your system files are fine
   - **"Windows Resource Protection found corrupt files and successfully repaired them"** — It fixed something! Restart your computer
   - **"Windows Resource Protection found corrupt files but was unable to fix some of them"** — Some files couldn't be repaired. See the "Still Stuck?" section below

![Windows Command Prompt showing SFC scan reaching 100% verification complete](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Common mistake:** Running SFC before DISM, or running only SFC and skipping DISM entirely. DISM repairs the master copy of system files that SFC uses as a reference. If the master copy itself is damaged, SFC can't fix anything. Always run DISM first, then SFC.

## Step 6: Install Windows Updates and Check Your Disk

Old Windows versions and disk errors are two more common BSOD causes — and both are easy to check.

**Windows Update:**
1. Open **Settings** (press **Windows key + I**)
2. Go to **Windows Update** (Windows 11) or **Update & Security → Windows Update** (Windows 10)
3. Click **Check for updates**
4. Install ALL available updates, including optional driver updates
5. Restart your computer when prompted

**Check your hard drive for errors:**
1. Open **Command Prompt as administrator** (same as Step 5)
2. Type this command and press Enter:
   ```
   chkdsk C: /f /r
   ```
3. It will say the drive is in use and ask if you want to schedule it for the next restart. Type **Y** and press Enter.
4. Restart your computer. The scan runs automatically before Windows loads — it may take 20 minutes to over an hour depending on your drive size. Do not interrupt it.

> **Common mistake:** Typing CHKDSK without the `/f` or `/r` flags (or typing them lowercase — they must be with a forward slash and lowercase letters). CHKDSK with no flags only scans for problems but doesn't fix anything. Always use `chkdsk C: /f /r`.

## Still Stuck?

- **BSOD only happens when playing games or watching videos?** Your graphics card is the likely culprit. Download the latest driver directly from **NVIDIA, AMD, or Intel's website** instead of using Windows Update. The manufacturer's website often has newer drivers than Windows Update provides.

- **Same stop code keeps appearing even after all fixes?** Search the exact stop code plus your computer model (e.g., "DRIVER_IRQL_NOT_LESS_OR_EQUAL Dell Inspiron 15") — you might find a specific fix for your exact hardware.

- **Computer is more than 5 years old and BSODs are getting worse?** Failing RAM sticks or a dying hard drive become more common as computers age. A repair shop can run memory diagnostics and test your hard drive's health. Replacing RAM or switching from an old hard drive to an SSD often solves the problem.

- **SFC reported "unable to fix some files"?** This means some system files are too damaged for SFC to repair. The most reliable fix is a **Windows repair install** (also called an "in-place upgrade"): download the Windows Media Creation Tool from Microsoft's website, run it, and choose "Upgrade this PC now" — it reinstalls Windows while keeping your personal files and apps.

- **Do you see the blue screen flash and then your computer restarts immediately with NO time to read it?** Disable automatic restart so you can actually read the stop code. Open **Control Panel → System and Security → System → Advanced system settings** (on the left) → **Startup and Recovery** settings → uncheck **"Automatically restart."** Now the BSOD stays on screen until you manually restart.

- **Using a work or school computer?** Ask your IT department before running system repair tools like DISM or CHKDSK — managed devices often have their own repair and update processes that could conflict.
