## Before You Start

You press the power button and nothing happens — no lights, no fan noise, no screen. Or maybe the lights come on for a second and then everything goes dark. A laptop that won't turn on is scary, but in most cases the problem is simple and fixable at home without any tools.

This guide walks you through five fixes, starting with the easiest and most common. Work through them in order — most people have their laptop running again by Fix 2 or 3.

**What you'll need:**
- Your laptop and its charger
- A wall outlet you know works (test it with a lamp or phone charger)
- About 15 minutes

> **Is the battery completely dead?** If your laptop was working fine yesterday and suddenly won't turn on today, the battery may have drained completely overnight. Plug in the charger and let it sit for at least 15 minutes before pressing the power button. A deeply discharged battery sometimes won't respond instantly — it needs a short charging period first.

## Fix 1: Check the Power Supply

The most common reason a laptop won't turn on is simply that it isn't getting power. Before assuming something is broken, rule out the power supply.

**Fix 1 — Check the charger and outlet:**

1. Look at the charger's power brick (the rectangular block on the cable). Does it have a small LED light? If it's lit, power is flowing through the charger. If it's dark, the charger isn't getting power.
2. Make sure the charger is plugged firmly into BOTH the wall outlet AND the laptop. The connector should click or seat snugly — if it's loose or wobbly, it may not be making contact.
3. Try a different wall outlet — preferably one in a different room. Don't use a power strip or extension cord for this test. Plug the charger directly into the wall.
4. Test the wall outlet with something else — a lamp, a phone charger, anything. If the outlet itself is dead, check your circuit breaker.

**Fix 1 — Inspect the charger cable:**

1. Look closely at the entire length of the charger cable. Check for cuts, kinks, frayed spots, or exposed wires — especially near where the cable meets the brick and the connector tip.
2. Gently wiggle the connector where it plugs into the laptop. If the charging light flickers on and off, the cable or the laptop's charging port may be damaged.
3. If you have access to a compatible charger (from a friend or family member with a similar laptop), try it. If a different charger works, your original charger needs to be replaced.

**Fix 1 — Look for charging indicator lights:**

Most laptops have a small LED light near the power port or on the front edge that glows when charging. Common colors:
- **White or green** — battery is charged or charging normally
- **Amber or orange** — battery is charging but low
- **Blinking** — possible battery or charger problem
- **No light at all** — no power is reaching the laptop

If there is no charging light at all after checking the outlet and cable, move on to Fix 2.

![Checking the laptop charger cable and power brick](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People assume the charger works because "it worked yesterday." Charger cables wear out over time, especially at the connector ends where they bend. A charger can fail without any visible damage. Also, power strips can fail — always test by plugging directly into a wall outlet.

## Fix 2: Perform a Hard Reset (Drain Residual Power)

Sometimes a laptop gets stuck in a "half-on" state where it won't fully turn on OR off. Electrical charge builds up inside and prevents the laptop from starting. A hard reset drains this leftover charge and gives the laptop a fresh start. This fix works for both Windows laptops and MacBooks.

1. **Unplug the charger** from the laptop. Remove the charger from both the laptop and the wall.
2. **If your laptop has a removable battery**, take it out. Look for a latch or release switch on the bottom — slide it and pull the battery out. (Many newer laptops don't have removable batteries — if yours doesn't, skip this step.)
3. **Press and hold the power button** for a full 30 seconds. Don't just press it — hold it down and count to 30. You won't see anything happen, and that's normal. You're draining leftover electricity from the circuits.
4. **If you removed the battery**, put it back in now. Make sure it clicks or latches securely.
5. **Plug the charger back in** — into the wall first, then into the laptop.
6. **Press the power button normally** to turn the laptop on.

![Holding down the power button on a laptop for a hard reset](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Tip:** If the laptop turns on after the hard reset but won't turn on again the next day, the battery may be failing. A worn-out battery can prevent a laptop from starting even when plugged in. Try running the laptop with the charger connected at all times, and consider replacing the battery.

**Common mistake:** People press the power button for 5 or 10 seconds instead of the full 30. The 30-second hold is important — shorter times may not drain enough residual charge. Count it out: "one-Mississippi, two-Mississippi..." all the way to 30. Also, some people forget to unplug the charger before the hard reset. The charger must be disconnected or you're not draining anything.

## Fix 3: Check for Display Problems (Not a Power Problem)

Sometimes the laptop IS turning on — you just can't see anything because the screen is the problem, not the power. Before assuming the laptop is dead, check if it's actually running with a blank screen.

**Fix 3 — Listen and look for signs of life:**

1. Press the power button and listen closely. Do you hear a fan spinning? A faint whirring or humming sound? That means the laptop IS on — the screen just isn't showing anything.
2. Look for any lights on the keyboard or near the power button. A glowing power button, a lit Caps Lock key, or a blinking hard drive light all mean the laptop has power.
3. Press the **Caps Lock** key. If the Caps Lock light turns on and off, the laptop is running — it's a display issue, not a power issue.

**Fix 3 — Adjust the display brightness:**

Sometimes the screen brightness got turned all the way down by accident, making the screen look completely black.

1. Turn the laptop on and wait about 30 seconds.
2. Press the **Increase Brightness** key repeatedly. On most laptops, this is one of the function keys on the top row (F1–F12) with a sun icon ☀️. You may need to hold the **Fn** key (near the bottom-left of the keyboard) while pressing the brightness key.
3. On a MacBook, press the **F2** key (sun icon) or use the brightness slider in the Touch Bar if your MacBook has one.

**Fix 3 — Connect an external monitor or TV:**

Connecting an external screen is the fastest way to test if the laptop's built-in screen is the problem. If the external screen works, your laptop's screen or screen cable is likely damaged.

1. Find an external monitor or a TV with an HDMI port, and an HDMI cable.
2. Plug one end of the HDMI cable into the laptop, the other into the monitor or TV.
3. Turn on the monitor/TV and set it to the correct HDMI input.
4. Turn on the laptop. If you see your desktop on the external screen, your laptop IS working — the built-in screen needs repair.

![Connecting a laptop to an external monitor for testing](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Tip:** If the laptop works on an external monitor, you can keep using it that way until you can get the screen repaired. It won't be portable, but it buys you time.

**Common mistake:** People try the brightness key once and give up. On many laptops, the brightness can be turned down to literally zero — the screen looks completely black even in a dark room. Press the brightness-up key 10–15 times to make sure it's at maximum. Also, on some laptops the function keys are reversed — you might need to hold the Fn key to use the brightness controls, or you might NOT need to hold Fn (depending on the laptop's BIOS setting). Try both ways.

## Fix 4: Remove External Devices and Try a Minimal Boot

USB devices, external drives, SD cards, and even a faulty mouse or keyboard can sometimes prevent a laptop from starting. A device that's short-circuiting or drawing too much power can cause the laptop's protection circuits to block startup entirely.

1. **Unplug EVERYTHING** from the laptop except the charger. This includes:
   - USB devices (mouse, keyboard, flash drives, external hard drives, webcam, printers)
   - SD cards and microSD cards
   - Headphones or speakers plugged into the headphone jack
   - External monitors (HDMI, DisplayPort, VGA cables)
   - Ethernet (network) cables
2. **If your laptop has a DVD/CD drive**, make sure there's no disc inside. Press the eject button or use a paperclip in the emergency eject hole.
3. Once everything is disconnected, try turning the laptop on again.

If the laptop turns on with everything unplugged, one of those devices was causing the problem. To find out which one, plug devices back in one at a time — restart the laptop each time — until you find the one that prevents startup.

![Unplugging all USB devices from a laptop](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Tip:** USB-C hubs and docking stations are especially common culprits. If you use a USB-C hub to connect multiple devices, try unplugging the hub from the laptop and see if the laptop starts without it.

**Common mistake:** People forget about devices plugged into the BACK of the laptop or into a USB hub they can't easily see. Check every port — run your finger along all sides of the laptop to feel for anything plugged in, including in ports you never use. SD cards especially are easy to miss because they sit almost flush with the laptop body.

## Fix 5: Check for Overheating and Let It Cool Down

Laptops have built-in safety systems that automatically shut them down if they get too hot. If the laptop refuses to turn on and feels warm to the touch — especially on the bottom or near the hinge — overheating may be the culprit. Laptops can overheat from blocked air vents, dust buildup inside, or being used on soft surfaces like pillows and blankets.

1. **Feel the bottom of the laptop.** Is it noticeably warm or hot? If so, the laptop may have shut down to protect itself and won't turn on again until it cools down.
2. **Unplug the charger** and let the laptop sit for 30 minutes in a cool, well-ventilated area. Don't put it in the refrigerator or freezer — that causes condensation and water damage. Just leave it on a desk or table.
3. **Check the air vents** — they're usually on the sides, back, or bottom of the laptop. Look for dust, pet hair, or debris blocking the vents. If you have a can of compressed air, use it to blow dust out of the vents with short, controlled bursts. (Do NOT use a vacuum cleaner — it creates static electricity that can damage the internal parts. Do NOT blow into the vents with your mouth — moisture from your breath can cause damage.)
4. After 30 minutes, plug the charger back in and try turning it on.

If the laptop turns on after cooling down, you need to prevent future overheating:
- Always use your laptop on a hard, flat surface — never on a bed, pillow, blanket, or carpet.
- Consider using a laptop cooling pad (a flat stand with built-in fans, costs about $20-30).
- If the laptop overheats often, take it to a repair shop for internal cleaning. Dust buildup on the cooling fan and heat sink is the most common cause of chronic overheating.

![Checking laptop air vents for dust buildup](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People try to turn the laptop on immediately after it shuts down from overheating. The safety system won't allow it — the laptop needs to actually cool down first. Waiting 30 minutes is important. Also, people use laptops on pillows and blankets without realizing these soft surfaces completely block the air intake vents on the bottom. If your laptop's fan runs loud and hot, lift it up — the vents are probably blocked.

## Still Stuck?

- **Tried all five fixes and still nothing?** The problem may be internal — a failed motherboard, a dead battery that's so worn out it prevents startup, or a damaged power port. Take the laptop to a repair shop for diagnosis. Most shops charge $30-50 for a diagnostic and will tell you what's wrong before doing any work.
- **Laptop is more than 5-6 years old?** Batteries naturally wear out over time. A replacement battery costs $30-80 depending on the model and is the most likely fix for an older laptop that suddenly won't turn on. Many repair shops can replace it in under an hour.
- **The laptop turns on, shows the brand logo, then goes black?** This is a different problem — the laptop IS getting power but the operating system (Windows or macOS) is crashing during startup. Search for a guide on fixing a laptop stuck on the boot screen.
- **The laptop beeps or flashes a pattern of lights when you try to turn it on?** Count the beeps or flashes. This is an error code that tells you what's wrong. Search online for "[your laptop brand] beep codes" or "[your laptop brand] LED error codes" to decode the pattern.
- **Liquid spilled on the laptop before it stopped working?** If you spilled water, coffee, or any liquid on the laptop, do NOT try to turn it on. Every time you press the power button while there's moisture inside, you risk permanently destroying the circuits. Take it to a repair shop immediately and tell them liquid was spilled.
