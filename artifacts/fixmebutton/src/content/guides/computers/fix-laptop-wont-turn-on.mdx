## Before You Start

You press the power button and... nothing. No lights, no fan noise, no screen — just dead silence. Or maybe a light blinks for a second and then goes dark. Either way, your laptop is acting like a brick, and that sinking feeling in your stomach says "this is going to be expensive."

Take a deep breath. A laptop that won't turn on is scary, but in most cases it's NOT a dead computer. It's usually a drained battery, a loose power connection, or a software glitch that a simple reset can fix. This guide walks you through every fix in order — from the 10-second checks to the deeper solutions. Most people have their laptop back by Fix 2 or 3.

**What you'll need:**
- Your laptop and its charger (power adapter)
- A different working wall outlet (for testing)
- A small screwdriver — only if your laptop has a removable battery (Fix 3)
- An external monitor or TV with HDMI input — if you have one (Fix 4)
- About 20 minutes

> **Work through the fixes in order.** The first three fixes solve the problem for about 85% of people. Don't skip ahead — each fix builds on the last.

> **Is the laptop hot to the touch?** If your laptop shut down suddenly and feels very hot, it may have overheated. Unplug it, let it cool down for 30 minutes in a cool, ventilated area (not on a bed or blanket), then try Fix 1. Overheating triggers an automatic safety shutdown — once the laptop cools down, it should turn on again.

## Fix 1: Perform a Hard Reset (Power Drain)

When a laptop won't turn on, the most common cause is **leftover electrical charge** trapped in the circuits. This is called "flea power" or "residual power." It's completely harmless but it can prevent the laptop from starting up — like a stuck switch that needs to be flipped back. A hard reset drains this charge and gives your laptop a true fresh start.

This fix costs nothing, takes 60 seconds, and solves more "laptop won't turn on" problems than anything else.

**Fix 1 — Drain the residual power:**

1. Unplug the charger from both the wall outlet AND the laptop. Make sure no power cable is connected to the laptop at all.
2. If your laptop has a **removable battery** (you can see a latch or release button on the bottom): slide the latch and remove the battery completely. If the battery is built-in and not removable, that's fine — just skip this step.
3. Now **press and hold the power button** for **60 seconds**. Yes, a full minute. Use a timer on your phone — most people let go way too early. You are NOT trying to turn the laptop on. You are draining the leftover electricity from the circuits.
4. While holding the power button, you won't see or hear anything happen. That's normal. The laptop is off — you're just emptying the electrical charge.
5. After 60 seconds, release the power button.
6. If you removed the battery, put it back in now. Make sure it clicks or locks into place.
7. Plug the charger back into the wall outlet first, then into the laptop.
8. Press the power button normally and see if the laptop starts.

![A hand pressing and holding the power button on a laptop for a full 60 seconds to drain residual charge](PLACEHOLDER)

> **Tip:** If the laptop turns on after a hard reset but the same problem happens again a few days later, your laptop may have a "stuck sleep state" — it's going to sleep but can't wake up. In Windows, go to **Settings → System → Power & battery** and change "When I close the lid" to **Shut down** instead of **Sleep**. This prevents the stuck-sleep problem from happening again.

**Common mistake:** People press the power button for 10 or 15 seconds and give up because "nothing happened." The point of a hard reset is NOT to see something happen — it's to drain electricity from the circuits while nothing is happening. You must hold for the full 60 seconds. Also, people forget to unplug the charger first. If the charger is still connected, the circuits can't drain — you're holding the button for nothing. Always unplug the charger before starting the hard reset.

## Fix 2: Test the Charger and Charging Port

If the hard reset didn't work, the next most likely culprit is the power supply. Your laptop can't turn on without power — and if the charger, cable, or charging port is damaged, the battery never gets charged.

**Fix 2a — Check that the charger is actually delivering power:**

1. Look at the charger's **power brick** (the rectangular block in the middle of the cable). Most laptop chargers have a small **LED light** on the brick. When plugged into the wall, this light should be on — solid, not flickering.
2. If there's NO light on the brick (or the light is flickering or very dim), try plugging the charger into a **different wall outlet** — ideally one in a different room. Don't use a power strip or extension cord for this test; plug directly into the wall.
3. If the charger light still doesn't come on in a different outlet, the charger itself is likely dead. This is actually good news — replacing a charger ($20–$60) is much cheaper than replacing a laptop. Make sure you buy a charger that matches your laptop's exact model number (found on a sticker on the bottom of the laptop). Using a charger with the wrong voltage can damage your laptop permanently.

**Fix 2b — Inspect the charger cable for damage:**

1. Run your fingers along the entire length of the charger cable — from the wall plug to the laptop connector. Feel for any bulges, kinks, cuts, or exposed wires.
2. Pay special attention to the ends: where the cable meets the power brick, and where the cable meets the connector that plugs into the laptop. These are the most common failure points.
3. Look for discoloration (browning or yellowing) on the cable or brick, which indicates overheating.
4. Gently wiggle the connector while it's plugged into the laptop. Does the charging light on the laptop flicker or come on briefly? If so, there's a loose connection inside the cable or the laptop's charging port.

> **Warning:** If you see exposed wires, burn marks, melted plastic, or if the charger cable feels hot to the touch, **stop using it immediately.** A damaged charger is a fire and electric shock hazard. Dispose of it safely and buy a replacement.

**Fix 2c — Check the laptop's charging port:**

1. Shine a flashlight into the charging port on your laptop. Look for bent or broken pins, dust, lint, or debris.
2. If you see lint or dust packed inside, gently clean it out with a wooden toothpick or a plastic dental pick. Do NOT use anything metal — metal can short the contacts and damage the port.
3. The charger plug should fit **snugly** into the port. If it's loose, wobbly, or falls out easily, the port may be physically damaged. A loose charging port needs to be repaired by a professional — it requires soldering inside the laptop.

![A close-up of a laptop's charging port being inspected with a flashlight, showing bent pins inside](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 2d — Try a different charger (if available):**

1. If you can borrow a charger from a friend or family member with the same laptop brand (and same connector tip), try it. Many laptop brands use the same charger across multiple models — Dell uses similar chargers across most of their laptops, HP uses similar chargers, and so on.
2. Make sure the voltage and amperage on the borrowed charger's label match your original charger. The voltage (V) must match exactly. The amperage (A) can be equal or higher — higher is fine, lower is not.
3. If a different charger powers on your laptop, your original charger is dead. Buy a replacement.

**Common mistake:** Assuming the charger is fine because "the light on the laptop came on for a second." A failing charger can deliver just enough power to briefly light up an LED but not enough to actually charge the battery or start the laptop. The only way to truly test a charger is to check the charger brick's own LED (if it has one), inspect the cable for damage, and ideally test with a different charger. Also, people often blame the battery when the charger is the real problem — test the charger first because it's easier to check and cheaper to replace.

## Fix 3: Test and Reset the Battery

If your charger is delivering power but the laptop still won't turn on, the battery itself — or the connection between the battery and the laptop — may be the problem.

**Fix 3a — For laptops with a REMOVABLE battery:**

1. Unplug the charger from the laptop.
2. Slide the battery release latch (usually on the bottom of the laptop) and remove the battery.
3. Look at the metal contacts on both the battery and inside the battery compartment. If they look dirty or corroded (greenish or whitish crust), gently clean them with a **dry** cotton swab or a soft cloth. Do NOT use water or cleaning liquid.
4. Try turning on the laptop with the charger plugged in but the battery REMOVED. This tests whether the laptop can run on AC power alone.
5. If the laptop turns on with no battery, the battery is dead or faulty. You can continue using the laptop plugged in while you order a replacement battery.
6. If the laptop still doesn't turn on with the battery removed and the charger connected, the problem is inside the laptop itself — not the battery. Move on to Fix 4.

**Fix 3b — For laptops with a BUILT-IN (non-removable) battery:**

1. Plug in the charger and make sure the charger is working (Fix 2).
2. Look for ANY sign of life. Even a tiny one. Check for:
   - A charging LED near the power port (white, amber, or green)
   - A brief flash of the keyboard backlight
   - The fan spinning for just a second
   - The screen flickering for a split second
3. Leave the laptop plugged in and charging for **at least 2 hours** without touching it. Don't try to turn it on during this time. A completely drained battery needs time to build up a minimum charge before the laptop can start.
4. After 2 hours, try the hard reset from Fix 1 again (unplug, hold power button for 60 seconds, plug back in, press power button).
5. If you still get no response at all — not even a tiny LED blink — the battery may be completely dead, or the motherboard's power circuit has failed.

![A laptop with its removable battery being taken out, showing the metal contacts in the battery compartment](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 3c — Check for battery-related warning signs before the problem started:**

1. Think back: was the battery draining unusually fast before the laptop died? (Going from 100% to 0% in under an hour when it used to last 3+ hours.)
2. Did the laptop only work when plugged in, shutting down immediately when you unplugged the charger?
3. Was the battery percentage jumping around — showing 40%, then suddenly 5%, then back to 30%?
4. If you answer yes to any of these, your battery was already failing. The "won't turn on" is just the final symptom. A replacement battery (usually $30–$80 depending on the model) will solve the problem.

> **Note:** Laptop batteries naturally wear out over time. Most laptop batteries last 2–4 years or 300–500 charge cycles (one cycle = one full charge from 0% to 100%). If your laptop is more than 3 years old and you've never replaced the battery, a dead battery is a strong possibility.

**Common mistake:** People open up their laptop and disconnect the internal battery without knowing what they're doing. On many modern laptops, the internal battery connector is small, delicate, and easy to damage. If you accidentally break the connector or short the wrong pins, you can permanently damage the motherboard. If your laptop has a built-in battery and the simple fixes above don't work, take it to a repair shop rather than opening it yourself — unless you have experience with electronics repair.

**Common mistake #2:** People buy a cheap replacement battery from an unknown brand on eBay or Amazon to save money. Low-quality third-party batteries can swell, overheat, or even catch fire. Always buy a replacement battery from your laptop manufacturer (Dell, HP, Lenovo, Apple, etc.) or from a reputable brand like Anker or iFixit. The $20 you save isn't worth the fire risk.

## Fix 4: Connect to an External Monitor (Rule Out a Dead Screen)

Here's a scenario that surprises a lot of people: the laptop IS turning on — you just can't see it. The screen is dead, but the computer itself is working fine. You press the power button, you hear the fan, the keyboard lights up... but the screen stays black. That's a screen failure, not a laptop failure.

**Fix 4 — Test with an external monitor or TV:**

1. Find a computer monitor or TV that has an HDMI port. (Most TVs made in the last 10+ years have HDMI.)
2. Connect your laptop to the external display using an HDMI cable. If your laptop doesn't have an HDMI port, you may need an adapter (USB-C to HDMI is common on newer laptops).
3. On the TV or monitor, change the input source to the correct HDMI port (use the **Input** or **Source** button on the TV remote or monitor buttons).
4. Now try turning on your laptop. Watch the external display.
5. If the external display shows your desktop, login screen, or any image at all, your laptop is working — the built-in screen has failed.

**If the external display works, here's what may be wrong with your laptop screen:**

- The **screen cable** inside the laptop may have come loose (this can happen if the laptop was dropped or bumped hard). A repair shop can reseat or replace the cable.
- The **screen backlight** may have burned out. If you shine a flashlight at an angle against the laptop screen and can faintly see your desktop, the backlight is dead. The screen itself still works, but you can't see it without external light.
- The **screen panel** itself may have failed and needs replacement. Laptop screen replacement typically costs $100–$250 at a repair shop, depending on the model.

> **Tip:** Try the keyboard shortcut to switch displays. On Windows, press **Windows key + P** and then press the **Down Arrow** twice, then **Enter** (this selects "Second screen only"). On Mac, press **Command + F1** or **Command + Brightness Up** (depending on your Mac model). This forces the laptop to output to the external display.

![A laptop connected via HDMI cable to a TV, with the TV showing the Windows desktop while the laptop screen remains black](PLACEHOLDER)

**Common mistake:** People see a black laptop screen and immediately assume the whole computer is dead. They buy a new laptop, only to discover the old one was working fine — the screen was just broken. A screen replacement costs far less than a new laptop. Always test with an external monitor before declaring a laptop dead. Also, check the screen brightness: it's surprisingly common for someone (or a cat walking on the keyboard) to turn the brightness all the way down to zero, making the screen appear completely black. Try pressing the brightness-up key (usually one of the F-keys with a sun icon) several times.

## Fix 5: Boot into Safe Mode / Recovery Mode

If your laptop shows ANY sign of life — lights come on, fans spin, you hear beeps, the screen flickers — but it never reaches the login screen or desktop, you may have a software problem. A bad Windows update, corrupted driver, or failed macOS upgrade can prevent the operating system from loading. Safe Mode (Windows) or Recovery Mode (Mac) loads only the bare minimum software, letting you fix the problem.

**Fix 5a — Windows: Force the Recovery Environment to open:**

Windows has a built-in recovery system that opens automatically after several failed startup attempts.

1. Press and hold the power button to force the laptop off. Wait 5 seconds.
2. Press the power button to turn it on. As soon as you see the manufacturer's logo (Dell, HP, Lenovo, etc.), press and hold the power button again to force it off.
3. Repeat this on-off cycle **three times**. On the fourth startup, let the laptop boot normally.
4. After three forced shutdowns, Windows should display a blue screen that says **"Automatic Repair"** or **"Preparing Automatic Repair."** Wait for it to finish.
5. When you see the "Automatic Repair couldn't repair your PC" screen, click **Advanced options**.
6. Click **Troubleshoot → Advanced options → Startup Settings → Restart**.
7. After the restart, you'll see a numbered list. Press **4** or **F4** to enter **Safe Mode**, or press **5** or **F5** to enter **Safe Mode with Networking** (if you need internet access).

![The Windows Automatic Repair blue screen with "Advanced options" button visible](PLACEHOLDER)

Once in Safe Mode, you can:
- Uninstall a recent Windows update: **Settings → Windows Update → Update history → Uninstall updates**
- Roll back a driver: Right-click **Start → Device Manager**, find the device, right-click → **Properties → Driver → Roll Back Driver**
- Run System Restore: Search for "Recovery" in the Start menu → **Open System Restore →** pick a restore point from before the problem started

> **Warning:** System Restore does NOT affect your personal files (documents, photos, etc.), but it DOES uninstall any programs and drivers installed after the restore point date. Make note of what you installed recently — you'll need to reinstall those programs.

**Fix 5b — Mac: Boot into Recovery Mode:**

The method depends on whether your Mac has an Intel processor or Apple Silicon (M1, M2, M3, M4).

**Apple Silicon Macs (2020 and later):**
1. Press and hold the power button (Touch ID button) until you see **"Loading startup options"** on the screen. This takes about 10–15 seconds.
2. Click **Options** (the gear icon), then click **Continue**.
3. You'll be asked to select a user and enter their password.

**Intel Macs:**
1. Press the power button, then immediately press and hold **Command + R**.
2. Keep holding both keys until you see the Apple logo or a spinning globe.
3. Release the keys when you see the Recovery screen (macOS Utilities window).

Once in Recovery Mode, you can:
- **Reinstall macOS:** This reinstalls the operating system without erasing your files. Choose "Reinstall macOS" from the Utilities window.
- **Run Disk Utility:** Select Disk Utility → choose your startup disk (usually "Macintosh HD") → click **First Aid** → **Run**. This checks for and repairs disk errors.
- **Restore from Time Machine:** If you have a Time Machine backup, choose "Restore from Time Machine Backup."

![The macOS Recovery screen showing the Utilities window with options including Restore from Time Machine, Reinstall macOS, and Disk Utility](PLACEHOLDER)

**Fix 5c — If your laptop makes beeping sounds when you try to turn it on:**

Beeping sounds are **beep codes** — your laptop's way of telling you what's wrong when it can't display anything on the screen. The pattern of beeps matters.

1. Count the beeps carefully. How many beeps? Are they long or short? Do they repeat?
2. Search the internet for "[your laptop brand] beep codes." For example: "Dell beep codes" or "HP beep codes."
3. Match the pattern to the error. Common meanings:
   - **Continuous beeping:** Memory (RAM) failure — the RAM stick may have come loose or failed
   - **2 short beeps:** Memory error
   - **3 long, 2 short beeps:** Possible memory issue
   - **5 beeps:** CMOS battery failure (a small coin-cell battery on the motherboard)
   - **8 beeps:** Display/graphics failure
4. For memory-related beep codes, the RAM stick may have come loose. If you're comfortable opening your laptop, reseating the RAM (removing it and putting it back in firmly) sometimes fixes this. If you're not comfortable, take it to a repair shop — RAM reseating is a quick, inexpensive fix.

**Common mistake:** People get to the Advanced Options screen on Windows and choose "Reset this PC" without understanding the difference between "Keep my files" and "Remove everything." **"Keep my files"** reinstalls Windows while preserving your personal documents, photos, and files — but it still removes all your installed programs. **"Remove everything"** wipes the entire drive — everything is gone. If you need to go this route, always try "Keep my files" first. Also, if you have important files on a laptop that won't boot, a repair shop can remove the hard drive or SSD and transfer your files to another computer — even if Windows or macOS won't start.

## Still Stuck?

- **Laptop turns on but immediately shuts off (within 1–5 seconds)?** This is usually a hardware failure — often the motherboard or a short circuit. Unplug EVERYTHING from the laptop: charger, USB devices, SD cards, external drives, everything. Try again. If it still shuts down immediately, a component inside the laptop has failed and needs professional repair.

- **Laptop turns on, fans spin loudly at full speed, but screen stays black?** This can be a failed BIOS/UEFI update, a loose RAM stick, or a motherboard issue. Try the hard reset from Fix 1. If that doesn't work, try reseating the RAM (if you're comfortable opening the laptop). If the problem started right after a BIOS update, the update may have failed — this requires professional repair.

- **Spilled liquid on the laptop before it stopped working?** Turn it off immediately (hold power button), unplug everything, and do NOT try to turn it on again. Flip the laptop upside down (like an open tent) to let the liquid drain out. Leave it in a dry, ventilated area for **at least 48 hours** before attempting to turn it on. Plugging in or turning on a wet laptop can cause permanent short circuits. If it still won't turn on after 48 hours of drying, take it to a repair shop — tell them about the spill so they can clean the internal components.

- **Laptop was dropped or suffered a hard impact?** Even a short fall from a couch to a carpet can crack internal components, knock the hard drive loose, or break the screen cable. A repair shop can diagnose physical damage — but be prepared: impact damage is often expensive to fix, and in many cases a new laptop is more cost-effective than repairing extensive impact damage.

- **Laptop is more than 5–6 years old and won't turn on?** At this age, the most likely causes are a dead battery, a failed motherboard component, or a dead hard drive/SSD. A repair shop can tell you which part failed and how much the repair costs. If the repair cost is more than half the price of a comparable new laptop, replacement is usually the smarter choice.

- **Need to recover files from a dead laptop?** Even if the laptop won't turn on, your files are likely still safe on the hard drive or SSD. A repair shop can remove the drive and connect it to another computer to copy your files. For laptops with a standard 2.5-inch SATA hard drive or SSD, you can buy an inexpensive USB enclosure ($10–$20) and do this yourself.

- **Is your laptop under warranty?** Most laptops come with a 1-year manufacturer warranty. If your laptop is less than a year old (or you purchased an extended warranty), contact the manufacturer before opening it or taking it to a third-party repair shop. Opening the laptop yourself usually voids the warranty. Check your warranty status on the manufacturer's website — you'll need your laptop's serial number (found on a sticker on the bottom).

- **Tried everything in this guide and still nothing?** Not even a tiny LED blink when the charger is connected? At this point, the power circuit on the motherboard has likely failed. This is the most expensive repair — motherboard replacement or component-level repair. Get a quote from a reputable repair shop. For laptops under 3 years old, repair is usually worth it. For laptops over 5 years old, replacement is often the better financial decision.
