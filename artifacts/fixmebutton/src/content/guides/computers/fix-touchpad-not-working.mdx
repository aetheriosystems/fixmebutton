## Before You Start

You're working on your laptop, and suddenly the touchpad stops responding. You swipe your finger across it, and the cursor doesn't move. You tap to click, and nothing happens. Maybe the touchpad was working five minutes ago and now it's frozen. Or maybe it stopped working after a Windows update, or after your laptop woke up from sleep.

The good news: a touchpad that stops working is almost never a hardware failure. The vast majority of the time, it's a setting that got toggled off by accident, a driver that needs a refresh, or a Bluetooth mouse that's confusing the system. This guide walks you through the fixes that solve the problem for the vast majority of people — starting with the fastest, easiest checks first.

**What you'll need:**
- Your laptop (with built-in touchpad)
- If you can't use the touchpad at all: a USB mouse or wired mouse to navigate while you fix it
- About 15 minutes

> **Can't move your cursor at all right now?** Plug in a USB mouse — any cheap wired mouse will work, or a wireless mouse with a USB receiver. You'll need a way to click through the settings menus in this guide. If you don't have a mouse, try using the **Tab key** to jump between buttons, the **arrow keys** to move around, and **Enter** to click. On Windows, you can also press the **Windows key** on your keyboard to open the Start menu and type to search for settings.

> **Work through the steps in order.** Start with Step 1 — accidentally disabling the touchpad with a function key is the #1 cause and takes 5 seconds to check. You probably won't need all five steps.

## Step 1: Check if the Touchpad Is Accidentally Disabled

Every laptop has a way to turn the touchpad on and off — usually a keyboard shortcut. It's incredibly easy to press this combination by accident while typing, cleaning the keyboard, or moving the laptop. When the touchpad is disabled this way, NO software setting, driver update, or restart will bring it back — only pressing the correct key combination will.

**Fix 1 — Press the touchpad function key**

1. Look at the top row of your keyboard (the F1–F12 keys). Find the key with a **touchpad icon** — it usually looks like a small rectangle or a rectangle with a finger pointing at it. On some laptops, the icon may show a **rectangle with an X through it** or a **rectangle with a hand**.
2. Press that key. On most laptops, you need to hold down the **Fn key** (bottom-left corner of the keyboard) first, then press the touchpad function key. For example: **Fn + F7** or **Fn + F9**.
3. Common touchpad key combinations by laptop brand:
   - **HP:** Fn + F7, Fn + F9, or Fn + F11 (look for the touchpad icon)
   - **Dell:** Fn + F3 or Fn + F5 (the key may show a touchpad icon or a glowing dot)
   - **Lenovo:** Fn + F6, Fn + F8, or Fn + F10 (some Lenovo models use a dedicated key above the touchpad itself)
   - **Acer:** Fn + F7
   - **ASUS:** Fn + F9
   - **Microsoft Surface:** The touchpad doesn't have a hardware disable key — skip to Step 2
   - **MacBook:** There is no keyboard shortcut to disable the Mac trackpad — skip to Step 2
4. After pressing the key combination, test the touchpad. If it works again, you're done — you accidentally disabled it.

**Fix 2 — Check for a physical touchpad on/off switch or double-tap zone**

Some laptops have a hardware switch, a sensor, or a special tap zone that physically disables the touchpad:

1. **Look for a small button or sensor** directly above the touchpad (between the touchpad and the keyboard). Some HP and ASUS laptops have a tiny sensor here. Double-tap it gently — a light may turn on or off to indicate the touchpad status.
2. **Check the top-left corner of the touchpad.** Some older HP laptops have a small dot or indentation in the top-left corner. Double-tapping this dot toggles the touchpad on and off. A small light next to the dot shows whether the touchpad is enabled.
3. **Look for a physical switch** on the laptop's front edge or sides — some older Dell Latitude and Lenovo ThinkPad models have a tiny physical slider switch.

**Fix 3 — Check if the touchpad is disabled in the BIOS (rare, but happens)**

If your touchpad stopped working after a BIOS update, or if it seems completely dead even after trying the keyboard shortcuts:

1. Restart your laptop.
2. As it starts up, repeatedly press the **BIOS key** — common keys are **F2**, **F10**, **Del**, or **Esc** (the correct key is usually shown briefly on the screen during startup, with a message like "Press F2 to enter Setup").
3. Once inside the BIOS menu, use the **arrow keys** to navigate (the touchpad won't work here — that's normal). Look for a section called **Advanced**, **System Configuration**, or **Built-in Device Options**.
4. Find a setting called **Touchpad**, **Internal Pointing Device**, or **Trackpad**. Make sure it's set to **Enabled** or **On**.
5. Save and exit (usually by pressing **F10** and confirming with **Enter**). Your laptop will restart.
6. Test the touchpad in Windows.

> **Common mistake:** People restart their laptop, update drivers, and dig through Windows settings for 30 minutes — but never check the function key. The touchpad disable shortcut is easy to press by accident while typing or cleaning the keyboard. Always check the function key FIRST. It takes 5 seconds and solves more touchpad problems than any other fix.

## Step 2: Enable the Touchpad in System Settings

If the function key shortcut didn't fix it, the next place to look is your operating system's touchpad settings. Windows and macOS both have settings that can disable the touchpad — and sometimes these settings get changed by a system update, a new mouse being plugged in, or an app that modifies touchpad behavior.

### On Windows 11

1. Click **Start** (Windows logo, bottom-left) and select **Settings** (gear icon — or press **Windows key + I**).
2. Click **Bluetooth & devices** from the left sidebar.
3. Click **Touchpad**.
4. At the top, make sure the **Touchpad** toggle is switched **On**. If it's off, turn it on and test immediately — your touchpad should start working.
5. If the Touchpad page says **"Your PC has a precision touchpad"** but the touchpad isn't working, click the downward arrow to expand **More touchpad settings** or scroll down and look for an option like **"Leave touchpad on when a mouse is connected."** Make sure it's turned **On** (or temporarily unplug any USB mouse to test).
6. Check the **Taps** section — make sure **"Tap with a single finger to single-click"** is turned on if you normally tap to click instead of pressing down physically.

### On Windows 10

1. Click **Start → Settings → Devices**.
2. Click **Touchpad** in the left sidebar.
3. Make sure the **Touchpad** toggle is switched **On**.
4. Under **Related settings**, click **Additional settings**. This opens the old-style Mouse Properties window.
5. In the window that opens, look for a tab labeled **Touchpad**, **Device Settings**, **ClickPad Settings**, or **Synaptics**. (If you don't see any of these tabs, your touchpad driver may need to be installed — jump to Step 3.)
6. Click that tab. Look for a button that says **Enable Device** or a checkbox for **Disable internal pointing device when external USB pointing device is attached**. If a checkbox like that is checked, uncheck it — this setting disables your touchpad whenever a USB mouse is plugged in.
7. Click **Apply** and then **OK**. Test your touchpad.

### On Mac (macOS)

1. Click the **Apple menu ()** in the top-left corner and select **System Settings** (or **System Preferences** on older macOS).
2. Click **Trackpad** in the sidebar.
3. Go through each of the three tabs — **Point & Click**, **Scroll & Zoom**, and **More Gestures**. Make sure the settings you normally use are turned on. If everything looks wrong (all sliders at zero, all checkboxes off), someone — or some app — may have reset your trackpad settings.
4. Under **Point & Click**, make sure **"Tap to click"** is enabled if you normally tap instead of physically pressing the trackpad.
5. Also check for an external mouse setting: go to **System Settings → Accessibility → Pointer Control**. Look for a checkbox that says **"Ignore built-in trackpad when mouse or wireless trackpad is present."** If this is checked, your Mac's trackpad will stop working whenever you connect an external mouse or trackpad. Uncheck it and test.

> **Common mistake:** On Windows, people find the Touchpad settings page and see the big toggle is On, so they assume settings aren't the problem — but they miss the "Additional settings" link at the bottom. Behind that link is the older Mouse Properties window where the real touchpad driver settings live, including the setting that disables the touchpad when a USB mouse is plugged in. Always check BOTH the new Settings page AND the old Mouse Properties window.

## Step 3: Update or Reinstall Your Touchpad Drivers (Windows) / Reset SMC and NVRAM (Mac)

If the touchpad is enabled in settings but still doesn't work, the driver — the software that tells Windows how to talk to your touchpad — may be corrupted, missing, or incompatible after a Windows update. On a Mac, there are no separate trackpad drivers, but low-level system controllers can get into a bad state.

### Windows: Update or Reinstall the Touchpad Driver

**Fix 1 — Find your touchpad in Device Manager**

1. Right-click the **Start button** (Windows logo) and select **Device Manager**.
2. Look for two categories:
   - **Human Interface Devices** (HID) — touchpads connected through the I²C bus usually appear here as "I2C HID Device" or "HID-compliant touch pad."
   - **Mice and other pointing devices** — some touchpads appear here as "HID-compliant mouse," "PS/2 Compatible Mouse," or with a brand name like "Synaptics TouchPad" or "ELAN Touchpad."
3. Click the arrow to expand each category. Look for any device with a **yellow warning triangle (⚠)** — this means the driver has a problem. If you see one, right-click it and jump to Fix 2.
4. If you don't see anything touchpad-related in either category, click **Action** in the top menu bar and select **Scan for hardware changes**. Windows will re-detect connected hardware.
5. If your touchpad still doesn't appear, look under a category called **Other devices** or **Unknown devices** — an unrecognized touchpad may show up there with a yellow triangle.

**Fix 2 — Update the touchpad driver**

1. Right-click your touchpad device in Device Manager and select **Update driver**.
2. Choose **Search automatically for drivers**. Windows will look for a newer driver online.
3. If Windows finds one, let it install, then restart your computer and test the touchpad.
4. If Windows says "The best drivers for your device are already installed," try Fix 3 — it's often more effective.

**Fix 3 — Uninstall the touchpad driver and restart**

This is often the most effective fix because it clears out corrupted driver settings and forces Windows to download a completely fresh copy:

1. Right-click your touchpad device in Device Manager and select **Uninstall device**.
2. In the confirmation popup, check the box that says **"Attempt to remove the driver for this device"** (on Windows 10, it says "Delete the driver software for this device"). Then click **Uninstall**.
3. **Restart your computer.** This is critical — don't just scan for hardware changes. A full restart is what triggers Windows to download and install a fresh driver.
4. After restarting, test your touchpad. If it works, you're done.

> **Warning:** After uninstalling the touchpad driver, your touchpad will definitely not work — and neither will any USB mouse until Windows loads. Use the **Tab key**, **arrow keys**, and **Enter key** on your keyboard to navigate to the restart button: press **Windows key + X**, then press **U**, then press **R** to restart. Or press **Ctrl + Alt + Delete**, then press **Tab** until the power icon in the bottom-right is selected, and press **Enter** to restart.

**Fix 4 — Roll back the driver if the problem started after a Windows update**

If your touchpad worked perfectly until a recent Windows update:

1. Right-click your touchpad in Device Manager and select **Properties**.
2. Click the **Driver** tab.
3. Click the **Roll Back Driver** button. If the button is greyed out, skip this fix.
4. Follow the prompts, restart, and test.
5. To prevent Windows from reinstalling the problematic driver, you can [pause Windows updates temporarily](https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/pause-updates-in-windows-2998663a-3f2b-bb6e-bcc2-e1b63bf654ea) until a fix is released.

**Fix 5 — Download the touchpad driver from your laptop manufacturer's website**

If the generic Windows driver isn't working, you may need the specific driver from your laptop maker:

1. Go to your laptop manufacturer's support website: **HP**, **Dell**, **Lenovo**, **Acer**, or **ASUS**.
2. Enter your laptop's **model number** (usually printed on a sticker on the bottom of the laptop or visible in **Settings → System → About**).
3. Go to the **Drivers & Downloads** section and look under **Mouse, Keyboard & Input Devices** or **Touchpad**.
4. Download and install the touchpad driver for your version of Windows. After installation, restart and test.

> **Important:** Some Windows 11 24H2 updates in 2025–2026 have been reported to break touchpad drivers on certain HP, Lenovo, and Dell laptops — especially models with Synaptics or ELAN touchpads. If your touchpad stopped working right after a Windows update, and the generic driver fixes aren't helping, check your laptop manufacturer's website for an updated driver specifically marked as compatible with your Windows version.

> **Common mistake:** People see their touchpad listed in Device Manager with NO warning triangle and assume the driver is fine. A driver can be installed but be the wrong version or partially corrupted. If your touchpad isn't working and you've done Steps 1 and 2, always try the uninstall-and-restart driver trick — it fixes a surprising number of "mystery" touchpad problems.

### Mac: Restart and Reset Low-Level Controllers

On Macs, there are no separate trackpad drivers to install — the trackpad is managed directly by macOS. The fixes are different:

**For Apple Silicon Macs (M1, M2, M3, M4 — 2020 and later):**

1. Click the **Apple menu ()** → **Restart**. A simple full restart often clears whatever background process is blocking the trackpad.
2. If restarting doesn't work, [update macOS](https://support.apple.com/en-us/108382): go to **System Settings → General → Software Update** and install any available updates. Apple bundles hardware fixes into macOS updates.

**For Intel-based Macs (2019 and earlier): Reset NVRAM and SMC**

The NVRAM (non-volatile RAM) stores low-level settings including trackpad behavior. If it gets corrupted, the trackpad may stop working:

1. Shut down your Mac (Apple menu → **Shut Down**).
2. Press the **power button** to turn it on, then immediately press and hold **Option + Command + P + R** all at once.
3. Keep holding these four keys for about 20 seconds. If your Mac plays a startup chime, you'll hear it once, then a second time — release the keys after the second chime. On Macs with the T2 chip, release the keys after the Apple logo appears and disappears for the second time.
4. Let your Mac start normally and test the trackpad.

If resetting the NVRAM doesn't help, try resetting the SMC (System Management Controller), which controls low-level hardware including the trackpad:

1. Shut down your Mac completely.
2. For **MacBooks with the T2 chip** (2018–2020): press and hold **Control + Option + Shift** on the left side of the keyboard for 7 seconds. Then, while still holding those keys, press and hold the **power button** for another 7 seconds. Release all keys, wait a few seconds, then turn on normally.
3. For **older Intel MacBooks:** press and hold **Shift + Control + Option** on the left side plus the **power button** simultaneously for 10 seconds. Release and turn on normally.
4. For **Intel iMac and Mac Mini:** unplug the power cord, wait 15 seconds, plug it back in, wait 5 seconds, and turn it on.

> **Common mistake:** Mac users skip the restart because "I never restart my Mac — I just close the lid." If you haven't fully restarted your Mac in weeks, low-level processes that manage the trackpad can build up glitches. A full restart (Apple menu → Restart, not just closing and opening the lid) fixes more trackpad problems on Mac than any other single step.

## Step 4: Check for External Mouse Interference and Bluetooth Conflicts

Sometimes the touchpad IS working — but your computer thinks you're using a different pointing device and is ignoring the touchpad. This can happen when you have a Bluetooth mouse that's connected but sitting in a drawer, a wireless mouse with its USB receiver still plugged in, or a drawing tablet connected to your laptop.

**Fix 1 — Unplug ALL USB devices and disconnect Bluetooth mice**

1. Unplug EVERYTHING from your laptop's USB ports except the power charger. This includes:
   - Wireless mouse USB receivers (the tiny dongle)
   - Wired mice
   - Drawing tablets (Wacom, Huion, etc.)
   - External keyboards (some have built-in touchpads or pointing devices)
   - USB hubs
   - Game controllers
2. After unplugging everything, test the touchpad. If it starts working, one of those devices was the culprit. Plug them back in one at a time to find which one.
3. The most common culprit is a **wireless mouse USB receiver** left plugged into the laptop. Even if the mouse itself is turned off or in a drawer, some laptops automatically disable the touchpad whenever a mouse receiver is detected.

**Fix 2 — Turn off Bluetooth entirely**

Even if your Bluetooth mouse is across the room, if it's still paired with your laptop, Windows or macOS may think you're using it and suppress the touchpad:

1. **Windows:** Go to **Settings → Bluetooth & devices** and toggle **Bluetooth** to **Off**. Wait 5 seconds, then test the touchpad. You can turn Bluetooth back on afterward.
2. **Mac:** Click the **Control Center icon** in the menu bar (top-right, looks like two sliders) and click the **Bluetooth tile** to turn it off. Test the trackpad, then turn Bluetooth back on.

**Fix 3 — Check the touchpad disable-when-mouse-connected setting**

This setting is a common cause of "the touchpad works sometimes but not other times":

1. **Windows:** Open the old Mouse Properties window (see Step 2, Windows 10 instructions). Look for a checkbox called **"Disable internal pointing device when external USB pointing device is attached."** If it's checked, uncheck it and click **Apply**.
2. **Mac:** Go to **System Settings → Accessibility → Pointer Control**. Look for **"Ignore built-in trackpad when mouse or wireless trackpad is present."** If it's checked, uncheck it.

> **Common mistake:** People have a wireless mouse USB receiver plugged into their laptop "just in case I need it" or because "I always leave it in that port." They forget it's there. Then they wonder why the touchpad stopped working. Your laptop sees that receiver and thinks you're using a mouse — so it disables the touchpad to prevent accidental swipes while you're typing. Always check your USB ports before troubleshooting further.

## Step 5: Run the Hardware Troubleshooter and Check for System Updates

If you've tried everything above and the touchpad still won't work, the problem may be at the system level — a Windows service that stopped, a pending update that's causing a conflict, or a corrupted system file. This step uses Windows' built-in diagnostic and repair tools.

**Fix 1 — Run the Windows Hardware and Devices troubleshooter**

Windows has a built-in diagnostic tool that can detect and fix hardware problems automatically:

1. Press **Windows key + R** to open the Run dialog box.
2. Type **msdt.exe -id DeviceDiagnostic** and press **Enter**.
3. The Hardware and Devices troubleshooter will open. Click **Next**.
4. Wait for it to scan for problems — this may take a few minutes.
5. If it finds any issues, it will either fix them automatically or tell you what to do. Follow the on-screen prompts.
6. Restart your laptop when prompted and test the touchpad.

> **Note:** On some versions of Windows 11, the troubleshooter may redirect you to the Get Help app instead. If that happens, open **Settings → System → Troubleshoot → Other troubleshooters** and look for **Hardware and Devices** in the list.

**Fix 2 — Check for Windows optional updates (especially driver updates)**

Windows often hides touchpad driver updates inside "optional updates":

1. Open **Settings → Windows Update** (Windows 11) or **Settings → Update & Security → Windows Update** (Windows 10).
2. Click **Check for updates**.
3. After it checks, look for a link that says **"Optional updates"** or **"View optional updates"** (it may appear below the main update list).
4. Click it and look under **Driver updates** for anything related to your touchpad — it might say "HID," "Touchpad," "Synaptics," "ELAN," or your laptop manufacturer's name plus "Input" or "Pointing Device."
5. Check the box next to any touchpad-related updates and click **Download & install**.
6. Restart your computer when prompted and test.

**Fix 3 — Run System File Checker (Windows)**

Corrupted Windows system files can cause hardware devices to stop working:

1. Press the **Windows key**, type **cmd** or **Command Prompt**, right-click it, and select **Run as administrator**. Click **Yes** if asked for permission.
2. In the black Command Prompt window, type the following command and press **Enter:**
   ```
   sfc /scannow
   ```
3. The System File Checker will scan your Windows installation for corrupted files and attempt to repair them. This can take 10–20 minutes — don't close the window while it runs.
4. When it finishes, it will tell you whether it found and fixed any problems. Restart your laptop regardless of the result, then test the touchpad.

**Fix 4 — Create a new user account to test (Windows)**

This is a powerful diagnostic trick: if the touchpad works in a new user account but not your main one, the problem is a corrupted user setting — not the hardware or driver:

1. Go to **Settings → Accounts → Other users** (Windows 11) or **Settings → Accounts → Family & other users** (Windows 10).
2. Under **Other users**, click **Add account** (or **Add someone else to this PC**).
3. Click **"I don't have this person's sign-in information"** → **"Add a user without a Microsoft account."**
4. Give the account a name (like "Test") and a password you'll remember. Click **Next**.
5. Sign out of your current account (Start → your profile picture → **Sign out**) and sign into the new test account.
6. Test the touchpad. If it works in the test account but not your main one, the touchpad hardware and driver are fine — the problem is a corrupted setting in your main user profile. You can [migrate your files](https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/create-a-local-user-or-administrator-account-in-windows-20de74e0-ac7f-3502-a866-32915af2a34d) to the new account, or continue troubleshooting your main account's settings.
7. To delete the test account afterward: sign back into your main account, go to **Settings → Accounts → Other users**, click the test account, and click **Remove**.

**Fix 5 — For Mac: Check for conflicting accessibility or third-party software**

Some apps that modify cursor or trackpad behavior can interfere:

1. Check if you have any of the following installed: **BetterTouchTool**, **Karabiner-Elements**, **SteerMouse**, **USB Overdrive**, or similar cursor/trackpad utilities. These can override your trackpad's normal behavior. Try quitting them (right-click their icon in the menu bar and select **Quit**) and testing the trackpad.
2. Go to **System Settings → Accessibility → Pointer Control** and check that no unusual settings are enabled, like **"Enable Mouse Keys"** (which lets you use the keyboard to move the cursor — it can conflict with the trackpad).
3. If you recently installed new software, try [booting your Mac in Safe Mode](https://support.apple.com/guide/mac-help/start-up-your-mac-in-safe-mode-mh21245/mac) to temporarily disable all third-party software. If the trackpad works in Safe Mode, a third-party app is the problem.

> **Common mistake:** People run Windows Update, see "You're up to date," and stop — without clicking into "Optional updates." Touchpad driver updates are almost always listed as optional and won't install unless you specifically select them. Always check Optional updates after a main Windows Update check.

## Still Stuck?

- **Touchpad works in BIOS but not in Windows?** This means the touchpad hardware is fine — the problem is a Windows driver or setting. Go back to Step 3 and focus on uninstalling and reinstalling the touchpad driver, checking your laptop manufacturer's website for an updated driver, or rolling back the driver if the problem started after a Windows update.

- **Touchpad cursor moves but clicking doesn't work?** This is a different problem from "not working at all." Try these:
  1. Check the physical touchpad surface — is it warped or bulging upward? If so, a **swollen battery** underneath the touchpad may be pressing against it from below. This is a safety issue — stop using the laptop and get the battery checked immediately. Swollen batteries are a fire hazard.
  2. If the surface is flat, try enabling **"Tap to click"** in your touchpad settings. This lets you tap the surface to click instead of physically pressing down — it's a good workaround if the physical click mechanism is stuck.
  3. Clean the edges of the touchpad with a soft, dry cloth. Crumbs and debris can get wedged under the edges and prevent the physical click from working.

- **Touchpad cursor movement is jerky, erratic, or jumps around while you type?** This is usually a palm-rejection sensitivity issue:
  1. **Windows:** Go to **Settings → Bluetooth & devices → Touchpad** and look for **Touchpad sensitivity**. Set it to **Low sensitivity** or **Most sensitive** (different laptops respond differently — try both). Also, look for a **"Palm rejection"** or **"TouchGuard"** setting and turn it on.
  2. **Mac:** Go to **System Settings → Trackpad** and adjust the **Tracking speed** slider. Also go to **System Settings → Accessibility → Pointer Control** and make sure **"Ignore built-in trackpad when mouse or wireless trackpad is present"** is unchecked.

- **Touchpad stopped working after spilling liquid on the laptop?** Liquid damage can short-circuit the touchpad. Immediately:
  1. Shut down the laptop completely (hold the power button for 10 seconds if it won't shut down normally).
  2. Unplug the charger and any USB devices.
  3. If possible, remove the battery.
  4. Turn the laptop upside down (keyboard facing down) on a towel to let liquid drain OUT — not deeper into the laptop.
  5. Leave it to dry for at least 48 hours in a well-ventilated area. Do NOT use a hair dryer, rice, or any heat source.
  6. After drying, try turning it on. If the touchpad still doesn't work, the liquid may have permanently damaged the touchpad hardware — contact a repair shop.

- **Touchpad light is on but the touchpad doesn't respond at all?** Some HP and ASUS laptops have a small light next to the touchpad or on the touchpad disable button. If the light is on, the touchpad is SUPPOSED to be active — but if it's not responding, try disabling and re-enabling it by double-tapping the top-left corner dot or pressing the function key (Step 1). Sometimes the software state and the light get out of sync — toggling it off and back on re-synchronizes them.

- **Is your touchpad still not working after every single step?** Before concluding it's a hardware failure, try one more thing: **boot your laptop from a Linux USB stick** (like Ubuntu — it's free and you don't need to install it, just run it from the USB). If the touchpad works in Linux but not Windows, the hardware is fine — the problem is definitely a Windows software/driver issue. If the touchpad doesn't work in Linux either, the hardware is likely broken and you should contact your laptop manufacturer or a repair shop.

- **Using a laptop from a lesser-known brand (MSI, Razer, Framework, Chuwi, etc.)?** These laptops sometimes use less common touchpad hardware that Windows Update doesn't automatically support. Go directly to your laptop manufacturer's website, find the support/download page for your exact model, and download the specific touchpad driver. Generic Windows drivers often don't work with these touchpads.
